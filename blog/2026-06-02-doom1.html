Computing with strange matter: why and how is biological matter playing Doom these days

June 2026

This post is a bit of a commentary on a simple but slightly unsettling, at least to outsiders, idea which is coming back in fashion: computation does not have to be done by silicon logic gates alone. I did not have much time to polish this, but I wanted to get it out before things get even more out of hand.

A living neural culture, a bucket of water, an optical cavity, a mechanical oscillator, or a memristive circuit can all be used as computational substrates, provided we know how to drive them and how to read them. When I hear about “neurons playing Doom,” I do not think about a tiny brain with a screen, but a tiny brain in front of a screen, which is basically me in 1995. However, in recent months this has been all over the news because of the work of Cortical Labs, a company that has developed a platform for interfacing living neurons with digital environments.

I visited Cortical Labs in Melbourne in December 2024, if I am not mistaken, and at that time they were setting up the CL1. I saw the racks, I met the team, and I was deeply impressed. I have always thought that neuromorphic systems should be democratized, in the sense that there should be a way to access these systems without needing specialized labs. These should be platforms that many people can use, and that is what Cortical Labs is trying to do. Kudos to them for that. They are building a platform that allows researchers to deploy code to living neural cultures and observe how they process information.

The fact that they are using Doom as a benchmark is a bit of a publicity stunt, of course, but it is also a useful test of whether the system can be coupled to a complex digital environment and produce meaningful adaptive responses. We are used to using digital computers to play games, typically using reinforcement learning algorithms. That is a well-established field. But the idea that a living neural culture can do it is remarkable. The key reason is that I have seen the data and I know how complicated this is. The neurons are not just responding to static images. They are responding to a stream of electrical stimulation that encodes the game state, and their collective activity is being decoded into game actions. This is a closed-loop system in which the biological substrate is part of the control loop.

I am absolutely supportive of this kind of research, simply because it will lead to more research on the subject while reducing the overall costs. I had this idea a few years back regarding memristive systems, but I think the biological case is even more striking. The fact that we can use living neurons as part of a computational system is a powerful demonstration of the idea that computation can be done in many different ways, and that we should not limit ourselves to silicon logic. It is not about replacing digital computers, but about exploring the computational potential of strange matter.

The first public demonstration of this approach was with Pong. I was talking a couple of days ago with Alon Loeffler from Cortical Labs, and he told me that they have been able to train the neurons to play Pong, and that they are now working on Doom. I forgot the precise order of all the steps, and whether they were using reinforcement learning for Pong or not, but the broader point is clear. Doom is richer than Pong, and it is a more interesting test of the system's ability to learn and adapt.

The point is not that this is a good way to build a games console. It is a benchmark for closed-loop adaptive behavior. I think this is about a strange hybrid system in which there is an interplay between classical computing architectures and unconventional ones. The question is: what is the matter? Literally.

Recently, Herbert Jaeger, Beatriz Noheda and Wilfred van der Wiel wrote a manuscript on formal computing frameworks with physical systems. They also have a shorter manuscript in Nature Communications about this. The paper is not always easy to read, but it gives a sense that something is moving in the background of the computing world. People are starting to think about computation in a more physical way, and that is exciting. We are going back, to some extent, to the pre-Turing era, when computation was not yet formalized as a purely symbolic process.

We are rediscovering the fact that matter itself can compute, and that we can use it in ways that are not just emulating digital logic. Many people are trying to free themselves from the constructs that the Turing machine gave us, and to think about computation in a more general way. This is not about replacing digital computers. They are there exactly because, as a result of Shannon's and Hamming's work and many others, there is a formal way of doing error correction for those devices. The key issue is that digitization is thresholding. It is an extremely nonlinear operation that allows us to abstract away from the messy details of physical systems. But it is not the only way to do computation.

In some cases, it may be more efficient to use the physical dynamics directly, without trying to force them into a digital mold. It is about expanding our notion of what computation can be, and what substrates can compute. Quantum computing is, to me at least, a step in that direction, trying to keep a balance between the quantum analog and the digital.

Computing architectures are complicated objects, but if one thinks about it, it boils down to a few key ingredients. There is an input, there is an internal state, and there is an output. We call these typically encoding and decoding, and the part in the middle is the computation. In reality, computation is the whole thing. The input is some signal that we feed into the system. The internal state is some high-dimensional representation of that signal, which may also have memory of the past. The output is some function of the internal state that gives us the answer we want.

The standard image of computation is still the digital computer: bits, gates, memory addresses, instructions, arithmetic units. But there is another tradition, older and in some ways more physical, in which one does not design every operation explicitly. Instead, one takes a dynamical system, feeds it a signal, lets its internal state respond, and then learns how to read out the answer. The system is not programmed in the ordinary sense. It is used.

This is the basic idea behind reservoir computing. A reservoir computer has three parts. First, an input signal is injected into a dynamical system. Second, the system evolves, producing a high-dimensional internal response with memory of the recent past. Third, a simple readout, often just a linear map, is trained to extract the desired output. The reservoir itself is usually not trained. Its job is to transform time-dependent inputs into a rich collection of nonlinear, history-dependent features.

In equations, one may write this schematically as

$$ x_{t+1}=F(x_t,u_t), \qquad y_t=W_{\mathrm{out}}x_t. $$

Here \(u_t\) is the input, \(x_t\) is the internal state of the reservoir, and \(y_t\) is the output. The complicated part is hidden in \(F\), the dynamics of the reservoir. The trained part is only \(W_{\mathrm{out}}\). This is why reservoir computing is so attractive for unconventional hardware: one does not need to manufacture a perfect digital processor. One only needs a physical system with sufficiently rich dynamics, fading memory, and a usable input-output interface.

This is also why reservoir computing provides a natural language for recent biological and physical computing experiments. The question is no longer: can this object implement NAND gates? The question is: can this object be driven, can it remember, can it separate signals, and can one train a readout from its internal state?

1. Reservoir computing: using dynamics instead of designing algorithms

A useful way to think about reservoir computing is that it separates computation into two parts: a physical transformation and a learned interpretation. The reservoir transforms the input into internal motion. The readout interprets this motion.

This is different from the usual deep-learning picture, where many layers of parameters are adjusted by backpropagation. In reservoir computing, the internal network may be random, fixed, biological, optical, mechanical, chemical, or electronic. One does not usually optimize all its microscopic degrees of freedom. Instead, one relies on the fact that a nonlinear dynamical system naturally mixes signals, stores recent history, and creates a high-dimensional representation of the input.

This sounds abstract, but the recipe is very concrete. Drive the system with a signal. Measure many internal variables. Fit a linear map from those measured variables to the desired answer. Then deploy the system, often in closed loop.

The reservoir therefore behaves a little like a complicated pond. You throw stones into it, waves interfere, and the pattern on the surface contains information about what was thrown in recently. You do not control every water molecule. You only learn how to read the waves.

This is why physical reservoirs are interesting. Matter already computes in the sense that it evolves. The question is whether we can make that evolution useful.

2. Cortical Labs and biological reservoirs

Cortical Labs is one of the more striking examples of this idea. Their systems use living neurons grown on a silicon interface. The neurons are not a metaphor for a neural network. They are actual biological cells, electrically stimulated and electrically recorded through an electrode array.

The company calls its current platform CL1, a biological computer designed to let researchers deploy code to living neural cultures and observe how they process information. The basic loop is reservoir-like. A digital system translates information from a task into stimulation patterns. The neural culture responds with spiking activity. That activity is recorded, decoded, and used to affect the task. The biological system is therefore placed inside an input-output loop.

One can think of this as a strange hybrid. The environment is digital. The interface is electronic. The adaptive medium is biological. The computation is not located in any one of these pieces alone, but in the closed loop between them.

This is important. When people hear that neurons are “playing a game,” it is tempting to imagine a tiny brain with a screen. That is not what is happening. The neurons receive structured electrical stimulation derived from the game state, and their collective electrical response is mapped back into game actions. The biological culture is not seeing pixels in the human sense. It is being coupled to a task through an artificial sensory-motor interface.

3. Why make neurons play Doom?

The public-facing result that attracted attention was the use of living neurons interfaced with Cortical Labs' platform to play Doom. This follows an earlier line of experiments in which neural cultures were trained on simpler games such as Pong. The point is not that this is a good way to build a games console. It is a benchmark for closed-loop adaptive behavior.

Pong is almost one-dimensional: track the ball, move the paddle. Doom, even in a simplified setup, is richer: there is movement, orientation, obstacles, enemies, damage, and delayed consequences. This makes it a more interesting test of whether a biological substrate can be coupled to a digital world and produce meaningful adaptive responses.

The mechanism is again reservoir-like. The game state is encoded into electrical signals. These signals stimulate the neurons. The neurons respond through patterns of activity. A decoder maps those patterns into actions such as moving or shooting. Feedback from the game changes the subsequent stimulation. The system is therefore not simply classifying static images. It is embedded in a dynamical loop.

One should be careful not to oversell this. The performance is primitive compared with ordinary software. But that is not the interesting comparison. The interesting point is that a living, adaptive physical substrate can be used as part of a computational control loop at all. It is a powerful demonstration of the idea that computation can be done in many different ways, and that we should not limit ourselves to silicon logic.

4. Physical systems as computers

Biological reservoirs are only one instance of a broader trend. Many groups are exploring computation with physical systems: photonic reservoirs, spintronic devices, mechanical metamaterials, chemical oscillators, soft matter, and memristive networks. These systems are very different physically, but the computational philosophy is similar.

Rather than forcing matter to imitate a conventional processor, one asks what the matter already does well. Does it oscillate? Does it synchronize? Does it remember? Does it relax slowly? Does it respond nonlinearly? Does it separate inputs into different regions of state space? If yes, then perhaps it can serve as a computational substrate.

This is a reversal of the usual engineering instinct. We normally try to suppress the messy dynamics of matter so that clean digital logic remains. Reservoir computing does the opposite. It uses the mess.

Memristive systems are particularly natural in this context because they combine nonlinearity with memory. A memristor is a circuit element whose resistance depends on its internal state, and that internal state depends on the history of the current or voltage applied to it. In other words, memory is not added externally. It is part of the device physics.

5. SOMN: a self-organizing memristive network

Let me now move from living neurons to a substrate that is not alive, but that is also not quite a conventional circuit: a self-organizing memristive network, or SOMN. The acronym is ugly, but the object is beautiful. A SOMN is a network of nanoscale resistive-memory elements whose conductances change when they are electrically stimulated. In the most common experimental examples, these networks are made from nanowires or nanoparticles. They are not laid down one by one like transistors in a clean digital architecture. They are often grown, deposited, or assembled in a way that produces a dense, irregular, strongly interconnected physical graph.

That irregularity is not a defect. It is the point. In a conventional digital circuit, disorder is usually the enemy because the aim is to make every logical operation reproducible, thresholded, and independent of microscopic accidents. In a SOMN, the disorder is part of the computational material. The network has many possible conduction pathways. When voltage is applied, some junctions become more conductive, others relax, and the effective circuit reorganizes itself. The system has memory because its present conductance depends on its past stimulation. It has nonlinearity because the response is not proportional to the input. It has collective behavior because all of this happens under Kirchhoff constraints: the current and voltage distributions are global objects, not local decorations placed on independent edges.

This is why I find the comparison with biological matter useful, even if one should not push it too far. In a biological neural culture, the adaptive units are cells and synapses. In a SOMN, the adaptive units are memristive junctions and conduction paths. In both cases, the substrate is not merely storing numbers. It is changing its own effective wiring as it is driven. This is the sense in which the network is self-organizing. The input does not simply pass through a fixed filter. It reshapes the filter.

The recent perspective by myself, Gianluca Milano, Adam Stieg, Carlo Ricciardi, Simon Brown, and Zdenka Kuncic, Self-Organising Memristive Networks as Physical Learning Systems, makes precisely this point. SOMNs should not be understood only as another way of implementing artificial neural networks. They are better viewed as physical learning systems: matter whose intrinsic dynamics can be harnessed for adaptation, classification, memory, and control. This is not the usual neuromorphic story in which one first writes down an algorithm inspired by the brain and then asks hardware to execute it. The more interesting possibility is that some of the learning is already in the physics.

At the microscopic level, the relevant effect is resistive switching. In a nanowire network, for instance, the junctions between wires can change conductance because ions move, conductive filaments form or rupture, and atomic-scale rearrangements modify the local transport. A single junction can therefore behave a little like a synapse: its conductance depends on its recent electrical history. But the crucial step is to stop thinking about a single junction. A SOMN is a network, and networks do things that single devices do not. A local conductance change modifies the current distribution, which changes the fields elsewhere, which changes the probability that other junctions switch. The network responds to itself.

This feedback is where the physics becomes interesting. The network can show short-term plasticity, because recently activated pathways remain temporarily easier to activate. It can show longer-lived changes, because repeated stimulation can reinforce certain conduction paths. It can show avalanche-like dynamics, because one switching event can redistribute voltages and trigger others. It can also show transitions between low-conductance and high-conductance regimes, which is why statistical physics enters naturally. One is not just measuring a device characteristic. One is watching a disordered adaptive system move between collective states.

This is also why these systems are not simply noisy resistors. A noisy resistor fluctuates around a fixed response. A SOMN changes the response itself. The network has an internal state, even if that state is not directly addressable junction by junction. In fact, the lack of microscopic addressability is part of the appeal. Biological brains do not give us access to every synapse as an individually programmable variable either. Yet they compute. A SOMN asks whether a much simpler abiotic material can do something analogous: not in the sense of being intelligent, but in the sense of using collective physical adaptation as a resource.

In reservoir-computing language, the SOMN is a reservoir because an input voltage signal drives the network into a high-dimensional internal trajectory. The measurable output may be a set of terminal currents, voltages, effective conductances, or selected internal variables in a simulation. The task is not to control every microscopic junction. The task is to stimulate the system, let its memory and nonlinearity expand the input into a richer dynamical representation, and then train a readout.

$$ E(t) \longrightarrow w(t), I(t), V(t) \longrightarrow y(t). $$

Here \(E(t)\) denotes the external drive, while \(w(t)\) represents the internal memory variables of the memristive network. The readout \(y(t)\) may be a classification, a prediction, or a control signal. The important point is that the intermediate state is not an artificial hidden layer stored in RAM. It is a physical state of matter.

This gives a slightly different way of thinking about learning. In software, learning usually means changing parameters in an explicit model. In a SOMN, learning can also mean guiding the substrate into a useful dynamical regime. One can use the network as a reservoir, keeping the physical system fixed during readout training. Or one can exploit the adaptive conductance changes themselves, so that the substrate participates more directly in learning. This is why SOMNs sit somewhere between reservoir computing, neuromorphic engineering, adaptive matter, and statistical mechanics.

There is also a practical motivation. If one wants intelligence at the edge — in a sensor, a robot, a medical device, or an autonomous system — then sending all data to a giant server is often wasteful. The physical world produces continuous, noisy, time-dependent signals. A physical learning system that sits directly at the interface with those signals, processes them in situ, and adapts with low power is conceptually very attractive. Whether SOMNs become a mature technology is a separate question. But the direction is clear: stop separating sensing, memory, computation, and adaptation into different boxes when matter can sometimes do several of these things at once.

So the right mental image is not a tiny digital computer made out of nanowires. It is closer to an adaptive electrical material. You perturb it, it reorganizes, it remembers, it relaxes, and if you are clever enough you can use that trajectory as a computation.

6. A closed memristive-network equation

The advantage of SOMNs is that they look messy enough to be useful. The danger is that they look so messy that one might be tempted to describe them only phenomenologically. That would be a mistake. One of the nice things about memristive circuits is that, under reasonable assumptions, one can write closed equations for the internal memory variables of the whole network. These equations do not ignore the circuit constraints. On the contrary, the constraints appear explicitly.

A useful compact model for this kind of network appears in The complex dynamics of memristive circuits: analytical results and universal slow relaxation and in related work on the graph-theoretic description of memristive networks. In one common form, the memory vector \(w(t)\) evolves according to an equation of the type

$$ \frac{dw}{dt} = -\frac{w}{\beta} + \frac{\alpha}{V_0} \Omega \left(I-\xi W \Omega\right)^{-1} E(t), $$

with

$$ W=\mathrm{diag}(w_1,\ldots,w_N), \qquad \xi=\frac{R_{\mathrm{off}}-R_{\mathrm{on}}}{R_{\mathrm{off}}}. $$

Here \(w_i(t)\) is the internal state of the \(i\)-th memristive element. Roughly speaking, it tells us where that element sits between a high-resistance and a low-resistance state. The vector \(E(t)\) represents the external voltage sources or effective driving signal applied to the network. The parameter \(\beta\) sets a relaxation timescale, while \(\alpha\) and \(V_0\) set the strength and scale of the driven response. The parameter \(\xi\) measures the contrast between the two limiting resistances of the memristor.

The first term, \(-w/\beta\), is the boring but essential part: memory fades. Without some fading memory, a reservoir can become too rigid, carrying the scars of old inputs forever. The second term is the interesting part. It says that the drive does not simply act independently on each memristor. It is filtered through the topology and instantaneous state of the whole circuit. That is the role of the matrix

$$ \Omega \left(I-\xi W\Omega\right)^{-1}. $$

The matrix \(\Omega\) is a projector determined by the circuit graph. If \(B\) is the node-edge incidence matrix, then one useful expression is

$$ \Omega = I-B^T(BB^T)^+B, $$

where the plus sign denotes the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse. This projector encodes Kirchhoff constraints. It removes unphysical edge configurations and keeps only the degrees of freedom compatible with the circuit topology. In other words, the graph is not a decoration around the dynamics. The graph is inside the dynamics.

This is the mathematical reason why SOMNs are not just collections of isolated memory elements. A voltage applied at one part of the network changes the current distribution everywhere, subject to the graph constraints. That redistribution changes the evolution of other memory variables, which changes the effective resistance landscape, which changes the current distribution again. The inverse matrix is not just algebraic clutter. It is the signature of feedback.

This also clarifies why topology matters. A tree, a dense random graph, a lattice, and a scale-free network do not merely give different wiring diagrams. They give different projectors, different cycles, different correlations, and different dynamical responses. In a reservoir-computing setting, that means they give different computational features. Some topologies may store input history well but be too linear. Others may be highly nonlinear but forget too quickly. Others may sit close to a transition where small inputs produce large, structured responses. That last regime is often where reservoir computing becomes interesting.

The SOMN perspective paper emphasizes precisely this bridge between microscopic physics and macroscopic learning. At the junction scale, one has ionic motion, filament formation, tunneling, and local conductance changes. At the network scale, one has plasticity, avalanches, phase transitions, and long-range correlations. At the computational scale, one asks whether these dynamics can be used for memory, classification, prediction, and control. The same object is therefore a device, a circuit, a disordered physical system, and a learning substrate.

Mean-field models are useful here because experiments often measure global quantities such as effective conductance, not every microscopic junction state. A mean-field theory replaces the full network by a reduced description in terms of macroscopic variables, for instance the average conductance or the fraction of active junctions. This loses microscopic detail, but it can reveal transitions between low- and high-conductance regimes, the effect of voltage thresholds, and the conditions under which the network switches collectively rather than gradually.

From the point of view of computation, this is not a side issue. A reservoir needs both memory and separability. If it forgets instantly, it cannot process temporal signals. If it remembers everything too strongly, it becomes saturated and unresponsive. If it is too linear, it cannot generate useful features. If it is too nonlinear, it may become unstable or chaotic in the wrong way. The computational sweet spot is usually a balance: enough memory, enough nonlinearity, enough stability, and enough sensitivity to input. In memristive networks, these properties are physical. They are controlled by device timescales, resistance contrast, network topology, stimulation protocol, and operating point.

This is also the lesson of the work with Forrest Sheldon and Artemy Kolchinsky on the computational capacity of LRC, memristive, and hybrid reservoirs: Computational capacity of lrc, memristive, and hybrid reservoirs. Purely linear circuits are good at memory but poor at nonlinear transformations. Purely memristive circuits provide nonlinear memory, but one still has to tune them carefully. Hybrid reservoirs, where inductors, capacitors, resistors, and memristive elements are combined, can trade memory against nonlinearity in a more controlled way. This is important because it moves the discussion away from the vague statement “memristors are brain-like” toward the more useful engineering question: what functions can this physical reservoir approximate, and how does that capacity scale with system size?

So the model equation above is not just a toy. It is a compact way of saying something physically important: the computation performed by a memristive network is the result of memory, nonlinearity, and Kirchhoff-constrained topology acting together. That is exactly why such a network is a natural candidate for reservoir computing.

7. From memory to control: a memristive reservoir for satellite dynamics

Up to this point, I have described the reservoir as something that transforms signals. But the really interesting case is when the reservoir is placed inside a control loop. This brings us back to the biological example. The neurons in Cortical Labs' system are not merely classifying a frozen frame from a game. Their activity changes the game, and the changed game produces the next input. The same closed-loop structure appears in the satellite-control toy model.

The setup is simple in spirit. There is a satellite, or more precisely a dynamical system representing the satellite's motion. There is a reference orbit we would like it to follow. A perturbation pushes the satellite away from that reference. The controller observes some error signal, for instance the difference between the current state and the desired state, and produces a thrust command. The question is whether a reservoir with memory can help produce a useful control signal.

Schematically, the loop is

$$ \text{orbit error} \longrightarrow \text{encoding} \longrightarrow \text{memristive reservoir} \longrightarrow \text{linear readout} \longrightarrow \text{thrust} \longrightarrow \text{new orbit error}. $$

This is the entire philosophy of reservoir control in one line. The memristive network is not solving the equations of orbital mechanics symbolically. It is not deriving a control law in the way one would in a classical control textbook. Instead, the orbital error is converted into a time-dependent electrical drive \(E(t)\). That drive pushes the memristive network through a trajectory of internal states \(w(t)\). A readout then maps those states to a thrust command.

$$ u_{\mathrm{thrust}}(t)=W_{\mathrm{out}}\,\phi(w(t)). $$

Here \(\phi(w(t))\) denotes the reservoir features. In the simplest implementation, \(\phi(w)=w\), so the readout is just a linear combination of memory variables. In a slightly richer implementation, one may include currents, voltages, delayed states, nonlinear functions of \(w\), or combinations of these. The point is that the trainable part remains simple. The complexity is delegated to the physical dynamics.

The satellite itself can be represented by a state vector \(s(t)\), containing positions and velocities, evolving under natural dynamics plus a control input:

$$ \dot{s}(t)=f(s(t)) + G(s(t))u_{\mathrm{thrust}}(t). $$

The reference orbit \(s_{\mathrm{ref}}(t)\) gives an error

$$ e(t)=s(t)-s_{\mathrm{ref}}(t). $$

This error is encoded as the reservoir drive:

$$ E(t)=A_{\mathrm{in}}e(t), $$

where \(A_{\mathrm{in}}\) is an input mask or encoding matrix. The reservoir then evolves according to the memristive network equation, and the readout produces the thrust. During training, one adjusts \(W_{\mathrm{out}}\), not the whole reservoir. This is the great practical convenience of reservoir computing: the difficult nonlinear temporal expansion is performed by the substrate, while the learning problem is often reduced to a regression problem.

In a standard supervised version, one first generates examples of good corrective behavior. These may come from a known controller, an optimal-control calculation, a hand-designed stabilizing policy, or a set of simulated trajectories where the desired thrust is known. The reservoir is driven by the corresponding error signals, producing a time series of internal states. One then solves

$$ W_{\mathrm{out}} = \arg\min_W \sum_t \left\| u_{\mathrm{target}}(t)-W\phi(w(t)) \right\|^2 + \lambda \|W\|^2, $$

where the last term is the usual ridge regularization. After training, the target controller is removed. The satellite is perturbed, the error drives the reservoir, and the reservoir readout generates the thrust in real time.

Why use a reservoir here at all? Because control is not just a function of the instantaneous error. In many dynamical systems, what matters is the recent history: whether the satellite is moving toward or away from the reference orbit, whether the error is growing or shrinking, whether a correction has already been applied, and how the system has responded. A memoryless controller sees only a snapshot. A reservoir sees a fading trace of the recent past.

This is exactly where a memristive reservoir is conceptually natural. The memory variables \(w_i(t)\) are not extra registers attached to the circuit. They are the circuit. The network carries a physical trace of previous inputs, and because the response is nonlinear, that trace is not merely a moving average. It is a structured, topology-dependent embedding of the recent error history. The readout can then use this embedding to produce a control action.

In the reservoir-control mode, the perturbation is fed into the memristive reservoir, and the readout attempts to pull the trajectory back toward the reference orbit. It is explained the idea of how this is implemented, how the training works, and how the reservoir dynamics affect the control performance.

The toy is not meant to claim that this is the optimal way to control a satellite. A serious spacecraft controller must deal with constraints, uncertainty, fuel limits, actuator saturation, safety margins, verification, and all the things that make real control engineering difficult. The point is more basic and, I think, more interesting: a physical reservoir can be coupled to a dynamical environment and used as part of a feedback controller. This places the memristive example and the Cortical Labs example in the same conceptual family. In one case, the environment is a game and the substrate is a living neural culture. In the other, the environment is an orbital dynamical system and the substrate is a memristive network. The details are completely different, but the architecture is the same:

$$ \text{state of world} \rightarrow \text{physical substrate} \rightarrow \text{action} \rightarrow \text{new state of world}. $$

Once this loop is closed, the substrate is no longer just a passive device being measured. It becomes part of the causal structure of the task. Its memory affects the action, the action affects the environment, and the environment drives the next state of the substrate. This is where unconventional computing becomes more than an exotic way to classify data. It becomes a way to build adaptive physical agents.

There is a deeper point here about what we mean by computation. A Turing machine computes by manipulating symbols according to rules. A digital computer implements this idea with astonishing reliability by using thresholded logic and error correction. A reservoir computer computes differently. It computes by being a dynamical system whose response can be interpreted. A memristive reservoir computes by letting electrical memory, nonlinearity, and topology do part of the work. A biological reservoir computes by letting living excitable tissue adapt inside a feedback loop.

None of this replaces digital computers. In fact, all of these demonstrations still rely on ordinary computers for encoding, decoding, measurement, simulation, training, and analysis. But they change the role of the digital computer. The digital machine is no longer necessarily the place where all computation happens. It can become the scaffold around another physical substrate. It can stimulate, measure, train, and stabilize a piece of strange matter that performs the transformation we need.

That, to me, is the real story behind neurons playing games and memristive networks controlling toy satellites. The headline is weird, but the underlying question is serious: what kinds of matter can be made computational, and what kinds of tasks become easier when memory and dynamics are already built into the material?

SOMNs are one answer. Biological neural cultures are another. Optical cavities, spintronic systems, soft matter, chemical media, and mechanical metamaterials are others. They are all reminders that computation is not only what happens after we have cleaned matter up into bits. Sometimes computation begins before that, in the messy physical evolution we usually try to suppress.
