The need for more "Indie" Institutes and National labs in Europe

February 28th, 2026

I’ve been quite busy lately—in the good way, the kind of busy where you end up thinking about the plumbing of research as much as the research itself. Also, it's been quite busy at work.

I returned to Italy (workwise) after roughly 15 years. I have worked abroad since I was 22. I've had the chance to see quite a few research models, and also how academia has changed over this period, in a way that quite honestly I'm not particularly fond of. I see a lot of concentration of resources, maybe in a way that is unjustified, but I digress. Moving between different environments over the years (universities, national labs, independent institutes, and now industry) has made one thing very hard to ignore: the institution you’re in quietly shapes what kinds of problems you can even afford to work on. I keep telling people that it does not matter (as a scientist) whether you're an academic or not, the urge you typically feel as a scientist is to try to solve problems, and if you move out of academia you should be intellectually stimulated. I've been reading the book The Jasons by Ann Finkbeiner (see here) talking about this "secret" group of scientists working for the government, post Manhattan project. There are a lot of stories that resonate with me and it is quite entertaining. I will use as a way to segway into how different Europe is in this respect.

Europe produces astonishing science. But whenever the conversation turns from “papers” to platforms—chips, energy systems, AI infrastructure, biomanufacturing, quantum hardware, resilient supply chains—we often talk as if excellent universities plus competitive grants are the whole story.

They are not. And in this post I want to start talking more explicitly about alternative models for doing science: mission-oriented national laboratories, and the ecology of independent institutes that sit outside the standard university pipeline. These institutions are often boring, expensive, and absolutely decisive—especially if what you want is not just discovery, but durable capability. They are somehow in the shadow of bigger institutions, but they are also the places where a lot of the most consequential work happens. They are the places where you can build and maintain shared platforms, run long-horizon programs, and turn use-inspired basic research into something that can actually be used. What I really liked about Los Alamos is that almost nobody was bragging about what he or she was doing, not too much anyway. It also became very "corporate", in the bad sense of the word, which made me loose the magic.

In general, I'm trying to argue that we should be more ambitious. While Italy seem to lack a strategic long term plan, it does not seem to be that the other countries have a coherent vision either, in particular not in a way that is coherent with the "Europe" story. It is a patchwork of ecosystems, but it requires a bit of a reorganization. I don't mean to ditch the "Human Brain Project" approach, but clearly the project did not work, and I have not seen a post-mortem. The project ran for 10 years, but aside from producing scientific papers on the subject, it did not produce a platform, or a community, or a training program. It is a bit of a shame, because the project had a lot of potential, and it was quite well funded. Now, Europe I think is still quite ahead in Neuromorphic computing compared to the US. Yet, companies like Intel and IBM have been investing in this area for a long time, and they have been doing it in a way that is quite different from the way the "Human Brain Project" was doing it. So I want to use this point to try to argue that we do need to think more about the fact that building the car is useless if we don't get it out of the garage. Using another jargon, we should stop ``fucking spiders". (I love Austrlia.) Here is my thesis.

Thesis: Europe’s research ecosystem is strong at discovery and training, but structurally weaker at (i) sustaining long-horizon, high-fixed-cost programs, (ii) building shared platforms that industry can reliably sit on, and (iii) turning “use-inspired basic research” into durable capability. This is exactly the niche national labs and indipendent institutes were built to occupy.

The university model is starting to crack

This part is personal, so let me start there. I’ve been in and out of academia since 2014—sometimes fully inside it, sometimes just adjacent to it. I returned to Los Alamos for part of my career, and even now I’m “back outside” again, working as a researcher in a private company. In between, I’ve also spent meaningful time (often part-time, sometimes as a visitor) in places that don’t fit the standard university template: Perimeter Institute, the London Institute for Mathematical Sciences, and the Santa Fe Institute.

Moving between these environments does something interesting to your perception. When you’re only in one system, you tend to interpret its pressures as “the natural state of research.” When you step out and come back in, you realize how much of what we call “how science works” is actually “how a specific incentive machine currently works.”

Universities remain remarkable engines of discovery and training—some of the best thinking I’ve ever seen happens in seminar rooms and departmental hallways. But the model is visibly strained in ways that weren’t as pronounced when I first entered it. The first strain is volume: the number of publications keeps rising, and with it the pressure to always be producing. Paper counts, citation metrics, conference acceptances, and grant totals become stand-ins for understanding. If your career is evaluated through proxies, you learn to optimize proxies.

The second strain is the reward model’s tendency to amplify fashion. When funding and hiring reward what looks new, hot, and easily legible to committees, researchers respond rationally: they cluster around trends. Sometimes this accelerates a field; sometimes it produces a kind of coordinated shallowness—fast cycles, thin slices, and too little time spent on slow work: careful theory, rigorous replication, negative results, infrastructure, tooling, or the boring-but-critical act of making a method reliable.

The third strain is human: the scarcity of permanent positions. A large fraction of research labor is carried by people on short contracts, constantly reapplying for funding and stability. That shapes what problems are “safe” to attempt, how much time you can afford to spend on risky ideas, and even how mentorship works when everyone is living near the edge of uncertainty.

And then there is the publication pipeline itself. Peer review is essential, but it is also increasingly overloaded and oddly structured: an enormous amount of unpaid labor, compressed timelines, fragile norms, and sometimes a sense that the goal becomes “satisfy the process” rather than “improve the science.” Many of us have experienced the doom-loop version of this: long revise-resubmit cycles, conflicting referee demands, and months of latency that do not obviously correlate with better outcomes.

The paradox is that almost everyone can see these incentives, and yet individually we behave rationally inside them: we publish more because others publish more; we chase fashion because committees reward it; we accept bad equilibria because exiting has a cost. It’s a system that can work locally while failing globally.

One reason I’m convinced this is not just “complaining” is that I’ve seen how different it can feel elsewhere. In places like Los Alamos, or at independent institutes like Perimeter, LIMS, and SFI, the texture of work changes: there’s often more tolerance for long-horizon thinking, more emphasis on building shared intellectual or technical infrastructure, and (crucially) a different relationship to “output.” You still publish, of course—but the publication is more often a byproduct of building or understanding something, rather than the central object being optimized.

The conclusion—one we’ll return to later—is simple: the university is not the unique place where research happens, nor the only institutional form through which people can contribute to knowledge. In fact, if we want science to compound into capability, we probably need multiple complementary habitats for research: universities for training and discovery, independent institutes for focused depth, and mission-driven labs (and other long-horizon organizations) for platforms, infrastructure, and sustained programs.

A short origin story: Vannevar Bush and the post-war bargain

To understand why the "DOE lab" model exists, let's start from the beginning. The Manhattan project. In July 1945, Vannevar Bush—then Director of the U.S. Office of Scientific Research and Development—delivered Science, the Endless Frontier, a report to President Truman that shaped modern science policy. It was supposed to be a document that explained why the Manhattan project worked. Bush argued that publicly supported research is a pillar of health, security, and prosperity, and that government should sustain the “upstream” research pipeline in peacetime, not only during emergencies. The report is now canonical; it is also surprisingly readable. I bought it a few years ago and read parts of it (it is a technical report). See the report itself: Bush (1945), PDF, or NSF’s 75th anniversary edition: NSF edition.

The key point for this post is not “basic science good” (everyone says that). It’s the institutional response: large-scale research capacity requires stable organizations with stable funding, able to run big machines, maintain facilities, keep teams together, and pursue missions that outlive a single grant cycle.

In the U.S., the post-war era produced a layered ecosystem: universities, agencies, and a network of national laboratories. In the 1940s–1950s the Atomic Energy Commission built a lab system to host large machines and long-horizon nuclear science; DOE’s historical summary captures that logic well: DOE Office of Science history.

Pasteur’s Quadrant: escaping the “basic vs applied” cartoon

The Bush-era story is often retold as a neat dichotomy: “basic research” in universities, “applied research” in industry. It’s a useful simplification—until it becomes policy dogma.

Donald Stokes’ book Pasteur’s Quadrant (1997) pushed back on this tidy story. Cozmin Ududec once suggested I read it, and I now think of it as a natural “follow up” to Bush’s report—less about why public science matters (Bush’s main task), and more about what kinds of science policy tends to misclassify when it forces everything into “basic” versus “applied.” (Brookings has a clean overview page for the book: Pasteur’s Quadrant.)

The historical backdrop here is important. After WWII, Bush’s framing was enormously influential: protect fundamental research (especially in universities), and trust that applications will flow downstream. That “linear model” was never completely wrong, but over time it hardened into a cartoon: universities do curiosity-driven basics; industry does applications; government funds one end and buys the other. Stokes’ point is that this cartoon misses a huge—and arguably central—region of the landscape: research that is driven by practical needs and simultaneously reaches for fundamental understanding.

Why “Pasteur’s Quadrant”? Because Louis Pasteur is the perfect symbol of this hybrid mode. Pasteur was not doing “basic science” in the modern, detached sense, nor was he doing narrow product development. He was trying to solve concrete societal problems (fermentation, spoilage, disease, vaccination), and in the process he created deep conceptual frameworks—germ theory, microbiology, immunological principles—that reshaped science itself. The use-case was not a distraction from fundamentals; it was the engine that forced new fundamentals into existence.

Stokes proposes a simple 2×2 map with two axes: (i) “Is there a quest for fundamental understanding?” and (ii) “Is there consideration of use?” These two questions generate four quadrants:

  • Bohr’s Quadrant (high understanding, low use): pure, curiosity-driven basic research—the emblem is Niels Bohr. The goal is to understand, and any applications are incidental or far downstream.
  • Edison’s Quadrant (low understanding, high use): pure applied research / invention—the emblem is Thomas Edison. The goal is to make something work; deep theory is optional.
  • Pasteur’s Quadrant (high understanding, high use): use-inspired basic research. Practical constraints and societal needs guide the questions, but the answers are fundamental and general.
  • (The fourth quadrant) (low understanding, low use): work that is neither seeking deep understanding nor directly driven by use—often cataloging, routine measurement, or activity that can be valuable in context but isn’t the archetype of discovery or innovation. (Stokes’ point is not to shame it; it’s to show it’s a different mode.)

The policy punchline is that the “basic vs applied” dichotomy collapses Bohr and Pasteur into one bucket, and Edison into another, then pretends the buckets cover reality. But Pasteur’s Quadrant behaves differently from Bohr’s in terms of time horizons, infrastructure, evaluation, and institutional needs. Use-inspired basic research often requires stable platforms, long-lived teams, and feedback loops with real-world constraints. It is simultaneously harder to do in a pure grant-and-paper economy and more likely to produce the kind of compounding capability societies care about.

This is exactly why national labs and independent institutes matter in the story I’m telling here: they are institutional forms that can hold Pasteur’s Quadrant work without forcing it to pretend it is either “pure basic” or “pure applied.” They make it legitimate to say: we are building understanding because we are trying to build something that must work.

If you buy Stokes’ framing, you immediately see the institutional gap: use-inspired basic research needs places where (i) the “use” is real and persistent (missions), and (ii) the “basic” is not punished for being long-horizon. National labs are one way to hold that tension productively.

What a national lab is (and isn’t)

A national lab is not “a university but bigger,” and not “an industrial R&D department owned by the state.” In the best case, it is a platform institution:

  • Runs shared, high-fixed-cost infrastructure (cleanrooms, accelerators, testbeds, HPC, metrology, field sites).
  • Keeps long-lived engineering teams that can iterate hardware and processes over years, not semesters.
  • Turns prototypes into reproducible capability (standards, reference designs, validation pipelines).
  • Bridges sectors: universities ↔ industry ↔ government, with rules that make collaboration routine.
  • Holds missions that are too cross-cutting (or too unsexy) for single-PI grants.

The “boring” parts matter: procurement, QA, safety, security, uptime, documentation, and institutional memory. Scientific revolutions are often enabled by reliable plumbing.

Europe today: excellence, fragmentation, and the “valley of platforms”

Europe already has world-class intergovernmental labs and infrastructures—CERN is the obvious flagship— and strong national institutes in several countries (France’s CEA is a good example of a mission-oriented organization: CEA overview).

But the continent-wide picture is still fragmented along borders, ministries, and funding instruments. That fragmentation is not just a governance annoyance—it shapes what kinds of technical work are feasible.

Here is the pattern I keep seeing:

  • We fund discovery well.
  • We fund “projects” well.
  • We underfund the platform phase: building and operating the shared capabilities that many projects depend on.

Call this the “valley of platforms.” It’s adjacent to the valley of death, but different: you can have brilliant science and even good startups, and still lack stable testbeds, pilot lines, validation frameworks, and compute/measurement infrastructure that make an ecosystem compound.

Why now: energy, security, compute, and industrial sovereignty

The argument for labs becomes sharper under three pressures that are hard to solve with short, competitive grants alone:

  • Energy transition: grids, storage, materials, reactors, and industrial heat require testbeds and standards.
  • Security and resilience: dual-use tech, supply chains, cyber-physical infrastructure, and trusted manufacturing.
  • Compute as infrastructure: AI training/validation, scientific HPC, and sovereign capability are now strategic.

These are not just “research questions.” They are systems. Systems need places that can integrate components, run long experiments, maintain facilities, and keep engineering talent attached to the mission.

The moment you say “pilot line,” “certification,” “reference implementation,” “national testbed,” or “24/7 facility,” you are already describing lab-shaped work.

A design sketch for Europe: principles, not bureaucracy

If Europe wants “national labs,” it probably shouldn’t copy-paste a single-country model. The scale is continental, the governance is plural, and the strength of Europe is diversity. So I’d suggest principles:

  • Networked, not monolithic: a federation of labs with shared roadmaps and interoperability, not one mega-agency.
  • Platform metrics: measure uptime, access, throughput, and validated capability—not just publications.
  • Porous boundaries: joint appointments, visiting engineer programs, industrial resident teams.
  • Mission clarity: pick a few areas where scale and sovereignty matter (energy systems, compute, biosecurity, advanced manufacturing).
  • Procurement and IP that enable collaboration: make it easy to build with industry without turning labs into vendors.
  • Training as a first-class output: staff scientists and engineers mentoring the next cohort, not only PhD pipelines.

Europe already knows how to do parts of this (shared infrastructures, cross-border facilities, scientific governance). The opportunity is to extend that competence from “big science instruments” to “big technology platforms.”

Takeaways

  • Vannevar Bush’s 1945 bargain was not just about funding science—it was about building institutions that can sustain capability. (Bush/NSF edition)
  • Stokes’ Pasteur’s Quadrant is a reminder that the best research is often use-inspired and fundamental—lab-shaped work. (Brookings page)
  • Europe’s excellence is real, but fragmentation makes platform-building harder than it needs to be.
  • A network of mission-oriented labs (national + European) is one of the most practical ways to make European innovation compound.

I’ll likely follow this with a more concrete proposal: what specific “platform missions” make sense for Europe in 2026, and what an actually workable governance/funding split could look like (without creating a new cathedral of paperwork).
