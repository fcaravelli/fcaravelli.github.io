Your computer is a heater

We usually describe computers in the language of logic, algorithms, transistors, memory, and software. We are so used to that language that it can be hard to see the machine in any other way. Chips, gates, instructions, and data structures are the natural way to talk about computation, and they are very useful for many purposes. This has been like this since the late 80s, when the stored-program architecture became dominant and software began to be the main way we interact with machines. Between the 1930s and 1960s it was more common to talk about the physical implementation of computation. One of my favorite models of computing is lambda calculus, which is a very abstract mathematical model of computation. It is not a physical model, but it is a very useful one for understanding the theoretical limits of computation (actually, I have an idea for a blog post about that, so I will not say more here). But that language has fallen out of fashion, and it is now mostly used by a small community of researchers in physics and engineering. Gates, operations, registers, algorithms are the dominant way to talk about computation, and that is fine. But it is not the only way, and it can be useful to step back and look at the machine from a different angle. But there is a much more primitive description that is impossible to avoid: a computer is a device that takes high-quality electrical energy and turns almost all of it into heat. Hear me out.

This sounds flippant, but it is not. The machine on your desk, the server in a data center, the GPU training a large model, and even your phone are all physical systems running far from equilibrium. The easiest way to see this is to look at the power consumption of a computer. A typical laptop consumes tens of watts, while a high-end GPU can consume hundreds of watts. That energy is not just being used to do useful work; it is also being dissipated as heat. In fact, the heat generated by a computer is often the main reason why we need fans, cooling systems, and thermal management. The machine is not just processing information; it is also exchanging energy with its environment, and that exchange inevitably produces heat. They compute, yes, but in doing so they also dissipate. However my point here is not just that they dissipate, but that the dissipation is a fundamental part of what they do. It has to do with the logical operations they performs. Around 1961, Rolf Landauer showed that there is a fundamental thermodynamic cost to erasing information. This is known as Landauer’s principle, and it states that erasing one bit of information requires a minimum amount of energy, which is dissipated as heat. This principle is not just a theoretical curiosity; it has practical implications for the design of computers and the limits of computation. It tells us that there is a fundamental trade-off between the speed of computation and the amount of heat generated. If we want to compute faster, we have to accept that we will generate more heat. If we want to reduce heat, we have to accept that we will compute slower. This is not just a technical detail; it is a fundamental aspect of the physics of computation.

The machine is not just a logical device; it is also a thermodynamic one. I have talked recently about thermodynamic computing, which is a paradigm that takes this idea seriously and tries to design computers that are more efficient by leveraging thermodynamic principles. Even in that case, however, the heat dissipation is connected (literally), to the tradeoff between diffusion and drift of electrons across a potential barrier. The point is that the heat is not a side effect; it is a consequence of the physical processes that implement computation.

Information is physical, as Rolf Landauer famously said. Before him, people like Szilard and Brillouin had already shown that there is a deep connection between information and thermodynamics. More recently, researchers in stochastic thermodynamics have developed a rich framework for understanding the thermodynamics of information processing. But I want to go a step further and say that not only is information physical, but also that computation has a thermodynamic structure, associated with lower bounds on the energy cost of logical operations, and with a tradeoff between speed and dissipation. This is, in a sense that I will make more precise, the thermodynamic structure of computation. I have learned this from various conversations I had with David Wolpert, and before my collaboration with him, with several conversations with Artemy Kolchinsky, who has done a lot of work on the thermodynamics of computation and the connection between information and energy.

1. Every computation ends as heat

There is a very old temptation, especially in theoretical computer science, to think of computation as something abstract and almost ethereal. We think of an algorithm as a sequence of instructions. A Turing machine manipulates symbols, and the "calculator" performing the process is a finite state machine with reading and writing capabilities on the tape. Computer scientists came up with realistic models to implement something close to a Turing machine, also called stored-program architecture, in which instructions are stored in a register. This has led us to think of computation as a purely logical process, divorced from the physical world, unless when we need to come up a device implementing it. But that is a very misleading way to think about it. In addition, we think of a (modern) computing apparatus as a device that, each time, repeats a series of processes that involve moving charges, switching voltages, stabilizing and destabilizing memory states, refreshing signals, amplifying, transmitting, and correcting. However, at each clock cycle, the machine is repeating the same process, e.g. read an instruction, decode it, execute it, write back the result, and so on. Each time the device activates the same circuitry, and what changes is the initial distribution of the state of the registers. The machine is not just doing different things; it is also repeating the same physical process over and over, while the distribution of states changes as a result of such process. In deterministic machines, such process is the same, but does not mean that the whole process is deterministic. This is due to the fact that we are still dealing with initial probability distributions over the states of the machine. Writing a poem or a blog post is not a deterministic process. The machine producing the poem is literally the same (or close), and each time the inspiration is different, and this is due to the fact that the input to the machine is probabilistic per se. This process, that processes information, as a cost due to the manipulation of the machine. LLMs are no different, in this respect. If this interests you, keep reading.

2. Why Landauer is not the whole story

The most famous statement in this area is Landauer’s principle. In its simplest form, one often hears that erasing one bit costs at least

$$ k_B T \ln 2. $$

This is one of those beautiful formulas that is both true and dangerously easy to misuse. It is true that there is a lower bound relating logical irreversibility and heat. But it is very misleading to jump from that statement to the conclusion that this is the energy cost of actual computing.

The reason is straightforward. Landauer’s bound is only the entropy-change part of the story, and it has to do to the switching of a single bit. However, the bit might belong to a more complicated distribution that cannot be factorized, e.g. that bit might have correlation due to the intricacies of the computation. Real computers are not unconstrained reversible devices slowly exploring equilibrium configurations. They are fast, clocked, modular, noisy, engineered systems operating far from equilibrium. And that means there is an additional contribution, usually called entropy production, that reflects irreversibility in the actual physical process. In the case of the mismatch cost, that we describe below, dissipation is connected to the logical irreversibility of a process. In the case of Landauer's bound, the irreversibility is due to the fact that once you have resetted the bit, you don't know what was the initial value of the bit (e.g. the process is not bijective).

In stochastic thermodynamics one writes, schematically,

$$ \Sigma = \Delta S + \sum_i \frac{Q_i}{k_B T_i}, $$

where \(\Sigma\) is the entropy production, \(\Delta S\) is the change in Shannon entropy of the system, and the \(Q_i\) are the heat flows to thermal reservoirs. The generalized Landauer bound comes from the fact that \(\Sigma \ge 0\). But the important thing is that \(\Sigma\) is often the dominant term in real machines.

This is exactly why the slogan “computation costs \(k_B T \ln 2\) per bit” is not a good guide to the thermodynamics of modern hardware. It is a lower bound in a very special limit. It is not a faithful description of what your laptop, CPU, GPU, or data center is actually paying.

Said differently: the classical story focuses on what can be recovered in principle, while the modern nonequilibrium story focuses on what is irretrievably lost in practice.

3. A clocked computer is a repeated physical process

One of the points that I find particularly elegant is the following. A modern computer is not just “doing different things” all the time. At the hardware level, it is also repeating the same kinds of physical cycles over and over: fetch, decode, update, move information, increment the program counter, repeat.

A clocked machine applies the same basic dynamical template again and again, which has been encoded in the BIOS, while the distribution over the machine’s logical states changes during the run. Biological systems also satisfy a similar feature, in the sense. The machine is therefore in a very peculiar situation: the physical mechanism is periodic, but the computational state it acts on is evolving.

That mismatch already forces dissipation. Even if one could optimize the physical process for one very special distribution over states, the same process will generally no longer be optimal once the computation has advanced and the distribution has changed. In the language of the recent literature, this extra unavoidable contribution is called mismatch cost.

This is a nice place where the programmer’s view and the thermodynamic view begin to meet. A programmer says: the same CPU cycle is applied to many different machine states during a run. A thermodynamicist says: then the same physical process is being reused off-design, and that produces irreversible entropy. These are really the same statement in two different dialects.

It also explains why the architecture of the machine matters. Periodicity, modularity, hierarchy, limited communication, finite-time execution: all of these are useful design features, but they are also thermodynamic constraints. And constraints are exactly what generate positive entropy production.

4. Programs have thermodynamic costs too

Up to here the discussion could still sound rather general. Fine, one might say: hardware dissipates, clocks dissipate, wires dissipate. But does this really tell us anything about programs?

I think the interesting answer is yes.

In recent work with Abhishek Yadav and David Wolpert, we proposed a framework (based on previous work developed by David and collaborators) to assign lower bounds on the entropy production of running actual computer programs, only based on the structure of the program. The idea is to model a stored-program machine in a simplified but faithful way: memory stores data and instructions, a control unit fetches the current instruction, a program counter tracks where we are, and each clock tick updates the machine state. This means that if you give me a program, in principle I can tell what is the minimum value that that program will dissipate notwithstanding the implementaiton.

Once you take that view seriously, the state of a program is not just the value of its variables. It is the joint state of those variables plus the program counter and the relevant internal registers. In other words, the “state of the computation” lives in a graph of possible machine states, and executing the program traces a path through that graph.

Then one can ask a thermodynamic question: given a probability distribution over possible inputs, and given the induced evolution over program states, what is the minimal unavoidable mismatch cost generated by running that program step by step?

Formally, if \(G\) is the map induced by one iteration of the machine and \(q_X\) is the prior minimizing the cost, the mismatch cost at iteration \(i\) can be written as

$$ MC_i(p_{X_0}) = D(p_{X_{i-1}} \Vert q_X) - D(Gp_{X_{i-1}} \Vert Gq_X), $$

and the total mismatch cost is the sum over all iterations until the program halts. The details are technical, but the conceptual point is simple: running a program generates a sequence of distributional updates in machine-state space, and those updates can carry a strictly positive lower-bound thermodynamic cost.

This is appealing because it moves us one step beyond slogans. We are no longer saying only that “computation costs energy.” We are saying that the logical structure of a program, when embedded in a clocked stored-program architecture, induces a concrete thermodynamic lower bound.

5. Algorithms are not thermodynamically equivalent

If that framework is even approximately right, a very natural consequence follows: two algorithms that solve the same problem need not look the same thermodynamically.

This is already familiar from ordinary complexity theory. Bubble sort and bucket sort both sort, but not in the same way. They have different control flow, different use of memory, different numbers of repeated sub-operations, and different dependence on the input distribution. So why should their dissipation look identical?

In our paper we used the mismatch-cost framework on simple examples, including sorting routines. Even at that level one already sees that thermodynamic cost depends not just on the function being computed, but on the way the program explores its state space. Input distributions matter. Repeated values can matter. Conditional branches matter. Invoking subroutines matters.

This is, to me, the beginning of a very interesting research program that allows one to think about a third component in computing, which is not only time and space resources, but also dissipation. Traditional complexity theory asks how running time or memory scale with input size. A thermodynamics of algorithms would ask, in addition, how unavoidable dissipation scales with input size, input structure, architecture, and implementation constraints.

That does not mean that tomorrow we will replace big-O notation with joules. But it does suggest that “algorithmic efficiency” has a missing physical dimension. Two programs may be equivalent as abstract computations and still differ substantially as physical processes.

Once one says it this way, it feels almost obvious. Computer science abstracted away from the substrate for extremely good reasons. But abstractions do not abolish physics. They only postpone it.

6. The uncomfortable moral

So, yes: your computer is a heater.

This is not a joke and not merely an engineering complaint. It is a reminder that computation is always implemented by a physical system constrained by time, architecture, noise, communication bottlenecks, and control. Those constraints force irreversibility. Irreversibility forces entropy production. Entropy production shows up as heat.

The old dream was that the energetic cost of computation could be captured by a tiny reversible ideal. The more modern lesson is harsher and more interesting: the real cost lies in the fact that actual computers are designed to be fast, modular, programmable, reusable, and reliable. In other words, the cost comes from the very features that make them computers.

This perspective may eventually become practically important. As hardware improves and margins shrink, the question will no longer be only how fast an algorithm is, or how much memory it needs, but also how much dissipation is fundamentally baked into the way it computes.

And at that point, thermodynamics may stop being a metaphor for information processing and become part of algorithm design itself.

If you want the more technical version of this story, see also the recent papers on stochastic thermodynamics and the energetic cost of computation, including our work on entropy-production bounds for systems running computer programs.
