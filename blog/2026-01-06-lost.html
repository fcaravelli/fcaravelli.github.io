The sacred art of being lost – Francesco Caravelli

The sacred art of being lost - Francesco Caravelli

In the next few weeks I’ll share more technical posts, but today I wanted to write something more personal.

In February 2023, I made the decision to leave my position as a Staff Scientist at Los Alamos National Laboratory. I won’t go into the reasons (there were several, including personal ones), but that day I literally packed my office into boxes. It took a few weeks to bring everything home.

For the next three months I looked for a new job—basically, something interesting enough to pull me forward. I applied to a few universities, national labs, and research institutes. I had some interviews, but nothing really clicked. I’m not entirely sure why, but I don’t think I’m “the professor type.” I’ve always felt more at home in private research institutes.

During my PhD I was at the University of Waterloo, but spent much of my time at Perimeter. I later visited the Santa Fe Institute, and consulted for them while I was a postdoc at OCIAM in Oxford. Then I moved to the private sector as a researcher, while being based at the London Institute for Mathematical Sciences. And finally I ended up at Los Alamos. Looking back, it’s pretty clear: I’ve consistently gravitated toward places with a strong institute culture.

Leaving LANL was bittersweet. I really liked Los Alamos, and overall I liked my job. It was a permanent position, and I had been there for more than seven years.

What I loved most was the problem-solving. Research culture at LANL is strong: people are constantly talking science at whiteboards and blackboards, it’s highly collaborative (at least in T-Division, where I was), and—most of the time—the science is what matters. Resources are excellent, too. And to be clear: leaving wasn’t about failing as a scientist. If anything, it was the opposite.

One thing that always sticks with me during transitions is the need for an interesting problem to solve. I once read that Feynman had a similar itch; in one of his books he mentions spending time trying to understand the dynamics of a dish spinning in the air. National labs are full of fascinating problems, and LANL is no exception. But if you haven’t been sleeping under a rock over the last year or so, you can probably guess what was coming. Let’s just say I wasn’t excited about the direction things were heading.

The reasons were multiple, but the main one was that I felt stuck—not stuck in terms of growth or ability, but stuck in terms of exploration. I felt fixed on a direction, at a time when I needed to be able to wander.

There’s a line (from The Night Flier, based on a Stephen King story) that I’ve always liked: “Don’t believe what you publish, and don’t publish what you believe.” That’s a version of scientific discipline: stay honest, stay provisional. But the track I was on started to feel like following a compass needle while the compass itself was being dragged around—always pointing somewhere, but no longer toward anything I trusted as “true north.”

So I left. And what I realized, in the months after, was that I needed to be lost again.

The thing is, I also don’t love jumping on bandwagons. Science can feel like a cycle of hype waves—at least at a superficial level. At a deeper level, of course, there are movements driven by real curiosity and substance. But those aren’t always the ones getting the attention they deserve.

On a side note, lately I’ve been following the search for Céline Cremer in Tasmania (see here). There are few feelings as helpless as being lost in the wild. If it’s happened to you, you know what I mean.

I once got lost for a few hours while trail running alone in Santa Fe. It wasn’t dramatic—I made a dumb decision and tried to find my way back without a map or GPS, and ended up running in circles until I hit a road. I had to call my wife to come pick me up. I was lucky: it was still daylight, and I had water and some food. A friend of mine got lost nearby for an entire night, and he told me it was one of the scariest experiences of his life.

Being lost in nature isn’t so different from being lost in your career (or your life). When you’re lost outside, you focus on survival: water, shelter, food. When you’re lost professionally, you focus on the equivalents: opportunities, direction, challenges—and if you have a family to provide for, the stakes are even higher.

I’ve felt this before. Midway through my PhD, I was lost too. I wasn’t sure what to do next, and I wasn’t sure I wanted to stay in academia. I remember telling my advisor that I didn’t think quantum gravity was for me. She told me to slow down, take a break, and not rush the decision. I was lucky to be in Berlin at the time—that period became a turning point. I realized I wanted to explore new directions, and work on problems that were more interdisciplinary.

If you’re lost in your research, a good move is often lateral but adjacent: switch problems, learn new methods, meet new collaborators—then come back. If the problem really matters to you, you’ll be drawn back to it. Career-wise, I think the same applies. If you feel stuck, move. Explore. Get out of the mud.

Paul Graham (cofounder of Y Combinator) once said that “the most important thing you can do as a startup founder is to be working on something you care about.” Maybe we should think of the scientific method the same way: as something that needs room to iterate, pivot, and grow organically. Are small institutes—where that kind of organic growth is more natural—the way forward? I don’t know.

But I do know this: being lost isn’t always a failure. Sometimes it’s the beginning of something better.
