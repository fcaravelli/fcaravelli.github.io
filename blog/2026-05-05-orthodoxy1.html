Non-orthodox science careers

On tribal fields, scapegoats, Kuhnian stagnation, and the uneasy business of discovering that a scientific career and a scientific vocation are not always the same thing.

1. An early education in non-naivety

This post will deviate a little from the technical ones I posted over the last few weeks. The goal is to move back and forth between some more soft and more technical subjects, and to share some of the things I have learned about scientific careers, scientific fields, and the social dynamics of science. Who knows me well has also heard me talking about these things quite often, possibly in a non-crystallized form. I think I have however reached a certain clarity on these topics, and I thought it would be useful to share that clarity in a more organized way. I will also share some of the books that have been most helpful to me in understanding these things, and I will try to connect those books to my own experience, and some literature (outside the scientific one) that will help me make the points I am trying to bring home a bit stronger. When I mentioned to some of my profs that I was going to Perimeter, one of them said (I'm paraphrasing "gli speciali della fisica") "oh, they are the exotic ones in Physics". He was neither right nor wrong.

During the speech for his Oscar, Roberto Begining thanked his parents for giving him the greatest gift: poverty. What he meant was that adversity helps forming characters. Today, sociologists call this "trauma intelligence". In this regards, one of the more useful accidents of my early career was that I never had enough stability to become fully naive. During my PhD, my supervisor was already on his way out of academia, and the field itself was so toxic that even as a student I could tell that what was being defended in many arguments was not only truth. People were defending territory, genealogy, prestige, and occasionally something as small and ridiculous as a parameter \(\delta\), though by then it was no longer really about \(\delta\). I did not have the language for any of this at the time. I only had the strong impression that intelligent adults were behaving in a way that was at once highly technical and embarrassingly ancient. I had essentially left my environment, at that time, because

I did my PhD abroad (I'm Italian), in a place now known for its research environment, top-notch institute very well known in Physics. When I arrived, the institue was not even 10 years old I think. I had decided to leave Italy for a variety of reasons, some less important and some more important, but the main one was that I wanted to be in a place where I could do good science and learn from good scientists. Some form of immaturity and naivety is probably necessary to make that decision, but I think it was a good decision. Italy had started to go downhill in the early 2010s, both economically and politically. I heard recently that CNR (Centro Nazionale delle Ricerche) had not hired a single permanent researcher since 2017. It was a good decision, a posteriori, but also means that I needed to spend close to two decades away from my home country, family and friends. But the field I was in was also very small, and it had a very toxic atmosphere.

Around the same time, my supervisor began the process of leaving science as a profession and, shortly afterwards, opened a company. I was caught in the middle of that transition, although in truth I had already been fairly alone. He was rarely there anyway, and most of my scientific life during those years was spent working with postdocs and learning, more or less by necessity, how to move through a subject without much supervision. I finished the PhD alright, but I finished it with an unusually early suspicion that scientific careers and scientific vocations were not quite the same thing.

That topic in physics was absolutely toxic. I do not mean merely competitive, though it certainly was that. I mean that it had the atmosphere of a small religious schism that had somehow acquired equations. It often felt like those scenes where priests gather in one place to worship the same god, only because they belong to different churches the whole thing starts drifting toward the nearest available version of a bar fight, except that nobody involved can lift five kilograms off the ground. It would have been funny if it had not also been career-forming for so many people.

2. The war over \(\delta\)

There was, in that world, a small group of perhaps twenty or thirty people working on a genuinely interesting subfield. And then, somehow, the thing began to deform. A dispute emerged over the definition of a parameter, \(\delta\). This sounds too small to matter, which is part of why it mattered so much. The person who had invented that subfield found himself opposed to one of the three people who had invented the broader field, and before long he was being marginalized. I remember watching this happen with a mixture of fascination and disgust.

At the time I called it tribal, partly because I could not think of another word, and partly because it seemed obvious that whatever was going on had very little to do with the noble self-image science likes to project. That self-image, of course, is not entirely false. Science does contain real inquiry, real discipline, real moments of collective intelligence. But any field that is small enough, prestigious enough, and insecure enough will also contain the older ingredients: rivalry, coalition, ritual humiliation, lineage, memory, and strategic forgetting.

3. Girard and the scapegoat

Much later I found René Girard, and Girard gave me a language for what I had seen long before I could explain it. What struck me most in The Scapegoat was not only the historical or anthropological thesis, though that is fascinating enough. It was the uneasy recognition of a pattern. Girard’s claim, roughly speaking, is that human groups under tension often restore order by converging against a scapegoat. Conflict that is in fact diffuse gets concentrated onto one person or one group. The victim comes to carry the disorder, and by excluding or condemning that figure the community rediscovers a temporary solidarity.

When I read that, I could not stop thinking about that poor professor who had been turned into a problem over a \(\delta\). In science one does not usually see this in sacrificial language. One sees it instead in the form of dismissive jokes, strategic silence, exclusion from networks, subtle rewritings of origin stories, and the repeated suggestion that one person has somehow become the obstacle to the field’s health. But once one has seen enough of it, it becomes very hard to maintain the childish belief that science is automatically protected from ancient social mechanisms by virtue of being scientific.

Scientists are still people. Put them in a small prestige economy, tie their identities to fragile symbolic distinctions, and give them a reason to think the fate of the world depends on their side of a technical argument, and the older machinery wakes up quickly enough.

4. Kuhn and the stagnation of fields

Kuhn resonated with me for related reasons, though on a different axis. What stayed with me from The Structure of Scientific Revolutions was not just the familiar slogan about paradigms, but the more uncomfortable thought that stagnation is often the normal social result of success. A field consolidates. It develops its language, methods, tastes, and standards of seriousness. Young people are trained into these habits. Careers get built around them. At that point, genuinely new moves become difficult not only because they are intellectually risky, but because they threaten a settled world.

The problem is not merely that people resist being wrong. It is that whole systems of recognition depend on not having to become different too quickly. A field can be full of intelligent people and still behave in ways that are both socially primitive and conceptually stagnant. In that sense, Girard helps explain the violence of small intellectual tribes, and Kuhn helps explain their inertia.

Since then I have always looked at scientific careers with a certain suspicion. Not science itself. That is an important distinction. I never stopped caring about science. If anything, my attachment to science became clearer as my confidence in scientific careers became weaker.

5. Leaving academia, badly

I have been in and out of academia for years, and looking back I think that instability was present from the beginning. Even when I was inside, I was never fully at ease with the story academia tells about itself. But being suspicious of a system does not spare you from wanting its approval. If anything, it produces a more exhausting split: you see the machinery clearly enough to distrust it, yet not clearly enough to stop being wounded by it.

I am fairly sure that, to some of my old colleagues in that earlier discipline, I count as a failure. I did not become a professor. I did not remain on the canonical path long enough to acquire the right titles in the right order. In many academic environments, that is enough to settle the judgment. The verdict becomes almost administrative: he did not make it.

What they do not know, or perhaps would not know how to value, is that I did eventually have some modest success in a different discipline, one slightly more applied, and that in doing so I remained close to what I think is my actual identity as a scientist. It simply took longer. The timing was wrong by the standards of one tribe, but perhaps right by the standards of the work itself.

After a couple of postdocs in complex systems, at Oxford and UCL, I went to work as a private researcher for the first time in 2014. The decision was entirely mine, and still it felt brutal. The feeling of having failed was vivid. Even in 2016 I was still publishing in that old subject as an external researcher, but in retrospect a nontrivial part of that was not pure intellectual necessity. I was doing it, at least partly, to demonstrate that I had not become illegible to the old order.

This is one of the more difficult things to explain to people who have not had to make this transition. Rationally, one can make a very good case for leaving academia. There is the bureaucracy, the grant writing, the managerial bloat, and the bizarre amount of life consumed by performances of seriousness that are only indirectly related to actual scientific work. In my own case, the split had become almost caricatural: something like 50% writing grants, 30% being a kind of manager, and maybe 20% doing active science. That is not my natural habitat.

And yet leaving is still brutal. The brutality is not only financial or logistical. It is psychological. Academic life may be irrational in many ways, but it still offers a very strong identity structure. It tells you where you stand, or at least where you are supposed to stand. It tells you what counts, what to aspire to, and how to interpret your own biography. Once one leaves, one does not merely lose a job path. One loses a scoreboard.

6. Status, time, burnout, and reward

This is why I have never found the usual language of “escaping the rat race” entirely satisfactory, though it captures something real. There are indeed people who quietly stop organizing their lives around status, title, and upward visibility. They choose differently. They choose time, or autonomy, or room to think. But what that language often misses is that leaving a prestige structure does not merely remove stress. It also removes the game itself. One no longer has the same metrics, the same rhythm of comparison, the same reliable if vulgar answers to the question of whether one is winning or losing.

And that question matters more than people like to admit. There is now a reasonable body of evidence suggesting that people who place greater value on time than money tend, over important life transitions, to choose more intrinsically rewarding paths and report greater well-being afterwards. The point is not sainthood. It is valuation. Some people continue to find the old rewards compelling; others begin to find them expensive.

Something similar appears in the literature on future-self continuity. One of the more interesting ideas there is that people vary in how vividly they experience their future selves as continuous with their present selves, and that this affects how willing they are to sacrifice immediate rewards for long-term well-being. In less academic language, some people are better at feeling that the exhausted person five or ten years from now is still them. That matters when one is choosing between short-term prestige and a life that remains livable.

Burnout belongs in this story too, unfortunately. The burnout literature is now less metaphorical than it once was: occupational burnout is recognized as a syndrome resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed, and recent reviews connect it with measurable changes in brain systems involved in stress and regulation. One does not need a brain scan to know when one is not living properly, but it is sobering to realize that chronic professional strain is not merely a mood problem. It leaves marks.

In that sense, the transition out of academia is not just a career choice. It is also, very often, a renegotiation of reward. Prestige starts to look thin compared to time, compared to freedom from idiotic status rituals, compared to the ability to work on problems one actually cares about without converting every hour into a performance for a tribunal.

7. Keeping the work

What eventually clarified things for me was not a grand revelation but a gradual simplification. I still cared about the old subject. I still do, and I still work on it actively. But I also had other interests that had always been there: this narrow but fertile zone between physics and computation, physics and brain science, and more recently physics and quantum computing. Quantum computing is newer for me, perhaps the last five years, but importantly it was a conscious move. It was not an inheritance from a local hierarchy. It was something I chose because it felt alive.

So much of an academic life proceeds by inertia. A person stays with a topic because that is where the jobs are, because that is what the nearest senior people value, because there is a local ecosystem built around it, because by the time one begins to doubt it there is already too much biography attached. One continues because one has continued. It takes a certain amount of dislocation to break that spell and ask the much simpler question: what do I actually want to spend my mind on?

In that respect, self-determination theory has always felt closer to the truth than the cruder language of success and failure. Ryan and Deci’s framework is built around autonomy, competence, and relatedness as basic psychological needs, and it is difficult not to recognize in that picture something essential about intellectual life. Work becomes thinner when it is governed mainly by external control, however prestigious the control may be. It becomes more durable when it remains connected to volition and intrinsic interest.

I think this is why the move out of academia can feel so confusing even when it is right. One loses many things at once, some of them admittedly real. Academia did have, or once had, certain perks: flexibility, a certain permission structure around thought, a sense that curiosity itself was not a private eccentricity but part of one’s official business. Some of this has eroded badly. Still, the symbolic residue remains strong enough that leaving can feel like stepping away from seriousness itself.

And yet I do much more science now than I did before. That fact took me an embarrassingly long time to trust. For a while I still needed to prove continuity with the old standards. I still needed papers in the old area, visible signs that I had not become a failed academic wandering around in civilian clothes. But eventually the thing became much simpler. I was doing more of the work I actually cared about, and less of the work that merely sustained the appearance of the right kind of career.

None of this has made me immune to the feeling of failure. These things linger. The old academic superego lingers with them. It still knows how to whisper that not having ended up in the proper chair at the proper age must mean something essential was missing. It takes time to realize that this voice is not truth. It is just the afterlife of a ranking system.

What I do have now, much more than before, is clarity. I have a clearer sense of the kind of work I want to do, of the boundaries within which I can do it well, of the sort of collaborations that help rather than deform me, and of the work-life balance without which I become less serious rather than more.

If someone in science were quietly considering a similar move, the practical part is simple enough: try not to let the loss of one institutional structure become the loss of intellectual momentum itself. Keep working on something genuinely alive. Stay near people who are still curious in a way that has not completely collapsed into rank. Do not assume that leaving one tribe automatically frees you from its categories. Very often you carry the walls with you for years.

Perhaps that is the main thing I understand differently now. I did not really want to leave science. What I wanted, though I could not have put it this way at the beginning, was to separate science from a particular kind of tribal life. Girard helped me see the tribe. Kuhn helped me see the stagnation. Experience then did the rest. It showed me that careers built around knowledge are not always organized around truth, and that one can remain faithful to inquiry while becoming increasingly unfaithful to the institutions that claim to manage it.

I still think about that younger version of myself watching grown scientists turn a parameter into a battlefield and sensing, before he had any decent vocabulary for it, that something old and rather embarrassing had entered the room. He was right to be suspicious. He was also right not to confuse the tribe with the work.
