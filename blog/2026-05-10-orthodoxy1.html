Non-orthodox science careers

May 2026

Tribes, scapegoats, Kuhnian stagnation, and the uneasy business of discovering that a scientific career and a scientific vocation are not always the same thing.

1. An early education in non-naivety

This post will deviate a little from the technical ones I posted over the last few weeks. It was meant to be posted a few weeks ago but I've been working on and off on this post given some brooding happening in the background. The goal for this blog is to move back and forth between some more soft and more technical subjects, and to share some of the things I have learned about scientific careers, scientific fields, and the social dynamics of science. Let me say right away that I am not interested in dismissing academia at all, but I want only to make some observations about the fact that "orthodox"scientific careers are not always the same thing as scientific vocations. Who knows me well has also heard me talking about these things quite often, possibly in a non-crystallized form. I think I have however reached a certain internal clarity on these topics, and I thought it would be useful to share that clarity in a more organized way. I will also share some of the books that have been most helpful to me in understanding these things, and I will try to connect those books to my own experience, and some literature outside the scientific one that will help me make the points I am trying to bring home a bit stronger. When I mentioned to some of my Master's professors that I was going to Perimeter, one of them said — I'm paraphrasing — "gli speciali della fisica": "oh, they are the exotic ones in Physics". He was neither right nor wrong.

During the speech for his Oscar, Roberto Benigni thanked his parents for giving him the greatest gift: poverty. What he meant was that adversity helps forming characters. Today, sociologists call this "trauma intelligence". In this regard, one of the more useful accidents of my early career was that I never had enough stability to become fully naive. During my PhD, my supervisor was, unbeknownst to everybody, about to leave academia. My PhD was in a very niche area, probably around 100 to 200 people were working on it at its peak time. Much less now as the field declined over time, a general trend, I would say, for many fields in Physics, but in particular High Energy. The field itself was so toxic that even as a student I could tell that what was being defended in many arguments was not only truth. People were defending territory, academic genealogy, prestige, and occasionally something as small and ridiculous as a parameter \(\delta\), though by then it was no longer really about \(\delta\). I did not have the language for any of this at the time. I only had the strong impression that intelligent adults were behaving in a way that was at once highly technical and embarrassingly ancient.

I did my PhD abroad — I'm Italian — in a place now known for its research environment, a top-notch institute very well known in Physics. When I arrived, the institute was not even 10 years old, I think. I had decided to have an experience abroad for a variety of reasons, some less important and some more important, but the main one was that I wanted to be in a place where I could do good science and learn from good scientists.

Some form of immaturity and naivety is probably necessary to make that decision, but I think it was a good decision. Leaving was a good decision, a posteriori, but it also meant that I needed to spend close to two decades away from my home country, family, and friends.

As mentioned, around the same time, my supervisor began the process of leaving science as a profession and, shortly afterwards, opened a company. I was caught in the middle of that transition, although in truth I had already been fairly alone. My supervisor, who was and is a good person I really enjoy talking to to this day, was rarely there anyway, and most of my scientific life during those years was spent working with the great postdocs who were there. Learning, more or less by necessity, how to move through a subject without much supervision. I finished the PhD alright, but I finished it with an unusually early suspicion that scientific careers and scientific vocations were not quite the same thing.

Unfortunately, if you have seen a comedy movie about a high school full of bullies, that is how I see that period now. Using a terminology that I will make more precise later, I saw a lot of tribalism, a lot of people being treated as if they were the problem, and a lot of people being treated as if they were the solution. I had no language for any of this, but I had a strong sense that something was very wrong, and that it had very little to do with the pursuit of some form of idealized truth, at least the way I had imagined it before. I also had a strong sense that the people who were doing the most interesting work in that field were not the ones who were doing the most prestigious work, and that the people who were doing the most prestigious work were not necessarily doing the most interesting work.

2. The wars over \(\delta\)s

Human trajectories very often can collide. However, when they happen in Science the outcome can be devastating for the individuals involved. If you are working at a company and lose your job, you can find another one. If you are working in a field of science and lose your position, you can find, maybe, another one. In Physics it is hard, you don't find a job nearby unless you are in a very big city, and sometimes not even that. Communities are small, spread out across the world, yet word of mouth goes around, and you can find yourself in a situation where you are not welcome anywhere. This is one of the most brutal things about the social aspects of science, and it is one of the things that I have seen happen more than once. These risks mean that you will be forced to pick a conservative approach to your career, and that you will be very careful about not burning bridges. This also means that, within your "tribe", you will have to respect the rules of the tribe, and that you will have to be careful about not being seen as a problem. This might also mean defining your axioms in a way that is not too different from the ones of the tribe. And by axiom I truly mean that \(\delta\) must be properly defined. This happened in the field I was working on during my PhD - I call it "the war over \(\delta\)".

I have seen this happen more than once in various forms, and it is always a little bit shocking and sad. It is shocking because it is so primitive, and because it is so brutal. It is primitive because it has nothing to do with the ideals of science, and everything to do with the oldest social mechanisms we have. It is brutal because it can end careers, and because it can make people feel like they are the problem when they are not. It is sad becauase of the outcome. It is so rooted in social mechanisms that it is very hard to fight against, and it is very hard to avoid. I mean that it seems that these occurrences of tribal wars resemble the dynamics of a small religious schism that had somehow acquired equations. It often felt like those scenes where priests gather in one place to worship the same god — some scientist, some equation, or theory — only because they belong to different churches the whole thing starts drifting toward the nearest available version of a bar fight, except that nobody involved can lift five kilograms off the ground. It would have been funny if it had not also been career-forming for so many people.

An example I know of personally and that is referenced above happened in the early 2010s. I was a PhD student, and I was working in a very small field, with only a few people in the world working on it. There was, in that world, a small group of perhaps twenty or thirty people working on a genuinely interesting subfield. And then, somehow, the thing began to deform. A dispute emerged over the definition of a parameter, \(\delta\) — the meaning does not matter here. This sounds too small to matter, which is part of why it mattered so much. The person who had invented that subfield found himself opposed to one of the three people who had invented the broader field, and before long he was being marginalized. I remember watching this happen with a mixture of fascination and disgust. People were shouting at each other at conferences. I remember this hot day in Madrid in 2010, where the person who had invented the subfield was giving a talk, and people were shouting at him from the audience. This was not over what the field was meant to achieve. It was over a small definition of a parameter that, let me be clear, nobody, nor to this day, has been able to explain why it was so important or measurable. What was wrong, however, was that he was against the dogma originating from one of the inventors of that field. This was creating instability. The person who was being shouted at was not a bad scientist, and he was not doing bad work. He was just on the wrong side of a tribal dispute, and that was enough to make him the problem. And to me, the merit of the scientific discussion became less relevant than the fact that a person was being turned into a problem, and that the problem was being turned into a kind of ritual.

At the time I called it tribal, partly because I could not think of another word, and partly because it seemed obvious that whatever was going on had very little to do with the noble self-image science likes to project. That self-image, of course, is not entirely false. Science does contain real inquiry, real discipline, real moments of collective intelligence. But any field that is small enough, prestigious enough, and insecure enough will also contain the older ingredients: rivalry, coalition, ritual humiliation, lineage, memory, and strategic forgetting.

3. Girard and the scapegoat

Much later I found René Girard, and Girard gave me a language for what I had seen long before I could explain it. What struck me most in The Scapegoat was not only the historical or anthropological thesis, though that is fascinating enough. It was the uneasy recognition of a pattern. Girard’s claim, roughly speaking, is that human groups under tension often restore order by converging against a scapegoat. Conflict that is in fact diffuse gets concentrated onto one person or one group. The victim comes to carry the disorder, and by excluding or condemning that figure the community rediscovers a temporary solidarity.

When I read that, I could not stop thinking about that poor professor who had been turned into a problem over a \(\delta\). In science one does not usually see this in sacrificial language. One sees it instead in the form of dismissive jokes, strategic silence, exclusion from networks, subtle rewritings of origin stories, and the repeated suggestion that one person has somehow become the obstacle to the field’s health. But once one has seen enough of it, it becomes very hard to maintain the childish belief that science is automatically protected from ancient social mechanisms by virtue of being scientific.

Scientists are still people. Put them in a small prestige economy, tie their identities to fragile symbolic distinctions, and give them a reason to think the fate of the world depends on their side of a technical argument, and the older machinery wakes up quickly enough. Girard, in this sense, helps explain the violence of small intellectual tribes, and the way in which they can be a sort of weird interplay between tribalism, religion, scapegoating, incentives based on prestige (or the desire of prestige of others).

4. Kuhn and the stagnation of fields

The second part of this story is not about scapegoats but about stagnation. Girard helped me see the tribe. Kuhn helped me see the stagnation aspects of it, and the fact that it was exactly because of the stagnation that people were focusing on these smaller details. In this sense, Thomas S. Kuhn resonated with me for related reasons, though on a different axis. What stayed with me from The Structure of Scientific Revolutions was not just the familiar slogan about paradigms, but the more uncomfortable thought that stagnation is often the normal social result of success. A field consolidates. It develops its language, methods, tastes, and standards of seriousness. Young people are trained into these habits. Careers get built around them. At that point, genuinely new moves become difficult not only because they are intellectually risky, but because they threaten a settled world. However, stagnation can occur also in "dying" fields, that lead to nowhere. No possible measurements or confirmations. The way in which a field dies is that fewer and fewer people work on it, essentially because fewer and fewer positions are opened, from junior to senior. Then, it becomes a form of "There will be only one" — to use a phrase from Highlander — where the only thing that matters is to be on the right side of the dogma, till there will be only one position left. People will move on. It starts with the field dividing in a myriad of subfields for people to differentiate. Then, it ends with the field being reduced to a single position, and then it dies. You can see how this process leads, for stagnant subfields, to more and more of these branches dying off. I am not particularly interested in the sociology of science, but I am interested in the sociology of scientific fields, and in the way in which they evolve when they are about to die off. The many last breaths of a dying subfield lead to the toxicity. Of course, a field can be revamped, or pumped, if new results convince funding agencies to provide funding for it. Or if a technological breakthrough brings funding into that field. But if that does not happen, the field will die, and it will die in a very toxic way, with a lot of people fighting over the last positions, and a lot of people being turned into scapegoats eventually.

At a broader level, the problem is not merely that people resist being wrong. It is that whole systems of recognition depend on not having to become different too quickly. A field can be full of intelligent people and still behave in ways that are both socially primitive and conceptually stagnant. In that sense, Girard helps explain the violence of small intellectual tribes, and Kuhn helps explain their inertia.

Since then I have always looked at "orthodox" scientific careers with a certain suspicion, admitting however that this is the best system we have in place for scientific production. Not science itself. That is an important distinction. I never stopped caring about science. If the incentive system of universities was aligned with the genuine pursuit of truth, I would have never considered its alternatives. If anything, my attachment to science became clearer as my confidence in "normal" scientific careers became weaker.

5. Unorthodoxy

I have been in and out of academia for years, and looking back I think that instability was present from the beginning. Even when I was inside, I was never fully at ease with the story academia tells about itself. Even before I started my PhD, my advisor then told me: are you sure that you want to do Physics and not Biology? At that time, I took it weirdly as a form of offense, but now I understand that it was a warning. My supervisor was trying to tell me that I should be careful about the field I was going to work on, and that I should be careful about the people I was going to work with. In retrospect, that was very good advice, and I also now understand what was the reason for that advice. My supervisor had seen the toxicity of the field I was going to work on, and wanted to warn me about it. But being suspicious of a system does not spare you from wanting its approval. If anything, it produces a more exhausting split: you see the machinery clearly enough to distrust it, yet not clearly enough to stop being wounded by it.

I am fairly sure that, to some of my old colleagues in that earlier discipline, I count as a failure. I did not become a professor. I did not remain on the canonical path long enough to acquire the right titles in the right order. In many academic environments, that is enough to settle the judgment. The verdict becomes almost administrative: he did not make it.

What they do not know, or perhaps would not know how to value, is that I did eventually have some modest success in a different discipline, one slightly more applied, and that in doing so I remained close to what I think is my actual identity as a scientist. And in fact, multiple disciplines. It simply took longer. The timing was wrong by the standards of one tribe, but perhaps right by the standards of the work itself. Actually, my second advisor told me explicitly that I should not worry about the timing, and that I should just do good work. That not specializing immediately would have punished me in the short term, but it would have been the right thing to do in the long term. I think that was very good advice, and I am glad I followed it, even if it meant that I had to endure a lot of the feelings of failure that I had during those years. This does not mean that I did not have to specialize at all. My interest in neuromorphic computing was, in fact, engineered by me, and it was a conscious choice.

After a couple of postdocs in complex systems, at Oxford and UCL, I went to work as a private researcher for the first time in 2014. The decision was entirely mine, and still it felt brutal. The feeling of having failed was vivid. Even in 2016 I was still publishing in that old subject as an external researcher, but in retrospect a nontrivial part of that was not pure intellectual necessity. I was doing it, at least partly, to demonstrate that I had not become illegible to the old order.

This is one of the more difficult things to explain to people who have not had to make this transition. Rationally, one can make a very good case for leaving academia. There is the bureaucracy, the grant writing, the managerial bloat, and the bizarre amount of life consumed by performances of seriousness that are only indirectly related to actual scientific work. In my own case, the split had become almost caricatural: something like 50% writing grants, 30% being a kind of manager, and maybe 20% doing active science. That is not my natural habitat, and the latter became smaller over time.

And yet leaving is still brutal. The brutality is not only financial or logistical. It is psychological. Academic life may be irrational in many ways, but it still offers a very strong identity structure. Think of that not as a single human being moving, but as a whole family packing up. At a certain point it is not feasible. You cannot have a kid living in three different countries with three different languages over six years. That is nonsense and not human. It tells you where you stand, or at least where you are supposed to stand. It tells you what counts, what to aspire to, and how to interpret your own biography. Once one leaves, one does not merely lose a job path. One loses a scoreboard.

6. Status, time, burnout, and reward

This is why I have never found the usual language of “escaping the rat race” entirely satisfactory, though it captures something real. There are indeed people who quietly stop organizing their lives around status, title, and upward visibility. They choose differently. They choose time, or autonomy, or room to think. But what that language often misses is that leaving a prestige structure does not merely remove stress. It also removes the game itself. One no longer has the same metrics, the same rhythm of comparison, the same reliable if vulgar answers to the question of whether one is winning or losing.

And that question matters more than people like to admit. There is now a reasonable body of evidence suggesting that people who place greater value on time than money tend, over important life transitions, to choose more intrinsically rewarding paths and report greater well-being afterwards. The point is not sainthood. It is valuation. Some people continue to find the old rewards compelling; others begin to find them expensive.

Something similar appears in the literature on future-self continuity, and I have a few papers referenced below. One of the more interesting ideas there is that people vary in how vividly they experience their future selves as continuous with their present selves, and that this affects how willing they are to sacrifice immediate rewards for long-term well-being. In less academic language, some people are better at feeling that the exhausted person five or ten years from now is still them. That matters when one is choosing between short-term prestige and a life that remains livable.

Burnout belongs in this story too, unfortunately. The burnout literature is now less metaphorical than it once was: occupational burnout is recognized as a syndrome resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed, and recent reviews connect it with measurable changes in brain systems involved in stress and regulation. One does not need a brain scan to know when one is not living properly, but it is sobering to realize that chronic professional strain is not merely a mood problem. It leaves marks. And what I described at the beginning of this post should be recognizable as a form of chronic strain leading to this burnout. I have known people who committed suicide because of this toxicity. Also close friends, unfortunately.

In that sense, the transition out of academia is not just a career choice. It is also, very often, a renegotiation of reward. Prestige starts to look thin compared to time, compared to freedom from idiotic status rituals, compared to the ability to work on problems one actually cares about without converting every hour into a performance for a tribunal. This said, I keep telling people that a scientist moving out of academia should focus on taking jobs that are intellectually stimulating. Seeing what are the open problems, why they have not been tackled, and rewiring their brains to tackle these new problems rather than the old ones. Instead of mimetic competition, focus on intrinsic motivation and personal fulfillment. Girard, in fact, introduced the notion of mimetic desire, which is the idea that we often desire things not because of their intrinsic value but because other people desire them. This is a powerful force in human behavior, and it can lead to a lot of unnecessary competition and stress. In science, this can manifest as a desire for prestige, recognition, and status, rather than a genuine interest in the work itself. By focusing on intrinsic motivation and personal fulfillment, one can avoid falling into the trap of mimetic desire and find more satisfaction in their work. Desire your own work, not the work or achievements of others.

7. Keeping the work

What eventually clarified things for me was not a grand revelation but a gradual simplification. I still cared about the old subject. Not my PhD one; I left it for good, and I consider that now as a clear career mistake. I still do, however, care about the multiple fields I am interested in and that I worked on subsequently, and I still work on them actively in my spare time. But I also had other interests that had always been there: this narrow but fertile zone between physics and computation, physics and brain science, and more recently physics and quantum computing. Quantum computing is newer for me, perhaps the last five years, but importantly it was a conscious move. It was not an inheritance from a local hierarchy. It was something I chose because it felt alive.

So much of an academic life proceeds by inertia. A person stays with a topic because that is where the jobs are, because that is what the nearest senior people value, because there is a local ecosystem built around it, because by the time one begins to doubt it there is already too much biography attached. One continues because one has continued. It takes a certain amount of dislocation to break that spell and ask the much simpler question: what do I actually want to spend my mind on?

In that respect, self-determination theory has always felt closer to the truth than the cruder language of success and failure. Ryan and Deci’s framework is built around autonomy, competence, and relatedness as basic psychological needs, and it is difficult not to recognize in that picture something essential about intellectual life. Work becomes thinner when it is governed mainly by external control, however prestigious the control may be. It becomes more durable when it remains connected to volition and intrinsic interest.

I think this is why the move out of academia can feel so confusing even when it is right. One loses many things at once, some of them admittedly real. It also means losing a reference system, a community, a sense of identity, and a certain kind of momentum. The loss of a star telling the direction of motion. Yet, one should have clear what is idealized and what is real. Academia did have, or once had, certain perks: flexibility, a certain permission structure around thought, a sense that curiosity itself was not a private eccentricity but part of one’s official business. Some of this has eroded badly. Still, the symbolic residue remains strong enough that leaving can feel like stepping away from seriousness itself.

None of this has made me immune to the feeling of failure. These things linger. The old academic superego lingers with them. It still knows how to whisper that not having ended up in the proper chair at the proper age must mean something essential was missing. It takes time to realize that this voice is not truth. It is just the afterlife of a ranking system. However, the more one listens to it, the more one realizes that it is not only wrong but also irrelevant. The old system is not just wrong. It is also dead.

What I do have now, much more than before, is clarity. I have a clearer sense of the kind of work I want to do, of the boundaries within which I can do it well, of the sort of collaborations that help rather than deform me, and of the work-life balance without which I become less serious rather than more.

If someone in science were quietly considering a similar move, the practical part is simple enough: try not to let the loss of one institutional structure become the loss of intellectual momentum itself. Keep working on something genuinely alive. Stay near people who are still curious in a way that has not completely collapsed into rank. Do not assume that leaving one tribe automatically frees you from its categories. Very often you carry the walls with you for years.

Perhaps that is the main thing I understand differently now. I did not really want to leave science. What I wanted, though I could not have put it this way at the beginning, was to separate science from a particular kind of tribal life. Girard helped me see the tribe. Kuhn helped me see the stagnation. Experience then did the rest. It showed me that careers built around knowledge are not always organized around truth, and that one can remain faithful to inquiry while becoming increasingly unfaithful to the institutions that claim to manage it.

I still think about that younger version of myself watching grown scientists turn a parameter into a battlefield and sensing, before he had any decent vocabulary for it, that something old and rather embarrassing had entered the room. He was right to be suspicious. He was also right not to confuse the tribe with the actual work.

In many ways, I think that National Labs, private research labs and/or small institutes that are not universities, are potential places to tackle scientific problems. My suggestion to people who are considering leaving academia is to look for places like that, where the work is still alive, and where the reward structure is not entirely based on prestige and status.
