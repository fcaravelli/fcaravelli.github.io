Let me remain on this theme. I recently posted a paper on arXiv: How to Train Your Resistive Network: Generalized Equilibrium Propagation and Analytical Learning. The paper is in collaboration with my coauthors at LANL, Jonathan Lin (who's now doing a PhD at USC) and who started this while at Los Alamos, and Aman Desai and Frank Barrows who are at LANL. The paper explores how to train physical resistive networks optimally. The short version: if your “model” is a physical resistive network (or a memristive / programmable-impedance mesh), you can compute exact gradients using Kirchhoff + graph theory — without running a second “nudged” phase or having to guess the finite-perturbation bias to estimate the gradient.

The punchline is an operator (a weighted cycle-space projector) that the circuit implements at steady state: $$v = -\Omega_{A/R}\, s,$$ and once you write the response like that, the learning rule becomes a clean analytical expression involving $\Omega_{A/R}$ and its adjoint $\Omega_{A/R}^\top$. (This is the core of the paper.) I have not talked about these projector operators yet in this blog, which is rather suprising because I spent quite a lot of time studying them in the context of my previous life in neuromorphic computing. Why analog learning is hard: locality vs gradients

A lot of modern digital ML training quietly assumes global access: you form a loss at the outputs, backpropagate an error signal through the entire model, and update parameters everywhere. In a physical substrate (circuits, mechanical networks, etc.) you usually don’t have that luxury — you can poke and read locally (voltages/currents, forces/displacements), but you can’t reliably “broadcast” a global gradient signal through the hardware.

That tension is exactly why Equilibrium Propagation became such an influential idea: in the framework introduced by Scellier and Bengio, you run the same dynamical system twice — a free phase and a gently nudged (I will also use clamped interchangingly) phase — and extract learning updates from the difference of local measurements. See the original paper: Scellier & Bengio, “Equilibrium Propagation: Bridging the Gap Between Energy-Based Models and Backpropagation”.

EP is not the only route, though. A closely related family of approaches uses two coupled copies of the system rather than a single system run twice in time. In particular, work by Andrea J. Liu’s group and collaborators introduced coupled learning, where two replicas are weakly coupled and the learning signal is extracted from their steady-state relationship (instead of a temporal free/nudged contrast). See, e.g., Stern, Dillavou, Goodrich, Liu, “Learning a force law from a scalar potential” (PNAS, 2019). Conceptually, the “two-copies” trick trades a second phase in time for a second physical instance running in parallel.

In all these works, you need to have access to each single variable that you plan to modify, e.g. the resistance. The direction in which you modify it has to do with a gradient of a certain figure of merit you need to minimize/maximize, which is usually a function of the output variables. The output variable(s) could be output currents, node voltages, or similar. The setup has the flexibility to differ. In general, you estimate such gradient by looking at a free copy of the system, and a nudged version. This means applying a certain voltage here and there to see how the voltage drops change, or the currents change. In the work by Scellier and Bengio, they show that a good estimator is the difference of the currents squared between the free and nudged copies. In the work by Liu et al they use the voltage difference.

In our paper, we set this up in a linear-response language: the gradient comes from how the steady state shifts under a weak perturbation. This is the bridge to Equilibrium Propagation (EP) and Coupled Learning (CL), and we focused on a resistor network, although this framework is more general than that. Both these methods are two-phase update rules, e.g. you need to have the contrast between a free and a nudged phase, either sequentially or in parallel with copy of the system being nudged. What we show in the paper is (a) sometimes these estimates can go parallel to the true gradient (b) they need continuous measurement. This is not a problem per se, let me be clear about this (c) they need to modify every single parameter to work. A lot of methods use (a) as a resource. Instead, (b) and (c) can be problematic in physical implementations. Let's discuss what we suggest in our manuscript.

“Vanilla Two-Phase” learning in resistor networks (EP-style)

As mentioned, we specialize to a passive resistor network on a graph. You apply an input by setting some edge voltage sources, read outputs on selected edges, then do a second run where you add a small output error as an extra source.

In this setting, the classic two-phase update becomes a difference of squared currents per edge:

$$ \frac{\partial J}{\partial r} = \lim_{\beta\to 0}\frac{1}{2\beta}\Big(i_C^{\odot 2}-i_F^{\odot 2}\Big), $$

where $i_F$ and $i_C$ are the free- and clamped-phase steady-state edge currents, and $\odot$ is elementwise power. (This is the “vanilla” two-phase estimator we compare against.)

Two important caveats show up immediately in this physical setting: (i) for any finite nudge $\beta$, you incur estimation bias; (ii) with noisy targets, the current-squared contrastive estimator becomes statistically biased in addition to the finite-$\beta$ issue. (Both are discussed explicitly in the paper’s analysis of two-phase learning in circuits.)

GEP: a single framework that contains EP and CL

A big conceptual part of the paper is to reframe “two-phase learning” as a perturbation theory statement. You write a nudged energy $$F(\beta,\theta,y)=E_\theta(y)+n(\beta,\theta,y),$$ where the second phase corresponds to applying a small training field of strength $\beta$.

The key distinction becomes the order of the perturbation:

  • EP: the nudge is linear, $n_{EP}=\beta\,C$ (first order in $\beta$).
  • CL: the “nudge” arises from clamping / state-shifting inside the same energy, and the leading change is quadratic (second order in $\beta$).

In the paper, this becomes a generalized identity where a $k$-th order finite difference recovers the gradient of a corresponding objective: $$\lim_{\beta\to 0}\frac{1}{\beta^k}\Big[\partial_\theta F(\beta,\theta,y_\beta)-\partial_\theta F(0,\theta,y_0)\Big]=\partial_\theta \ell(\theta).$$ EP is $k=1$, CL is $k=2$. This is the “unification” that we suggest in the manuscript, e.g. both methods emerge from a unique framework.

The analytical route: Ω-learning (exact gradients via graph theory)

Now for the main result: in a passive linear resistor network, the steady-state map can be written in a closed-form. I worked on this quite a lot for memristive networks, where there is a framework to understand how memristors interact in a network thereof. Using a cycle-space formulation (Kirchhoff constraints + dissipation minimization), the network implements an edge-space linear operator:

$$ v = -\Omega_{A/R}\, s,\qquad \Omega_{A/R} := R A^\top(ARA^\top)^{-1}A. $$

$\Omega_{A/R}$ is an oblique projector ($\Omega^2=\Omega$), and it is the natural “response operator” of the circuit. Once you write the physics this way, you can differentiate it and get the Jacobian:

$$ \frac{\partial v}{\partial r} = (I-\Omega_{A/R})\,\mathrm{diag}(i). $$

For least squares on output edges (selector $P_o$), $$L(v,y)=\tfrac12\|P_ოვ-y\|^2,$$ the chain rule gives an analytical gradient:

$$ \nabla_r L = \mathrm{diag}(i)\,(I-\Omega_{A/R}^\top)\,P_o^\top(P_o v-y). $$

Conceptually: two-phase learning estimates something like a projector action via a finite perturbation. Ω-learning just computes the projector gradient exactly — but still in a way that can be implemented with local measurements, provided you can realize $\Omega^\top$ (via reciprocity / current-mode probing).

What do you actually need to do on hardware?

The paper compares two “measurement protocols” that correspond to (a) two-phase contrastive learning and (b) Ω-based analytical gradients. The difference is the following: both require you to measure free-phase currents $i_F$ and apply a small nudge $\beta$ at the output, but the second phase differs:

  • Two-phase: run the network twice (either series or parallel, free + nudged), measure edge currents twice, update with $i_F^{\odot 2}-i_C^{\odot 2}$.
  • Ω-learning: run once in voltage-mode to get $v$ and store $i_F$, then do one adjoint probe to realize $\Omega^\top$, and update with a local Hadamard product $i_F \odot \Delta$.

This is exactly the “Algorithm 1 vs Algorithm 2” story in the paper: two-phase uses purely voltage-mode biasing, while Ω-learning mixes voltage-mode and a reciprocal/current-mode step to access the adjoint projector action.

What changes in practice

In simulation, both methods can reach similar accuracy on a simple classification task, but the two-phase estimator is visibly more unstable during training.

The bigger separation appears when you add realism:

  • Noisy targets: the analytical gradient stays unbiased under zero-mean noise, while the two-phase current-squared estimator acquires a noise-induced bias for any finite $\beta$.
  • Limited edge access: if you freeze a random fraction of tunable edges, Ω-learning degrades more gracefully.

If you want one clean soundbite: two-phase learning is a perturbative estimator that becomes delicate when nudges are finite or data is noisy; Ω-learning is an exact circuit gradient that avoids the finite-$\beta$ approximation and behaves better in those regimes.

When should you use which?

  • If you can’t implement reciprocity / current-mode probing, two-phase is the simplest “pure voltage-mode” training scheme.
  • If you care about bias (finite nudges) or noisy supervision, Ω-learning is the clean solution: exact gradients and better statistical behavior.
  • If you want a conceptual unifier, GEP tells you what EP/CL are doing as perturbation theory — and makes the comparisons precise.

The broader point (and where I think this gets interesting) is that once you express the physical system as an operator that it implements at equilibrium, learning becomes “just” differentiating that operator — and then figuring out which physical primitives can implement the adjoint needed by the gradient.
