Critical Casimir forces in spin systems

March 30th, 2026

Some months ago I attended a conference in Krakow (I wrote about it in a previous post). One of the talks I attended was about the critical Casimir force, which is a thermodynamic/entropic analog of the more well known Casimir force occurring between two conductive plates. Now, although I had heard about the effect before, I had never really sat down to understand the mechanism in a simple model, and I thought it would be nice to do that here. There is a very nice review by Andrea Gambassi here. I've been meaning to write about this topic for a while, and I thought it would be nice to start with a very simple toy model that captures the main mechanism in a transparent way. Now, as I discuss below, this effect is usually studied in the context of critical phenomena, but the basic idea of a mediated interaction that depends on distance is more general, and can be illustrated even in a simple one-dimensional Ising chain. The caveat, you might say, is that the one dimensional Ising model does not have a finite-temperature critical point, so it is not the full story. But as a cartoon of the mechanism, it already captures the main idea: integrate out the bulk, obtain a distance-dependent effective free energy, differentiate with respect to distance, and read off the induced force. My colleague Diego Dalvit is an expert on the topic and I discussed this with him before writing, so I hope this is a faithful representation of the physics. In the standard quantum mechanical effect, the force arises from the change in zero-point energy of the electromagnetic field due to the presence of the boundaries, and the attractive force scales as an inverse power of the distance between the plates. In the critical Casimir effect, the force arises from the change in free energy of a fluctuating medium (like a fluid near its critical point) due to the presence of boundaries, and it typically decays exponentially with distance, with a decay length given by the correlation length of the medium. In both cases, the key idea is that the presence of boundaries modifies the spectrum of fluctuations in the medium, leading to an effective interaction between the boundaries that depends on their separation. However, near a second order phase transition the critical fluctuations become long-ranged, and the correlation length diverges, leading to a long-range Casimir force that can be observed experimentally at large distances as well. This is the phenomenology that is usually associated with the critical Casimir effect, but the basic mechanism of a mediated interaction that depends on distance can be illustrated even in a simple one-dimensional Ising chain, as I show below (with a caveat).

1. The setup

As mentioned above, we focus on a simple one dimensional ferromagnetic Ising chain. This is a chain with spins \(s_i=\pm 1\), nearest-neighbor ferromagnetic coupling \(J>0\), and two distinguished boundary spins \(s_A\) and \(s_B\), separated by a finite segment of bulk spins. The Hamiltonian is simply written in the usual form

$$ H = -J\sum_i s_i s_{i+1}. $$

The thermodynamic free energy is, as usual,

$$ F = -k_B T \log Z, $$

where

$$ Z = \sum_{\{s\}} e^{-\beta H}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}. $$

What I want to do is slightly more specific: instead of summing over all spins including the two boundaries, I keep \(s_A\) and \(s_B\) fixed and sum over the bulk spins only. This gives a constrained partition function \(Z(s_A,s_B;n)\), where \(n\) is the number of transfer-matrix steps between the boundaries.

Once that quantity is known, one can read off the effective interaction mediated by the bulk.

2. Transfer matrix and fixed-boundary partition function

The transfer matrix for the one-dimensional Ising chain is

$$ T= \begin{pmatrix} e^{\beta J} & e^{-\beta J}\\ e^{-\beta J} & e^{\beta J} \end{pmatrix}. $$

It is symmetric, so we can diagonalize it immediately. The two eigenvalues are

$$ \lambda_+ = 2\cosh(\beta J), \qquad \lambda_- = 2\sinh(\beta J), $$

with eigenvectors proportional to

$$ e_+ \propto \binom{1}{1}, \qquad e_- \propto \binom{1}{-1}. $$

Therefore the spectral decomposition of \(T^n\) has the usual form

$$ T^n = \lambda_+^n P_+ + \lambda_-^n P_-, $$

where \(P_+\) and \(P_-\) are the projectors onto the symmetric and antisymmetric sectors. From this one obtains the matrix element between the two boundary spins:

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n) = (T^n)_{s_A,s_B}. $$

Since the transfer matrix is symmetric, it is convenient to diagonalize it first. Its two eigenvectors are the symmetric and antisymmetric combinations

$$ e_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\binom{1}{1}, \qquad e_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\binom{1}{-1}, $$

with eigenvalues

$$ \lambda_+ = 2\cosh(\beta J), \qquad \lambda_- = 2\sinh(\beta J). $$

Therefore the spectral decomposition of \(T\) is

$$ T = \lambda_+\, e_+ e_+^{\mathsf T} + \lambda_-\, e_- e_-^{\mathsf T}, $$

and hence

$$ T^n = \lambda_+^n\, e_+ e_+^{\mathsf T} + \lambda_-^n\, e_- e_-^{\mathsf T}. $$

Now compute the two rank-one matrices explicitly:

$$ e_+ e_+^{\mathsf T} = \frac12 \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1\\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad e_- e_-^{\mathsf T} = \frac12 \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. $$

Substituting these into the expression for \(T^n\), we obtain

$$ T^n = \frac{\lambda_+^n}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1\\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + \frac{\lambda_-^n}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. $$

At this point it is useful to label the row and column indices by the spin values \(s_A,s_B\in\{+1,-1\}\). With this convention, the matrix element can be written compactly as

$$ (T^n)_{s_A,s_B} = \frac{\lambda_+^n}{2} + \frac{\lambda_-^n}{2}\, s_A s_B. $$

In other words,

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac12\Bigl(\lambda_+^n + s_A s_B\,\lambda_-^n\Bigr). $$

Factoring out the dominant eigenvalue gives

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac{\lambda_+^n}{2} \left( 1+s_A s_B\left(\frac{\lambda_-}{\lambda_+}\right)^n \right). $$

Finally, since

$$ \frac{\lambda_-}{\lambda_+} = \frac{2\sinh(\beta J)}{2\cosh(\beta J)} = \tanh(\beta J), $$

one arrives at the compact form

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac{\lambda_+^n}{2} \left( 1+s_A s_B\,\tanh^n(\beta J) \right). $$

The factor \(1/2\) is just a normalization convention coming from the projectors. Since it does not depend on the boundary spins, it can be absorbed into the prefactor \(A(n)\) when we rewrite the answer as an effective two-spin Boltzmann weight.

Now, introducing

$$ t:=\tanh(\beta J)=\frac{\lambda_-}{\lambda_+}, $$

this becomes the much cleaner expression

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n)=\lambda_+^n\left(1+s_A s_B\, t^n\right). $$

This expression already contains the whole physical content of the calculation. The only dependence on the two boundary spins comes through the product \(s_A s_B\), exactly as one would expect from symmetry. Since \(t\) is smaller than one for any finite temperature, the contribution of the antisymmetric sector is suppressed with respect to the symmetric one, and the suppression increases with distance \(n\). What we do in the rest is just to massage this expression in another form that is a little bit more suggestive of an effective interaction between the two boundary spins, or "a force", and then read off the effective force from the distance dependence of that interaction.

As a side comment, one can see that summing the expression above over the two remaining spins gives the standard formula for the partition function of the Ising model in one dimension with free boundary conditions.

3. Effective coupling between the boundary spins

The next step is to rewrite the constrained partition function in the suggestive form

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n)=A(n)\,e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}, $$

where \(A(n)\) is an overall prefactor and \(J_n\) is the effective coupling between the boundary spins after the bulk has been integrated out. Comparing this with the previous expression gives

$$ \tanh(\beta J_n)=t^n=\tanh^n(\beta J). $$

Hence

$$ J_n=\frac{1}{\beta}\operatorname{arctanh}\!\big(\tanh^n(\beta J)\big). $$

So the boundary spins interact as if they were directly coupled, but with a distance-dependent renormalized coupling \(J_n\). The bulk has been reduced to a single number.

In particular, for parallel and antiparallel boundary conditions one gets

$$ Z_{++}=Z_{--}=A(n)e^{\beta J_n}, \qquad Z_{+-}=Z_{-+}=A(n)e^{-\beta J_n}. $$

The corresponding free energies are

$$ F_{++}=F_{--}=-k_B T\log A(n)-J_n, $$

$$ F_{+-}=F_{-+}=-k_B T\log A(n)+J_n. $$

Therefore

$$ F_{++}-F_{+-}=-2J_n<0. $$

So the effective interaction mediated by the bulk is ferromagnetic, as expected: aligned boundary spins are favored as one would expect. The effective coupling \(J_n\) decreases with distance, which is also expected: the further apart the boundaries, the weaker the mediated interaction. In this form, however, the distance dependence is somewhat hidden in the \(\operatorname{arctanh}\) function, so it is not immediately obvious how it behaves for large \(n\) and in particular near a critical point. For that, let's rewrite the answer in a more explicit form in the next section using a different tool: the potential of mean force.

4. Potential of mean force

There is another perfectly natural way to discuss the same phenomenon, namely through the potential of mean force. This concept comes up often in chemistry and biophysics, but it is really a general statistical-mechanics way of describing effective interactions. For instance, pressure itself can be viewed as an entropic force, and one can often read it off from the potential of mean force between two objects immersed in a fluctuating medium.

In the present setting, the PMF is the free energy associated with a given configuration of the boundary spins after integrating out the bulk degrees of freedom, but now with the constrained partition function normalized into a probability distribution. Instead of keeping the fixed-boundary partition function itself, we define

$$ P(s_A,s_B;n)=\frac{Z(s_A,s_B;n)}{\sum_{s'_A,s'_B} Z(s'_A,s'_B;n)}. $$

Using

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n)=A(n)e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}, $$

the denominator is

$$ \sum_{s_A,s_B} Z(s_A,s_B;n) = 4A(n)\cosh(\beta J_n), $$

and therefore

$$ P(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac{e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}}{4\cosh(\beta J_n)}. $$

Equivalently, using \(\tanh(\beta J_n)=\tanh^n(\beta J)\), this may be written as

$$ P(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac14\Bigl(1+s_A s_B\,\tanh^n(\beta J)\Bigr). $$

The potential of mean force is then defined by

$$ W_n(s_A,s_B):=-k_B T\log P(s_A,s_B;n). $$

Substituting the probability above gives

$$ W_n(s_A,s_B) = -k_B T\log\!\left(\frac{e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}}{4\cosh(\beta J_n)}\right) = -J_n s_A s_B + k_B T\log\!\bigl(4\cosh(\beta J_n)\bigr). $$

So, up to the \(n\)-dependent normalization term, the PMF is just the same effective coupling written in probabilistic language. The aligned configurations have lower PMF than the anti-aligned ones, again reflecting the induced ferromagnetic interaction.

If one wants a discrete force directly from the PMF, the natural lattice definition is

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n(s_A,s_B) := W_n(s_A,s_B)-W_{n+1}(s_A,s_B). $$

This gives

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n(s_A,s_B) = (J_{n+1}-J_n)s_A s_B + k_B T\log\!\frac{\cosh(\beta J_n)}{\cosh(\beta J_{n+1})}. $$

So the PMF contains both the interaction term and the normalization term coming from the full boundary distribution. It is the same physics, just packaged in a slightly different way. Note that at large distances the second term is subleading with respect to the first one, since \(J_n/J_{n+1}\approx 1\), so the PMF force asymptotically coincides with the force defined from the effective free energy.

5. A "Casimir-like" force from the effective free energy

If one wants to speak of a Casimir-like force, the natural thing to do is to define it from the change of effective free energy with separation. Since the distance \(n\) is discrete, the most natural lattice definition is a finite difference:

$$ \mathcal F_n(s_A,s_B) := F_{\mathrm{eff}}(s_A,s_B;n)-F_{\mathrm{eff}}(s_A,s_B;n+1). $$

Using

$$ F_{\mathrm{eff}}(s_A,s_B;n) = -k_B T\log A(n)-J_n s_A s_B, $$

one gets

$$ \mathcal F_n(s_A,s_B) = k_B T\bigl[\log A(n+1)-\log A(n)\bigr] +(J_{n+1}-J_n)s_A s_B. $$

If one is mainly interested in the interaction-dependent part, the relevant term is the one controlled by \(J_n\). Since \(J_n\) decreases with \(n\), the induced interaction weakens with distance, which is exactly what one expects.

In other words, the chain mediates an effective boundary-boundary interaction, and that interaction decays as the two ends are pulled apart. I am not trying to claim here that this is the full-fledged critical Casimir story. It is a very small toy model. But as a cartoon of the mechanism — integrate out the bulk, obtain a distance-dependent effective free energy, and differentiate with respect to distance — it already captures the main idea.

Let us now understand the large-distance behavior of the effective coupling \(J_n\), and how it is related to the correlation length of the system. The starting point is

$$ J_n=\frac{1}{\beta}\operatorname{arctanh}(t^n), \qquad t=\tanh(\beta J). $$

Away from zero temperature one has \(|t|<1\), so for large \(n\),

$$ t^n \ll 1. $$

Since \(\operatorname{arctanh}(x)\sim x\) for small \(x\), this gives

$$ J_n \sim \frac{1}{\beta} t^n. $$

Writing this in exponential form,

$$ t^n = e^{n\log t}=e^{-n/\xi}, $$

one identifies naturally the correlation length of the one-dimensional Ising model:

$$ \xi = -\frac{1}{\log(\tanh(\beta J))}. $$

Therefore the effective interaction decays as

$$ J_n \sim \frac{1}{\beta}e^{-n/\xi}. $$

So in this simple one-dimensional model the induced interaction is exponentially screened at finite temperature. That is exactly what finite correlation length should look like.

The ordinary one-dimensional nearest-neighbor Ising model has no finite-temperature critical point. The only place where \(\xi\) diverges is \(T\to 0\), where \(\tanh(\beta J)\to 1\), and this will matter in a moment.

We can now plug this asymptotic form into the PMF force. Recall that

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n(s_A,s_B) = (J_{n+1}-J_n)s_A s_B + k_B T\log\!\frac{\cosh(\beta J_n)}{\cosh(\beta J_{n+1})}. $$

Since

$$ J_n \sim \frac{1}{\beta}t^n = k_B T\, e^{-n/\xi}, $$

we immediately get

$$ J_{n+1}-J_n \sim k_B T\left(e^{-(n+1)/\xi}-e^{-n/\xi}\right) = -\,k_B T\left(1-e^{-1/\xi}\right)e^{-n/\xi}. $$

Now look at the logarithmic term. For large \(n\), \(\beta J_n\ll 1\), so

$$ \cosh(\beta J_n)=1+\mathcal O\!\big((\beta J_n)^2\big), $$

and hence

$$ \log\!\frac{\cosh(\beta J_n)}{\cosh(\beta J_{n+1})} = \mathcal O\!\big(e^{-2n/\xi}\big). $$

So this second term is subleading compared with the first one, which is of order \(e^{-n/\xi}\). Keeping only the dominant contribution, we find

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n(s_A,s_B) \sim -\,k_B T\left(1-e^{-1/\xi}\right)s_A s_B\, e^{-n/\xi}. $$

In particular, up to a sign and an order-one prefactor, the potential-of-mean-force interaction decays exponentially:

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n \propto k_B T\, e^{-n/\xi}. $$

The important point is not the exact prefactor, but that the decay is controlled by the same correlation length \(\xi\). At large separation, the PMF force is exponentially screened.

At the critical point, where \(\xi\to\infty\), this exponential screening disappears. In the present model, however, that limit occurs only at \(T=0\), so although the range diverges, the thermal prefactor \(k_B T\) also goes to zero. In that sense, the critical point of the one-dimensional Ising chain is not a very interesting place for a thermal Casimir force: the range becomes infinite precisely when the thermal scale vanishes. Despite these problems, I hope this helps to illustrate the general mechanism, though, and that it can be a useful toy model to keep in mind when thinking about the more complicated critical Casimir effect in higher dimensions.
