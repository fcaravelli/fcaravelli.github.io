Casimir force in a single-spin model

March 30th, 2026

I was scribbling through a very simple one-dimensional Ising model and ended up thinking about an equally simple question: if I take two boundary spins, integrate out everything in between, and then ask how their effective interaction depends on distance, what kind of force do I get?

Calling it a “Casimir force” in one dimension is maybe a bit grandiose, but the logic is the same enough to be useful: you eliminate intermediate degrees of freedom, obtain an effective free energy between distant boundary variables, and then study how that free energy changes with separation.

This is one of those calculations that I like because it is elementary, but not completely trivial. The transfer-matrix method does all the work, and at the end one gets a neat exponentially decaying effective interaction. Then, as a separate remark, one can also ask what an entropic point of view would look like.

1. The setup

Consider a one-dimensional Ising chain with spins \(s_i=\pm 1\), nearest-neighbor ferromagnetic coupling \(J>0\), and two distinguished boundary spins \(s_A\) and \(s_B\), separated by a finite segment of bulk spins. The Hamiltonian is

$$ H = -J\sum_i s_i s_{i+1}. $$

The thermodynamic free energy is, as usual,

$$ F = -k_B T \log Z, $$

where

$$ Z = \sum_{\{s\}} e^{-\beta H}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}. $$

What I want to do is slightly more specific: instead of summing over all spins including the two boundaries, I keep \(s_A\) and \(s_B\) fixed and sum over the bulk spins only. This gives a constrained partition function \(Z(s_A,s_B;n)\), where \(n\) is the number of transfer-matrix steps between the boundaries.

Once that quantity is known, one can read off the effective interaction mediated by the bulk.

2. Transfer matrix and fixed-boundary partition function

The transfer matrix for the one-dimensional Ising chain is

$$ T= \begin{pmatrix} e^{\beta J} & e^{-\beta J}\\ e^{-\beta J} & e^{\beta J} \end{pmatrix}. $$

It is symmetric, so it diagonalizes immediately. The two eigenvalues are

$$ \lambda_+ = 2\cosh(\beta J), \qquad \lambda_- = 2\sinh(\beta J), $$

with eigenvectors proportional to

$$ e_+ \propto \binom{1}{1}, \qquad e_- \propto \binom{1}{-1}. $$

Therefore the spectral decomposition of \(T^n\) has the usual form

$$ T^n = \lambda_+^n P_+ + \lambda_-^n P_-, $$

where \(P_+\) and \(P_-\) are the projectors onto the symmetric and antisymmetric sectors. From this one obtains the matrix element between the two boundary spins:

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n) = (T^n)_{s_A,s_B}. $$

After a little algebra, the result can be written as

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n) = \lambda_+^n\left(1+s_A s_B\left(\frac{\lambda_-}{\lambda_+}\right)^n\right). $$

Introducing

$$ t:=\tanh(\beta J)=\frac{\lambda_-}{\lambda_+}, $$

this becomes the much cleaner expression

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n)=\lambda_+^n\left(1+s_A s_B\, t^n\right). $$

This is already the whole physical content of the calculation. The only dependence on the two boundary spins comes through the product \(s_A s_B\), exactly as one would expect from symmetry.

3. Effective coupling between the boundary spins

The next step is to rewrite the constrained partition function in the suggestive form

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n)=A(n)\,e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}, $$

where \(A(n)\) is an overall prefactor and \(J_n\) is the effective coupling between the boundary spins after the bulk has been integrated out. Comparing this with the previous expression gives

$$ \tanh(\beta J_n)=t^n=\tanh^n(\beta J). $$

Hence

$$ J_n=\frac{1}{\beta}\operatorname{arctanh}\!\big(\tanh^n(\beta J)\big). $$

So the boundary spins interact as if they were directly coupled, but with a distance-dependent renormalized coupling \(J_n\). The bulk has been reduced to a single number.

In particular, for parallel and antiparallel boundary conditions one gets

$$ Z_{++}=Z_{--}=A(n)e^{\beta J_n}, \qquad Z_{+-}=Z_{-+}=A(n)e^{-\beta J_n}. $$

The corresponding free energies are

$$ F_{++}=F_{--}=-k_B T\log A(n)-J_n, $$

$$ F_{+-}=F_{-+}=-k_B T\log A(n)+J_n. $$

Therefore

$$ F_{++}-F_{+-}=-2J_n<0. $$

So the effective interaction mediated by the bulk is ferromagnetic, as expected: aligned boundary spins are favored.

4. Potential of mean force

There is another perfectly natural way to package the same information, namely through the potential of mean force. Instead of keeping the fixed-boundary partition function itself, we normalize it into a probability distribution for the two boundary spins:

$$ P(s_A,s_B;n)=\frac{Z(s_A,s_B;n)}{\sum_{s'_A,s'_B} Z(s'_A,s'_B;n)}. $$

Using

$$ Z(s_A,s_B;n)=A(n)e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}, $$

the denominator is

$$ \sum_{s_A,s_B} Z(s_A,s_B;n) = 4A(n)\cosh(\beta J_n), $$

and therefore

$$ P(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac{e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}}{4\cosh(\beta J_n)}. $$

Equivalently, using \(\tanh(\beta J_n)=\tanh^n(\beta J)\), one may write

$$ P(s_A,s_B;n) = \frac14\Bigl(1+s_A s_B\,\tanh^n(\beta J)\Bigr). $$

The potential of mean force is then defined as

$$ W_n(s_A,s_B):=-k_B T\log P(s_A,s_B;n). $$

Substituting the probability above gives

$$ W_n(s_A,s_B) = -k_B T\log\!\left(\frac{e^{\beta J_n s_A s_B}}{4\cosh(\beta J_n)}\right) = -J_n s_A s_B + k_B T\log\!\bigl(4\cosh(\beta J_n)\bigr). $$

So, up to the \(n\)-dependent normalization term, the PMF is just the same effective coupling written in probabilistic language. The aligned configurations have lower PMF than the anti-aligned ones, again reflecting the induced ferromagnetic interaction.

If one wants a discrete “force” directly from the PMF, the natural lattice definition is

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n(s_A,s_B) := W_n(s_A,s_B)-W_{n+1}(s_A,s_B). $$

This gives

$$ \mathcal F^{\rm PMF}_n(s_A,s_B) = (J_{n+1}-J_n)s_A s_B + k_B T\log\!\frac{\cosh(\beta J_n)}{\cosh(\beta J_{n+1})}. $$

So the PMF contains both the interaction term and the normalization term coming from the full boundary distribution. It is the same physics, just packaged in a slightly different way.

I like this formulation because it makes the probabilistic meaning very explicit: the mediated interaction can be read either from the fixed-boundary free energy or from the normalized probability of observing the two boundary spins in a given configuration.

5. A Casimir-like force from the effective free energy

If one wants to speak of a Casimir-like force, the natural thing to do is to define it from the change of effective free energy with separation. Since the distance \(n\) is discrete, the most natural lattice definition is a finite difference:

$$ \mathcal F_n(s_A,s_B) := F_{\mathrm{eff}}(s_A,s_B;n)-F_{\mathrm{eff}}(s_A,s_B;n+1). $$

Using

$$ F_{\mathrm{eff}}(s_A,s_B;n) = -k_B T\log A(n)-J_n s_A s_B, $$

one gets

$$ \mathcal F_n(s_A,s_B) = k_B T\bigl[\log A(n+1)-\log A(n)\bigr] +(J_{n+1}-J_n)s_A s_B. $$

If one is mainly interested in the interaction-dependent part, the relevant term is the one controlled by \(J_n\). Since \(J_n\) decreases with \(n\), the induced interaction weakens with distance, which is exactly what one expects.

In other words, the chain mediates an effective boundary-boundary interaction, and that interaction decays as the two ends are pulled apart.

I am not trying to claim here that this is the full-fledged critical Casimir story. It is a very small toy model. But as a cartoon of the mechanism — integrate out the bulk, obtain a distance-dependent effective free energy, differentiate with respect to distance — it already captures the main idea.

6. Large-distance asymptotics and correlation length

The large-\(n\) behavior is immediate once the effective coupling is written as

$$ J_n=\frac{1}{\beta}\operatorname{arctanh}(t^n), \qquad t=\tanh(\beta J). $$

Away from zero temperature one has \(|t|<1\), so for large \(n\),

$$ t^n \ll 1. $$

Since \(\operatorname{arctanh}(x)\sim x\) for small \(x\), this gives

$$ J_n \sim \frac{1}{\beta} t^n. $$

Writing this in exponential form,

$$ t^n = e^{n\log t}=e^{-n/\xi}, $$

one identifies the correlation length

$$ \xi = -\frac{1}{\log(\tanh(\beta J))}. $$

Therefore the effective interaction decays as

$$ J_n \sim \frac{1}{\beta}e^{-n/\xi}. $$

So in this simple one-dimensional model the induced force is exponentially screened at finite temperature. That is exactly what finite correlation length should look like.

The ordinary one-dimensional nearest-neighbor Ising model has no finite-temperature critical point. The only place where \(\xi\) diverges is \(T\to 0\), where \(\tanh(\beta J)\to 1\).

7. Alternative point of view: entropy

Up to now everything came from the transfer-matrix calculation. But there is another way to think about forces of this kind, and it is worth keeping conceptually separate. Namely: what if the force is not read primarily as an energetic interaction, but as an entropic effect?

The free energy is

$$ F = E - TS, $$

and entropy counts the logarithm of the number of accessible configurations:

$$ S = k_B \log \Omega. $$

So if the number of allowed configurations depends on the separation between two objects, then even without changing the microscopic coupling, the free energy can acquire a distance dependence simply because the entropy changes. That is the basic logic behind entropic forces.

In the present one-dimensional Ising example, the transfer-matrix derivation already gives the exact answer, so there is no need to invoke entropy separately. Still, it is useful to keep this alternative point of view in mind: integrating out bulk degrees of freedom may generate an effective interaction not only because energy is being transmitted through the medium, but also because the number of compatible bulk configurations depends on what the boundaries are doing.

So one can tell two complementary stories. The first is the one we actually computed: the bulk generates an effective coupling \(J_n\) between the two ends. The second is a more thermodynamic story: different boundary conditions constrain the bulk differently, and this changes the entropy, hence the free energy, hence the force.

I would not derive the transfer-matrix result from entropy alone here. That would be a different argument. But as an interpretation, it is perfectly fair: a distance-dependent force can also be thought of as the free-energy price of changing the number of available configurations in the medium.
