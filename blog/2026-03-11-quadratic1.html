Quadratic fixed points, trees, and background fields

March 10th, 2026

A deceptively simple equation can teach you most of what you need to know about perturbation theory, diagram expansions, and why “multiple solutions” are not a bug but a feature.

This post starts with a one-dimensional quadratic fixed-point equation. We will:

  • derive the tree expansion (Catalan numbers show up immediately),
  • explain why that expansion selects only one of the two quadratic roots,
  • recover the other root by expanding around a different background,
  • generalize the story to an $N$-dimensional quadratic system,
  • and connect “multiple branches” to mean-field / background-field approximations.

1. The scalar quadratic fixed point

Consider the fixed-point equation

$$ C = S + \\chi C^2. $$

Rearranging gives a quadratic polynomial:

$$ \\chi C^2 - C + S = 0. $$

As everyone learns in school, there are two solutions:

$$ C_{\\pm}(\\chi) = \\frac{1 \\pm \\sqrt{1 - 4\\chi S}}{2\\chi}. $$

The important point for what follows is not the formula—it’s the branch structure. The two solutions behave very differently as $\\chi \\to 0$.

2. Tree expansion and the analytic branch

Suppose we want a small-$\\chi$ expansion. Make the ansatz

$$ C(\\chi) = \\sum_{n\\ge 0} \\chi^n C^{(n)}, \\qquad C^{(0)} = S. $$

Plugging into $C = S + \\chi C^2$ yields the recursion

$$ C^{(n)} = \\sum_{m=0}^{n-1} C^{(m)} C^{(n-1-m)}, \\qquad n\\ge 1. $$

The first few coefficients are:

$$ C = S + \\chi S^2 + 2\\chi^2 S^3 + 5\\chi^3 S^4 + 14\\chi^4 S^5 + \\cdots $$

The integers $1,1,2,5,14,\\dots$ are the Catalan numbers: they count rooted binary trees. Diagrammatically, each factor of $\\chi$ corresponds to adding one branching vertex, and each leaf contributes a factor of $S$.

This is the key reconciliation: the rooted-tree sum is not “the” solution. It is the solution that is analytic at $\\chi=0$ and satisfies $C \\to S$ as $\\chi \\to 0$.

Indeed, the tree series is exactly the Taylor expansion of

$$ C_{-}(\\chi) = \\frac{1 - \\sqrt{1 - 4\\chi S}}{2\\chi}, $$

because this is the branch with $C_-(\\chi)\\to S$ as $\\chi\\to 0$.

3. Where is the second solution hiding?

The other quadratic root behaves like $C_+(\\chi) \\sim 1/\\chi$ for small $\\chi$, so it cannot be represented as a power series in $\\chi$ with a finite $\\chi\\to 0$ limit.

To recover it, you must expand around a different background. This is the simplest version of a background-field approximation: pick a reference solution and expand in fluctuations around it.

$$ C = C^0 + \\delta. $$

If $C^0$ is an exact root, it satisfies $C^0 = S + \\chi (C^0)^2$. Plugging $C=C^0+\\delta$ into the equation gives

$$ \\delta = 2\\chi C^0\\,\\delta + \\chi\\,\\delta^2. $$

Rearranging,

$$ (1 - 2\\chi C^0)\\,\\delta = \\chi\\,\\delta^2. $$

Around an exact root there is no constant forcing term, so the only small solution is $\\delta=0$. That is exactly what you want: you are already at the chosen branch.

The right way to generate a nontrivial perturbation is to deform the problem slightly. For example, let $S \\mapsto S + \\varepsilon \\Delta S$ and expand $\\delta$ in $\\varepsilon$. Then the linear operator $(1-2\\chi C^0)$ plays the role of a propagator around the chosen branch.

4. The $N$-dimensional quadratic system

Now replace the scalar $C$ by a vector $C\\in\\mathbb{R}^N$ and consider

$$ C_i = S_i + \\chi \\sum_{j,k=1}^N T_{ijk}\\,C_j C_k. $$

It is useful to package the quadratic term as a bilinear map

$$ (B(u,v))_i := \\sum_{j,k} T_{ijk}\\,u_j v_k. $$

Then the fixed point reads

$$ C = S + \\chi\\,B(C,C). $$

5. Generic tree expansion in small $\\chi$

As in the scalar case, assume a series

$$ C(\\chi) = \\sum_{n\\ge 0} \\chi^n C^{(n)}, \\qquad C^{(0)}=S. $$

Plugging into $C=S+\\chi B(C,C)$ gives the coefficient recursion

$$ C^{(n)} = \\sum_{m=0}^{n-1} B\\big(C^{(m)},\\,C^{(n-1-m)}\\big), \\qquad n\\ge 1. $$

The first two corrections are

$$ C^{(1)} = B(S,S), \\qquad C^{(2)} = B\\big(S, C^{(1)}\\big) + B\\big(C^{(1)}, S\\big). $$

If $T_{ijk}$ is symmetric in $j \\leftrightarrow k$, the two terms coincide and $C^{(2)} = 2B(S,C^{(1)})$.

Combinatorially, $C^{(n)}$ is a sum over rooted binary trees with $n$ internal vertices: each internal vertex contributes a tensor $T_{ijk}$, each leaf contributes a source $S$, and indices are contracted along edges.

6. Multiple solutions and why you should expect them

The scalar equation has two roots. The $N$-dimensional system is $N$ coupled quadratic equations. Over the complex numbers, a generic quadratic system has up to $2^N$ isolated solutions (counting multiplicity).

Over the reals the number can be anywhere between $0$ and $2^N$, and in special (degenerate) cases solutions can form continua. The moral is simply: branch structure is the rule, not the exception.

The small-$\\chi$ tree series constructs the branch that satisfies $C(\\chi)\\to S$ as $\\chi\\to 0$. Other branches need other choices of background.

7. Mean field and the background-field expansion

The background-field idea is straightforward: choose a reference solution (exact or approximate) and expand around it.

$$ C = C^0 + \\delta. $$

Here $C^0$ is chosen to solve the full equation (or a mean-field approximation to it):

$$ C^0 = S + \\chi B(C^0, C^0). $$

Plugging $C^0+\\delta$ into the full equation and using bilinearity gives

$$ \\delta = \\chi\\big(B(C^0,\\delta)+B(\\delta,C^0)\\big) + \\chi B(\\delta,\\delta). $$

Define the Jacobian at the background $C^0$:

$$ J := I - \\chi\\big(B(C^0,\\cdot)+B(\\cdot,C^0)\\big). $$

Then the fluctuation equation is

$$ J\\,\\delta = \\chi B(\\delta,\\delta). $$

If $J$ is invertible, define the propagator $G := J^{-1}$, giving the fixed-point form

$$ \\delta = \\chi\\,G\\,B(\\delta,\\delta). $$

Different choices of background $C^0$ correspond to different branches—exactly like choosing $C_-$ vs $C_+$ in the scalar quadratic. The linear operator $G=J^{-1}$ is the multi-dimensional analogue of the scalar denominator $(1-2\\chi C^0)^{-1}$.

To generate a nontrivial tree expansion around a chosen branch, deform the problem (e.g. perturb the source $S\\mapsto S+\\varepsilon\\Delta S$) and expand $\\delta$ in powers of $\\varepsilon$. Each internal line carries $G$, each vertex carries $T$, each leaf carries $\\Delta S$: a rooted-tree expansion around the background field.

Takeaways

  • The scalar fixed point $C=S+\\chi C^2$ has two roots; the small-$\\chi$ tree expansion selects the branch analytic at $\\chi=0$.
  • The $N$-dimensional quadratic system admits many branches; a generic quadratic system can have up to $2^N$ complex solutions.
  • Tree expansions are best understood as perturbation theory around a chosen background (mean field / saddle / branch).
  • The background-field Jacobian $J$ and its inverse $G$ play the role of propagator; they encode which branch you are expanding around.
