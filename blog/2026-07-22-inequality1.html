Who owns the debate on inequality?

July 20th, 2026

This is not an accusation against the journalists or editors who wrote the article discussed below. It is an attempt to follow a simple question: when a powerful institution asks society to debate inequality, what can we learn by examining the institution itself?

On 16 July 2026, Nature published an editorial entitled “Why there needs to be a global debate on inequality.” The article discussed an ambitious report proposing a more equal distribution of wealth, a global wealth tax, a global justice fund and a substantial transfer of resources towards health, education and climate adaptation. I agree, almost completely. The "almost" is important here, at least to who writes.

I agree with the central premise. Inequality is not a secondary issue that can be postponed until after climate change, political instability or technological disruption have been solved. Distribution affects how all of these problems are experienced and whether proposed solutions will be politically accepted.

But the editorial also made me curious. Who owns Nature? Where do the revenues produced by scientific publishing go? And who benefits when the business performs well?

1. The editorial

The article is based on the Global Justice Report, coordinated by economists at the World Inequality Lab. I am fairly sure that who wrote the article cares about the issue. Indeed, I commend them for bringing up the issue. The report considers a future in which higher living standards, ecological constraints and a much more equal distribution of economic resources are addressed together rather than as separate problems.

Among its more radical proposals are highly progressive taxes on wealth and income, a global redistribution fund, shorter working hours and a sovereign fund that would gradually acquire a portion of the world’s productive capital.

One can disagree with the assumptions, the political feasibility or the precise numbers. The editorial itself acknowledges that some of the proposals are utopian. Nevertheless, its call for a serious debate is reasonable. What matters is that the debate should not remain abstract.

Inequality is generated and reproduced through actual institutions: firms, property rights, financial vehicles, inheritance, universities, governments and markets. Scientific publishing is one of those institutions.

2. Who owns Nature?

Nature is one of the principal brands of Springer Nature. The company was created in 2015 by combining Nature Publishing Group, Macmillan Education and Springer Science+Business Media. Springer Nature was subsequently listed on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange in October 2024.

According to Springer Nature’s official shareholder information, updated on 1 June 2026, the ownership structure was:

50.6% Holtzbrinck Publishing Group
32.1% BC Partners-controlled investment vehicle
16.1% Public free float
1.2% Other shareholders

Holtzbrinck is a privately held and family-owned German publishing group. The second-largest block is held through Springer Science+Business Media Galileo Participation S.à r.l., which Springer Nature identifies as a majority-owned and controlled indirect subsidiary of funds advised by BC Partners.

It is therefore not quite correct to describe Nature as simply “owned by a billionaire,” because the corporate chain is more complicated than that. Nor is every euro earned by Springer Nature transferred directly to one person.

It is correct, however, to say that the largest portion of the company is controlled by a private family-owned group, while another large portion is controlled through private-equity funds. Only a relatively small part of the company is freely traded on the stock market.

3. Following the money

The financial reports make the scale of the business visible. Springer Nature reported revenue of approximately €1.93 billion in 2025 and adjusted operating profit of approximately €543.6 million. Its Research division, which includes scientific journals and related services, produced €486.4 million of that adjusted operating profit.

€1.926bn 2025 group revenue
€543.6m Adjusted operating profit
€486.4m Research-segment adjusted operating profit
€165.1m Dividend distribution approved in 2026

These numbers do not demonstrate wrongdoing. A publisher has employees, technology costs, offices, legal responsibilities, digital infrastructure and long-term investments. Revenue is not the same as personal income, and operating profit is not the same as cash paid to owners.

But profits can eventually reach owners through dividends, share sales and changes in the value of their holdings. For the 2025 financial year, Springer Nature approved a dividend of €0.83 per share, corresponding to a total distribution of approximately €165.1 million. The company states that its policy is to distribute approximately half of adjusted consolidated net income.

A shareholder owning 50.6% of the company would, in a simplified proportional calculation, be associated with roughly €83.5 million of this distribution:

$$ 0.506 \times 165.1\ \text{million euros} \simeq 83.5\ \text{million euros}. $$

This is an estimate of the dividend attributable to the shareholding block, not a claim that this amount was paid personally to Stefan von Holtzbrinck. The stake is held through corporate structures, and the subsequent allocation of funds within a privately held group is not fully visible from Springer Nature’s public reports.

This distinction is essential. The relevant observation is not that one identifiable individual receives every euro. It is that income generated by a vast international research system ultimately produces returns for a comparatively narrow ownership structure.

4. The individual level

Stefan von Holtzbrinck is the chair of Springer Nature’s Supervisory Board and the chief executive of the family-owned Holtzbrinck Publishing Group. Public rich lists have consistently classified him as a billionaire.

Historical wealth estimates should be treated with caution. Privately held companies do not have a continuously observable market price, and rich lists infer values from partial information. They are estimates, not audited personal balance sheets.

With that qualification, published German rich-list data estimated Stefan von Holtzbrinck’s wealth at roughly €1 billion in 2005 and around €4.8 billion in 2024. It would be tempting to interpret this as a precise increase caused by Springer Nature. That conclusion would be unjustified.

The Holtzbrinck group owns other publishing and investment assets, corporate structures changed during this period, and valuation methods changed when Springer Nature became publicly traded. The 2024 flotation gave the market a visible price for an asset that had previously been valued privately. Part of the apparent jump can therefore reflect better price discovery rather than newly created wealth in that single year.

We can make a firmer statement about Springer Nature’s salaried leadership. Its 2025 remuneration report records €2.253 million in remuneration granted and owed to chief executive Frank Vrancken Peeters for the year. His contractual target remuneration, including long-term incentives, was €3.3 million.

Once again, there is nothing illegal or hidden here: these figures are disclosed in the annual report. But they matter when thinking about the institutional setting in which calls for redistribution are produced.

5. The point is not hypocrisy

The easy conclusion would be to accuse Nature of hypocrisy: a profitable publisher, ultimately controlled by wealthy owners, publishes an editorial asking for greater equality.

I do not think that is the most interesting conclusion.

The editorial staff of a scientific journal is not identical to its shareholders. A serious institution should be capable of publishing evidence and arguments that are uncomfortable for its owners. Indeed, editorial independence is valuable precisely because it can produce this separation.

The more important point is that inequality is not external to the production of knowledge. Researchers write papers, provide peer review and often perform editorial work. Much of the underlying research is publicly financed. Universities and libraries then pay for access, while authors or funders increasingly pay article processing charges for open-access publication.

Springer Nature itself explains that the budgets of its principal Research customers depend substantially on public investment in universities, research and teaching. Its annual report also notes that under open access the payment moves from readers towards authors, generally through research funding.

There is therefore a circular structure:

$$ \text{public funding} \longrightarrow \text{research} \longrightarrow \text{articles and peer review} \longrightarrow \text{publishing revenue} \longrightarrow \text{profits and returns to capital}. $$

This does not imply that publishers add no value. They organize editorial processes, maintain archives and platforms, coordinate production, protect brands and provide forms of quality control. The proper question is not whether the value added is exactly zero.

The question is how the value jointly produced by researchers, universities, funders, editors and publishers should be divided. That is itself a question of inequality.

Perhaps this is the most productive way to read the editorial. A global debate on inequality should not only identify inequality in distant industries, tax havens or abstract macroeconomic models. It should also examine the institutions through which the debate is communicated.

Scientific publishing is unusually well suited for this discussion because researchers occupy several positions at once. We are the authors, referees, readers, editors, customers and, frequently through our taxes, the original funders of the work.

The problem is therefore not that Nature discussed inequality. The problem would be to discuss inequality everywhere except at home.
