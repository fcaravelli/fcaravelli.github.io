The New Pace of Civilizations

April 28th, 2026

I have been thinking a lot lately about the relation between technology and the feeling that history itself is speeding up. The question is not just how much we can do, but how fast we have to do it. We usually measure progress in output, productivity, growth, and GDP. But those quantities only capture one side of the story. Right now, there is quite a delay between the shock of a new technology and the full economic consequences of that shock. The other side is the delay between the shock and the social reaction to that shock. The first is about production; the second is about adaptation. So far we have barely felt the shock caused by the war in Iran, unless you are in the war zone, or if you are in the oil industry. But the shock will propagate through the economy, and it will cause a wave of reactions that will be felt by everyone. The speed of that shock is due to the speed at which the last loaded ship that left Iran is now being unloaded in a port, and the speed at which the oil is being processed, and the speed at which the price changes are being transmitted to consumers. That is the pace of the shock, and it is much faster than the pace of the war itself. On the other hand, our days are loaded with news, because those are fast to reach us. The medium, propagates information close to the speed of light, and then now in seconds what happened on the other side of the world. How quickly information moves, how rapidly decisions propagate, and how little time societies have to adapt before the next wave arrives: these are questions related to non-equilibrium.

There is something odd about the present moment. We do not merely feel that technology is improving. We feel that our time constant as a civilization is changing. Decisions that once took months now take days; reactions that once took days now take minutes; and expectations that once belonged to institutions are now imposed on individuals.

In that sense, technological progress is not just an increase in capability. It is an increase in the pace at which society is forced to reorganize itself.

1. What does it mean for an economy to have a pace?

We are used to thinking of an economy in static terms. We ask how rich it is, how much it produces, how fast it grows, how productive its workers are. These are all useful questions, but they are not quite the same as asking how fast the society itself is running.

Let me try a rough distinction. Productivity measures how much output is generated per worker or per hour. Pace, instead, measures how quickly a system absorbs information, updates expectations, reallocates resources, and changes behavior. One is about the amount produced. The other is about the speed of adaptation.

A society can become more productive without becoming dramatically faster in the lived sense. Our cells still run at the same speed. Our muscles still contract at the same speed. Our neurons still fire at the same speed. Conversely, a society can feel much faster even when measured output does not fully capture that acceleration. Anyone who has lived through the transition from letters to email, from email to smartphones, and from search engines to generative AI has felt this directly. In my lifetime I literally went through such transitions, and I can say that the pace of life has changed more than the amount of work I do.

Pace is not just economic. It is cultural, cognitive, institutional. It is the time it takes for news to circulate, for prices to adjust, for a panic to spread, for a style to become obsolete, for a scientific result to become a product, and for a product to become a dependency.

A civilization has a pace when information, production, and decision-making propagate on a characteristic timescale. Technology changes that timescale.

2. Ancient worlds, medieval worlds, modern worlds

For most of human history, economic life moved very slowly. Long-run historical reconstructions suggest that average incomes changed only modestly over many centuries before modern growth took off. This does not mean that nothing happened. It means that local bursts of sophistication did not yet translate into sustained, compounding acceleration at the scale of civilization.

Think of the Greeks. Their world was intellectually extraordinary: geometry, philosophy, political experimentation, drama, natural speculation. And yet the material and informational metabolism of society was still slow. News traveled by foot, by ship, by memory, by rumor. When we recall the legend of the messenger from Marathon, what strikes us is not just heroism. It is the fact that the body itself was the communication channel, which had to move at the pace of a human being. That is a very different world from the one we live in today, where information can travel at the speed of light, and where the physical world can be manipulated at the speed of electrons.

The Roman world increased scale. Roads, administration, engineering, and logistics created a more integrated imperial machine. The Chinese empires, in different ways and at different times, also achieved very high levels of administrative sophistication, agricultural organization, and technological ingenuity. These societies were not static. But their speed was still constrained by the speed of physical transport, the durability of record-keeping, and the slowness of coordination over long distances.

The Middle Ages are often misremembered as simple stagnation. That is too crude. There were institutional changes, urban networks, innovations in agriculture, finance, architecture, and trade. But still, the pace of transformation remained low enough that generations could live under roughly stable technological conditions. One could plausibly be born, work, marry, age, and die within the same technological regime.

The Renaissance and the early modern period changed something deeper. The printing press mattered not just because books became more available, but because reproducible information became a structural force. Knowledge was no longer merely transmitted; it could be replicated, standardized, compared, corrected, and accumulated. Before, books had to be copied by hand, and that was a bottleneck for the spread of ideas. With the printing press, that bottleneck was removed, and the pace of intellectual change accelerated. The Enlightenment and the scientific revolution then turned this into a method. At that point, society began to discover a new engine: knowledge could generate technology, technology could generate wealth, wealth could fund institutions, and institutions could generate more knowledge.

The Industrial Revolution did not just make economies richer. It changed their rhythm. Once growth becomes sustained and compounding, pace ceases to be an occasional disturbance and becomes the normal condition.

3. Productivity is not enough: information has its own physics

Economists often quantify progress through productivity. This makes sense. If one worker with one machine can produce what ten workers once produced by hand, something important has happened. Output per worker, or per hour, is one natural metric for technological change.

But productivity alone does not capture the feeling that societies are speeding up. The missing piece is information.

A messenger runs from Marathon to Athens. Then a horse carries dispatches. Then ships move letters across seas. Then the telegraph makes it possible to separate communication from transportation. That is a civilizational break. The telephone adds immediacy to voice. Radio adds broadcast. The internet collapses transmission times for text, sound, and image. The smartphone fuses communication, computation, memory, navigation, markets, and social presence into a single portable object.

What changed across these layers was not only the amount of information sent. It was the time needed for information to become socially effective. Once communication becomes nearly instantaneous, delays migrate elsewhere: into institutions, into regulation, into psychology, into attention, into trust.

Modern acceleration is not merely that we can do more. It is that signals now travel faster than our habits, and often faster than our institutions.

This is why the notion of pace cannot be reduced to GDP. GDP counts production. Pace concerns transmission and reaction. A slow society can be productive by the standards of its age. A fast society is one in which expectations must be continuously updated because information keeps arriving before equilibrium is restored.

4. Technology inside production networks

There is also a more economic way to say all this. In many standard growth models, such as the Solow model, technology appears as an aggregate driver: a parameter, or a residual, that shifts the production frontier and makes long-run growth possible. This is powerful, but also incomplete. It tells us that technology matters, but not how it spreads through the fine structure of the economy.

Real economies are not single production functions. They are networks of interconnected productive units: industries buying inputs from other industries, transforming them, and passing them downstream. At that more coarse-grained level, each node of the economy has its own local technological progress, but the aggregate consequences depend on the web of interdependence through which these improvements propagate.

This is exactly the perspective we developed in our paper from a few years back. The basic idea is simple but, I think, quite profound: technological progress does not act in isolation. It travels through production chains, and the network itself amplifies the effect.

Technology is not only an aggregate shock to “the economy.” It is a local change that propagates through a network, and that network can magnify its effects.

In the paper, we show that longer production chains tend to bias industries toward faster price reduction, and countries toward faster growth. Acemoglu and collaborators had already shown that the depth of production chains is a key determinant of aggregate growth. Our contribution was to show that the depth of production chains also shapes the pace of growth. In other words, the structure of the economy itself can amplify or dampen the effects of technological change. In other words, the depth of the production structure matters. Growth is not just a question of how much innovation exists, but also of how the economy is wired together. Technological change is not just a shock to the aggregate production function, but a local change that propagates through a network of interdependence and that can be amplified. In particular, the more layers of production there are, the more opportunities for amplification, and the faster the pace of growth.

This is one reason I find the language of pace so useful. Once you see an economy as a network, “acceleration” is no longer a metaphor. It becomes something like a propagation phenomenon. Innovations appear locally, but their consequences cascade across suppliers, users, complements, and final demand. The economy speeds up not only because each component improves, but because improvements diffuse through many layers of interdependence.

This also helps explain why older equilibrium pictures can feel unsatisfactory. If technologies are changing at different nodes, and if the effects of those changes propagate with delays, frictions, amplifications, and bottlenecks, then the economy is better understood as an evolving network than as a system sitting quietly near a fixed point.

In that sense, the recent emergence of Macrocosm is interesting not just as a company but as a signal. It suggests that out-of-equilibrium, networked, adaptive economies are increasingly becoming the natural object of analysis rather than an exotic alternative.

And this way of thinking extends far beyond economics. The same logic appears in social systems, in communication systems, in innovation systems, and in institutions. We live inside networks whose topology shapes the speed at which signals travel and the scale at which local changes become collective transformations.

5. Living in nonequilibrium

Physics offers a useful metaphor here. Equilibrium is a regime in which macroscopic observables are stable, fluctuations average out, and the system has enough time to relax. Nonequilibrium is what happens when driving is persistent, relaxation is incomplete, and the system is always in the middle of adjusting to something.

Increasingly, our societies feel like nonequilibrium systems. The next platform arrives before the last one is metabolized. The next regulation arrives before firms have adapted to the previous one. The next expectation arrives before careers, families, schools, or cities can reorganize around it.

The subjective feeling that “everything is going faster” may therefore be more than nostalgia. It may be a real structural property of a society whose forcing term keeps increasing. Technology keeps injecting novelty faster than the social body can dissipate it.

This has consequences. In equilibrium, optimization makes sense. In nonequilibrium, robustness often matters more. In a calm world, efficiency is attractive. In a rapidly driven world, slack, redundancy, and optionality become forms of intelligence.

That is one reason why institutions built for slower centuries often appear confused today. They are not necessarily irrational. They may simply have been designed for a world in which relaxation times were longer than innovation cycles. That world is gone. Or it feels gone. We are now inhabitants of a civilization in which the characteristic pace is set by rather complex networks, automation, and machine-mediated information.

6. AI as a new membrane

Artificial intelligence feels different not because it is “just another tool,” but because it acts on the layer that coordinates many other layers: language, search, classification, synthesis, planning, code, and increasingly decision support.

Every major technological threshold acts like a membrane. Once crossed, the internal tempo of society changes. Printing changed the pace of reproducible thought. Telegraphy changed the pace of long-distance coordination. The internet changed the pace of access. Smartphones changed the pace of daily life. AI may be changing the pace at which cognition itself can be externally scaffolded.

One rough way to picture it is this: AI is like a genie that can produce an effectively unbounded stream of answers. Of course, many of those answers are mediocre, some are wrong, and some are brilliant. But abundance is itself a structural fact. The bottleneck moves away from production of responses and toward judgment, verification, selection, and responsibility.

This matters economically. If the cost of generating drafts, summaries, prototypes, translations, software fragments, marketing variants, research outlines, and strategic scenarios collapses, then the organizations best adapted to the new pace are not the ones that merely “use AI.” They are the ones that can absorb and evaluate accelerated cognitive output without becoming incoherent.

In other words, the scarce resource is no longer always labor in the old sense. More and more often it is attention, trust, institutional bandwidth, and the ability to decide under abundance.

7. How do we cope?

The question I'm asking: is how do we cope? Once we see society this way, the problem becomes harder, but also clearer. Our technologies are embedded in networks that accelerate propagation. Social networks accelerate attention. Production networks accelerate cost changes and structural change. Digital systems accelerate coordination, imitation, contagion, and panic. Meanwhile, human beings still process only a finite amount of information in a finite amount of time.

That asymmetry may be one of the defining tensions of our age. The network can speed up without obvious bound; the human nervous system cannot. Institutions can digitize many processes; judgment remains scarce. Answers can now be generated at enormous scale; understanding cannot.

This is why this AI shift feels so different. It is not simply another machine in the factory. It operates closer to the layer of language, interpretation, synthesis, and decision support. It produces candidate thoughts faster than we can properly evaluate them. In that sense, AI intensifies the basic condition of modernity: abundance on the side of signals, scarcity on the side of attention.

The real question, then, is not whether we can accelerate further. Clearly we can. The real question is how we design societies that remain livable under acceleration. How should firms plan when technological frontiers shift continuously? How should governments regulate when equilibrium is the exception rather than the norm? How should individuals preserve depth, concentration, and responsibility in environments built for perpetual updating?

Planning in such a world cannot look like planning in 1970. In a slower environment, one can often extrapolate from the present. In a faster environment, that is not enough. You do not move toward where the frisbee is now; you move toward where it is going to be. Good planning means reasoning about trajectories, feedback loops, and adaptation speeds. It means thinking dynamically rather than statically.

The central task is no longer just to optimize within a stable world. It is to remain coherent inside a world that keeps changing faster than before.

Perhaps that is the deeper philosophical point. Every major technological threshold changes not only what we can do, but also the temporal structure of life itself. It changes what counts as timely action, what counts as delay, what counts as foresight, and even what counts as a mistake.

We are becoming inhabitants of a civilization whose characteristic pace is increasingly set by networks, automation, and machine-mediated information. The challenge is not merely to innovate. It is to learn how to think, govern, and live inside that accelerating web without losing the human capacity for judgment.

Some final thought

For most of history, people lived inside worlds whose basic tempo changed only slowly. Our world is different. The channels of production are faster, but even more importantly the channels of information are faster. And because information is what coordinates expectation, desire, fear, finance, politics, and action, a faster informational civilization becomes a faster civilization in every other sense.

That may be why the present feels so strange. We are not merely living through technological progress. We are living through a shift in the characteristic timescale of society itself.

We are, in a very literal sense, learning what it means to inhabit a civilization that is permanently out of equilibrium. What are you going to do to cope or adapt?
