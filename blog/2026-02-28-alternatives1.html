The need for more "Indie" Institutes and National labs in Europe

February 28th, 2026

I’ve been quite busy lately—in the good way, the kind of busy where you end up thinking about the plumbing of research as much as the research itself. Also, while it's been quite busy at work, my spare time has been devoted to writing Volume 3 of the upcoming "Practical Statistical Mechanics" book series.

I'm typically quite pensive. I returned to Italy (workwise) after roughly 18 years. I've had the chance to see quite a few research models, and also how academia has changed over this period, in a way that quite honestly I'm not particularly fond of. I see a lot of concentration of resources, maybe in a way that is unjustified, but I digress. Moving between different environments over the years (universities, national labs, independent institutes, and now industry) has made one thing very hard to ignore: the institution you’re in quietly shapes what kinds of problems you can even afford to work on. I keep telling people that it does not matter (as a scientist) whether you're an academic or not, the urge you typically feel as a scientist is to try to solve problems, and if you move out of academia you should be intellectually stimulated. I've been reading the book The Jasons by Ann Finkbeiner (see here) talking about this "secret" group of scientists working for the government, post Manhattan project. There are a lot of stories that resonate with me and it is quite entertaining. This was a "golden age" for science, and it was quite different from the one we have now. The influx of money was quite different, politicians thought that if you want to do great projects, just pour money into it, and the scientists were quite happy to do so. The book is quite good at describing the different projects that these scientists worked on, and how they contributed to the development of science and technology in the US. That model failed around the 70s, and it is quite interesting to see how the different scientists reacted to this change. Some of them left the government, some of them stayed and tried to adapt, and some of them tried to create new institutions to continue doing the kind of work they were doing before. I think that the book is a good read, and it is also a good way to understand how the national labs were born, and how they evolved over time. I will use as a way to segway into how different Europe is in this respect.

Europe produces astonishing science. But whenever the conversation turns from “papers” to platforms—chips, energy systems, AI infrastructure, biomanufacturing, quantum hardware, resilient supply chains—we often talk as if excellent universities plus competitive grants are the whole story.

They are not. And in this post I want to start talking more explicitly about alternative models for doing science: mission-oriented national laboratories, and the ecology of independent institutes that sit outside the standard university pipeline. These institutions are often boring, expensive, and absolutely decisive—especially if what you want is not just discovery, but durable capability. They are somehow in the shadow of bigger institutions, but they are also the places where a lot of the most consequential work happens. They are the places where you can build and maintain shared platforms, run long-horizon programs, and turn use-inspired basic research into something that can actually be used. What I really liked about Los Alamos is that almost nobody was bragging about what he or she was doing, not too much anyway. It also became very "corporate", in the bad sense of the word, which made me loose the magic.

In general, I'm trying to argue that we should be more ambitious. While Italy seem to lack a strategic long term plan, it does not seem to be that the other countries have a coherent vision either, in particular not in a way that is coherent with the "Europe" story. It is a patchwork of ecosystems, and I think it requires a bit of a reorganization and longer term vision. European institutions, I think, can do that, something that governments cannot. We have witnessed a lot of short government cycles, something that used to be the European south now also occurs in the European north: England, Belgium, the Netherland and Germany. I don't mean to ditch the "Human Brain Project" approach, but clearly that project did not work in the sense it had to, and I have not seen a post-mortem. The project ran for 10 years, but aside from producing scientific papers on the subject, it did not produce a platform, or a community, or a training program. They are starting (which is in the end a byproduct of that funding scheme), but these are people-driven and thus. It is a bit of a shame, because the project had a lot of potential, and it was quite well funded. Now, Europe I think is still quite ahead in Neuromorphic computing compared to the US. Yet, companies like Intel and IBM have been investing in this area for a long time, and they have been doing it in a way that is quite different from the way the "Human Brain Project" was doing it. So I want to use this point to try to argue that we do need to think more about the fact that building the car is useless if we don't get it out of the garage. Using another jargon, we should stop ``fucking spiders". (I love Australia.) Here is my thesis.

Thesis: Europe’s research ecosystem is strong at discovery and training, but structurally weaker at (i) sustaining long-horizon, high-fixed-cost programs, (ii) building shared platforms that industry can reliably sit on, and (iii) turning “use-inspired basic research” into durable capability. This is exactly the niche national labs and indipendent institutes were built to occupy.

The university model is starting to crack

This part is personal, so let me start there. I’ve been in and out of academia since 2014—sometimes fully inside it, sometimes just adjacent to it. I returned to Los Alamos for part of my career, and even now I’m “back outside” again, working as a researcher in a private company. In between, I’ve also spent meaningful time (often part-time, sometimes as a visitor) in places that don’t fit the standard university template: Perimeter Institute, the London Institute for Mathematical Sciences, and the Santa Fe Institute.

Moving between these environments does something interesting to your perception. When you’re only in one system, you tend to interpret its pressures as “the natural state of research.” When you step out and come back in, you realize how much of what we call “how science works” is actually “how a specific incentive machine currently works.”

Universities remain remarkable engines of discovery and training—some of the best thinking I’ve ever seen happens in seminar rooms and departmental hallways. But the model is visibly strained in ways that weren’t as pronounced when I first entered it. The first strain is volume: the number of publications keeps rising, and with it the pressure to always be producing. I fail myself at this, and I'm trying to stop. Paper counts, citation metrics, conference acceptances, and grant totals become stand-ins for understanding. If your career is evaluated through proxies, you learn to optimize proxies.

The second strain is the reward model’s tendency to amplify fashion. When funding and hiring reward what looks new, hot, and easily legible to committees, researchers respond rationally: they cluster around trends. Sometimes this accelerates a field; sometimes it produces a kind of coordinated shallowness—fast cycles, thin slices, and too little time spent on slow work: careful theory, rigorous replication, negative results, infrastructure, tooling, or the boring-but-critical act of making a method reliable (which is extremely important).

The third strain is human: the scarcity of permanent positions. A large fraction of research labor is carried by people on short contracts, constantly reapplying for funding and stability. That shapes what problems are “safe” to attempt, how much time you can afford to spend on risky ideas, and even how mentorship works when everyone is living near the edge of uncertainty.

And then there is the publication pipeline itself. Peer review is essential, but it is also increasingly overloaded and oddly structured: an enormous amount of unpaid labor, compressed timelines, fragile norms, and sometimes a sense that the goal becomes “satisfy the process” rather than “improve the science.” Many of us have experienced the doom-loop version of this: long revise-resubmit cycles, conflicting referee demands, and months of latency that do not obviously correlate with better outcomes.

The paradox is that almost everyone can see these incentive mismatches, and yet individually we behave rationally inside them: we publish more because others publish more; we chase fashion because committees reward it; we accept bad equilibria because exiting has a cost. It’s a system that can work locally while failing globally. It seems, in a way, to be the Rene Girard “scapegoat” problem: we all know the system is broken, but we can’t fix it without everyone else fixing it too, and no one wants to be the first to try. The best thing we can do is to "kick out" people that do not fit the system, and this is what we have been doing for a long time. Such system of incentives is also one of Girard's points: that we desire what others desire.. while I am aware of this, I am also aware that it is quite hard to break out of this cycle, and it is also quite hard to change the system. In particular when we are young, want to achieve and be respected.

Some partial conclusion—one we’ll return to later—is simple: the university is not the unique place where research happens, nor the only institutional form through which people can contribute to knowledge. In fact, if we want science to compound into capability, we probably need multiple complementary habitats for research: universities for training and discovery, independent institutes for focused depth, and mission-driven labs (and other long-horizon organizations) for platforms, infrastructure, and sustained programs.

A short origin story: Vannevar Bush and the post-war bargain

To understand why the "DOE lab" model exists, let's start from the beginning. The Manhattan project. In July 1945, Vannevar Bush—then Director of the U.S. Office of Scientific Research and Development—delivered Science, the Endless Frontier, a report to President Truman that shaped modern science policy. It was supposed to be a document that explained why the Manhattan project worked. Bush argued that publicly supported research is a pillar of health, security, and prosperity, and that government should sustain the “upstream” research pipeline in peacetime, not only during emergencies. The report is now canonical; it is also surprisingly readable. I bought it a few years ago and read parts of it (it is a technical report). See the report itself: Bush (1945), PDF, or NSF’s 75th anniversary edition: NSF edition.

The key point for this post is not “basic science good” (everyone says that). It’s the institutional response: large-scale research capacity requires stable organizations with stable funding, able to run big machines, maintain facilities, keep teams together, and pursue missions that outlive a single grant cycle.

In the U.S., the post-war era produced a layered ecosystem: universities, agencies, and a network of national laboratories. In the 1940s–1950s the Atomic Energy Commission built a lab system to host large machines and long-horizon nuclear science; DOE’s historical summary captures that logic well: DOE Office of Science history.

Pasteur’s Quadrant: escaping the “basic vs applied” cartoon

The Bush-era story is often retold as a neat dichotomy: “basic research” in universities, “applied research” in industry. It’s a useful simplification—until it becomes policy dogma.

Donald Stokes’ book Pasteur’s Quadrant (1997) pushed back on this tidy story. Cozmin Ududec once suggested I read it, and I now think of it as a natural “follow up” to Bush’s report—less about why public science matters (Bush’s main task), and more about what kinds of science policy tends to misclassify when it forces everything into “basic” versus “applied.” (Brookings has a clean overview page for the book: Pasteur’s Quadrant.)

The historical backdrop here is important. After WWII, Bush’s framing was enormously influential: protect fundamental research (especially in universities), and trust that applications will flow downstream. That “linear model” was never completely wrong, but over time it hardened into a cartoon: universities do curiosity-driven basics; industry does applications; government funds one end and buys the other. Stokes’ point is that this cartoon misses a huge—and arguably central—region of the landscape: research that is driven by practical needs and simultaneously reaches for fundamental understanding.

Why “Pasteur’s Quadrant”? Because Louis Pasteur is the perfect symbol of this hybrid mode. Pasteur was not doing “basic science” in the modern, detached sense, nor was he doing narrow product development. He was trying to solve concrete societal problems (fermentation, spoilage, disease, vaccination), and in the process he created deep conceptual frameworks—germ theory, microbiology, immunological principles—that reshaped science itself. The use-case was not a distraction from fundamentals; it was the engine that forced new fundamentals into existence.

Stokes proposes a simple 2×2 map with two axes: (i) “Is there a quest for fundamental understanding?” and (ii) “Is there consideration of use?” These two questions generate four quadrants:

  • Bohr’s Quadrant (high understanding, low use): pure, curiosity-driven basic research—the emblem is Niels Bohr. The goal is to understand, and any applications are incidental or far downstream.
  • Edison’s Quadrant (low understanding, high use): pure applied research / invention—the emblem is Thomas Edison. The goal is to make something work; deep theory is optional.
  • Pasteur’s Quadrant (high understanding, high use): use-inspired basic research. Practical constraints and societal needs guide the questions, but the answers are fundamental and general.
  • (The fourth quadrant) (low understanding, low use): work that is neither seeking deep understanding nor directly driven by use—often cataloging, routine measurement, or activity that can be valuable in context but isn’t the archetype of discovery or innovation. (Stokes’ point is not to shame it; it’s to show it’s a different mode.)

The policy punchline is that the “basic vs applied” dichotomy collapses Bohr and Pasteur into one bucket, and Edison into another, then pretends the buckets cover reality. But Pasteur’s Quadrant behaves differently from Bohr’s in terms of time horizons, infrastructure, evaluation, and institutional needs. Use-inspired basic research often requires stable platforms, long-lived teams, and feedback loops with real-world constraints. It is simultaneously harder to do in a pure grant-and-paper economy and more likely to produce the kind of compounding capability societies care about.

This is exactly why national labs, independent institutes (and companies!) matter in the story I’m telling here: they are institutional forms that can hold Pasteur’s Quadrant work without forcing it to pretend it is either “pure basic” or “pure applied.” They make it legitimate to say: we are building understanding because we are trying to build something that must work. I think that the US has a good foundation in this respect, philosophically grounded on Pearce's pragmatism.

If you buy Stokes’ framing, you immediately see the institutional gap: use-inspired basic research needs places where (i) the “use” is real and persistent (missions), and (ii) the “basic” is not punished for being long-horizon. National labs are one way to hold that tension productively.

What a national lab is (and isn’t)

A national lab is not “a university but bigger,” and it’s not “an industrial R&D department owned by the state.” In the best case, it’s a platform institution: a place designed to do work that is simultaneously scientifically deep and operationally heavy—work that needs long time horizons, stable teams, and expensive infrastructure.

This is one of the key differences with companies. A company can do extraordinary R&D, but it is ultimately constrained by product cycles, market timing, and the fact that research is justified by a business model that must close. Even in “deep tech,” patience is a scarce resource. National labs—DOE labs are the canonical example—can hold a problem for longer than a typical corporate horizon, and for longer than most grant cycles, because the mission is not a quarter and not a paper: it’s capability. This requires also investment in "pure" science, or basic research, and for instance in Los Alamos this was 6 percent of the whole budget. We are talking about a billion, and a lab that is between 15 and 18 thousand employees.

Said differently: companies optimize for competitive advantage; universities optimize for discovery and training. National labs are one of the few institutional forms that can optimize for national-scale infrastructure and long-lived missions.

Practically, that means a lab can afford to do the “unsexy middle” that society still needs: building testbeds, maintaining facilities, running validation campaigns, keeping expert engineers on staff, writing the documentation, and doing the QA/safety/security work that turns clever prototypes into systems you can trust. It’s not that these activities are intellectually dull—often they are incredibly challenging—but they rarely map cleanly onto the academic reward model, and they’re hard to justify inside a company unless the path to product is immediate.

  • Runs shared, high-fixed-cost infrastructure (cleanrooms, accelerators, HPC, metrology labs, grid and materials testbeds, field sites) that would be inefficient to duplicate across universities or firms.
  • Keeps long-lived engineering and science teams that can iterate hardware, processes, and software stacks over years (and keep institutional memory when people cycle through).
  • Turns prototypes into reproducible capability: reference implementations, standards, calibration pipelines, validated measurement and simulation workflows—basically, the part where “a demo” becomes “a platform.”
  • Bridges sectors: universities ↔ industry ↔ government, with collaboration rules and facilities access baked in, so the interface isn’t reinvented every time.
  • Holds missions that are cross-cutting, long-horizon, or politically important but not immediately profitable: energy resilience, nuclear stewardship, critical materials, advanced manufacturing, large-scale scientific computing.

This mission layer also gives governments a unique lever: the ability to pivot funding when the world pivots. When geopolitical or strategic priorities shift—energy shocks, supply chain fragility, security needs, compute constraints—labs let governments redirect effort quickly without starting from zero, because the infrastructure and talent base already exist. That pivot is not “random industrial policy”; at its best it stays grounded in rigorous scientific work while aiming at outcomes that matter at national (and continental) scale.

And this is where it helps to zoom out beyond any single agency: government-backed research ecosystems have repeatedly underwritten platform technologies that later became “obvious” infrastructure—navigation systems like GPS (DoD), foundational computing and networking, materials and fabrication advances, and entire measurement and standards stacks. The point isn’t credit assignment; it’s the pattern: platforms tend to come from institutions that can invest early, sustain long development arcs, and absorb uncertainty. While it is true that "modern" internet was invented for CERN.. do you think that CERN is the only place where the internet was invented? No, it is a network of labs, universities and companies that contributed to the development of the internet.

The “boring” parts matter: procurement, QA, safety, security, uptime, documentation, and institutional memory. Scientific revolutions are often enabled by reliable plumbing—and national labs are one of the places where that plumbing can be built without apologizing for not looking like a startup or a department. Afterall, the arXiv was invented and hosted on a Sun workstation in an office not far from where mine was at LANL, and it was not a startup, but it was a platform that enabled a lot of science to happen (from a string theorist, Paul Ginsparg).

Europe today: excellence, fragmentation, and the “valley of platforms”

Europe already has world-class intergovernmental infrastructures—CERN is the obvious flagship—and it also has a surprisingly rich landscape of national research organizations. Depending on where you look you run into different institutional species: France has CNRS (and mission-oriented entities like CEA), Italy has CNR (and ENEA in the energy space), Germany has the Max Planck Society and Fraunhofer, and other countries have their own equivalents and strong applied institutes. In other words: the “ingredients” exist. Yet, we miss a way to unify all of these, or a new layer that can connect them into a more coherent ecosystem. Under the same banner, while keeping them independent. Afterall, the European Commission has been trying to do this with the European Research Area, but it is still quite fragmented and it is not clear how to connect all these different institutions together.

The issue, at least from where I’m standing, is not a lack of talent or excellence. It’s coordination at continental scale. The continent-wide picture is still fragmented along borders, ministries, funding instruments, and administrative cultures. That fragmentation is not just a governance annoyance—it shapes what kinds of technical work are feasible. It makes it harder to build shared platforms, to sustain long-horizon programs, and to keep expensive infrastructure open and interoperable across the places that could use it most.

Here is the pattern I keep seeing:

  • We fund discovery well.
  • We fund “projects” well.
  • We underfund the platform phase: building and operating shared capabilities that many projects depend on.

This is why I like the phrase “valley of platforms.” It’s adjacent to the usual “valley of death,” but it’s not the same thing. You can have brilliant science and even good startups, and still lack stable testbeds, pilot lines, validation frameworks, procurement pathways, and compute/measurement infrastructure that make an ecosystem compound rather than reset every cycle.

And here’s the slightly provocative part: Europe is increasingly a single “big nation” in the only sense that matters for technology platforms—shared markets, shared standards, shared security concerns, shared energy constraints—while still retaining all its internal differences (which are real, and often valuable). If that is the world we live in, then it’s odd that we still behave as if platform-building should be solved country-by-country, duplicating some things and leaving other things undone.

My intuition is that the sensible move is not to erase national strengths, but to orchestrate them: take the best of what each country already does well—Germany’s applied-institute machinery, France’s large mission organizations, Italy’s networks and domain expertise, and so on—and deliberately connect them into an interoperable, long-horizon system. Not “one super-lab in Brussels,” but a coherent network with shared roadmaps, shared access rules, and shared platform missions.

Independent institutes: the agile fringe that universities can’t easily host

National labs are one complementary habitat for research, but they’re not the only one. There’s another species that matters a lot, especially for ideas that are too weird, too early, too cross-disciplinary, or simply too “non-conforming” to fit comfortably inside a department: independent institutes.

By “independent” I mean places that are intentionally smaller and more agile—institutions that don’t try to replicate the full university stack (teaching, degree programs, departmental politics, committee ecosystems), and that would probably be killed by that machinery if you forced them into it. They often sit at the fringe: not because the work is marginal in quality, but because it’s marginal in fit. Let's call them “indie institutes” for short (inspired by "indie" movie film festivals). They are often funded by a mix of philanthropy, government grants, and sometimes industry partnerships, and they tend to have more flexible organizational structures that allow them to pursue unconventional research agendas.

I’ve spent time in a few of these worlds—Perimeter Institute, the Santa Fe Institute, and the London Institute for Mathematical Sciences. What struck me, especially early on, is that these places can feel almost “unprofessional” in the best sense: more like a workshop than an institution. The social fabric matters as much as the formal structure. If the right people are there, the environment becomes unusually free: fewer ritual obligations, fewer performative outputs, more time for slow conversations, and more permission to follow an idea before it has a grant-shaped justification.

Perimeter is a good example. Before it became more mainstream and more legible to the outside world, it had a genuinely funky energy— the kind of place that would have been hard to explain to a conventional hiring committee, and even harder to reproduce inside a standard department. Santa Fe has its own version of this: an institute built around ideas that are fundamentally interdisciplinary, where the “unit of work” is often a conversation between people who don’t share a vocabulary yet.

And then there was LIMS - which I love to this day! When I was there as a postdoc there it felt like a small ship floating next to the academic continent: you could be free in a very direct way. Funding was a problem then, but the freedom was real. I owe this place a lot, and I think it’s a model worth thinking about. It was a place where you could do work that was too weird for a department, but still be around people who cared about deep questions. We had evening seminars where we would talk about anything, and we had a lot of fun. We had a lot of freedom to do what we wanted, and we had a lot of support from the institute. We had beers. That’s not a policy recommendation, it’s just a signal that the place optimized for thinking rather than appearances.

Independent institutes are the fringe in the ecological sense: they explore territory that larger, more stable organisms can’t easily explore. If you force them to behave like universities, you lose the very property that makes them useful and beautiful. And agile. In a way, it is what makes the Cambridge and Oxford colleges so special (where I spent a bit of my post-PhD time): they are not universities, but they are part of the university ecosystem. They are places where you can do work that would be hard to do in a department, and they have a different culture and different incentives.

Of course, success changes everything. If an institute works, it attracts attention, money, expectations, committees, and rules. Some amount of structure is necessary—sustainability is not optional. But there’s a real tradeoff: the more you professionalize, the more you risk sanding off the weird edges that made the place generative in the first place.

Still, when they work, these places are unusually nice environments to do research in—provided the right people are there, and provided the institute protects its core ingredient: low bureaucracy, high trust, and permission to be early. In the broader argument of this post, they matter because they remind us that “doing science” is not a single institutional template. Universities, national labs, industry, and independent institutes each occupy a different niche—and the ecosystem is healthier when all of them exist.

Why now: the fast world needs many paces

The main reason I think this conversation matters now is not a single crisis, or a single technology, or a single strategic sector. It’s something more structural: the modern world is running on multiple time scales at once, and our research institutions are still trying to pretend that one pace can serve them all.

Some problems want sprint speed: rapid prototyping, fast feedback loops, an appetite for failure, a culture that ships. Some problems want decade speed: slow accumulation of understanding, careful measurement, building instruments, maintaining datasets, refining theory, preserving craft. And some problems want something even slower than that: interpretation, historical perspective, ethics, meaning—the parts of knowledge that resist becoming a dashboard metric.

The mistake is to treat “science” as a single pipeline with a single output function. We’ve built a world where output is too often synonymous with countable output: papers, citations, grants, patents, benchmarks, spinouts. That creates a managerial illusion: if you can count it, you can manage it; if you can’t count it, it must not matter.

This is unfair to many kinds of work—but it is especially unfair to the humanities, which keep getting the short stick precisely because their most valuable outputs are not cleanly quantifiable. You can’t measure “a new way of seeing” with the same ruler you use for conference proceedings.

AI is a good example of why the “single pace” model breaks. It didn’t arrive because a committee wrote “AI transition” into a five-year plan. It arrived because multiple ecosystems interacted: universities generated ideas and talent, industry provided scale and iteration speed, open-source communities created a shared substrate, and governments woke up late to the fact that compute, models, and data are now infrastructural. It was organic, messy, and it moved faster than institutional reflexes.

The question “what’s next?” is exactly the point: we don’t know. And we shouldn’t pretend we can fully predict it. The best response is not a monolithic institute that tries to steer everything, but an ecology that can generate and absorb surprises: places that run at different paces, with different incentive structures, and different definitions of success.

  • Universities are still unmatched for training, breadth, and discovery—but they struggle when we overload them with platform-building and short-horizon productivity pressure.
  • National labs (or mission-oriented institutes) are good at long-horizon platforms, infrastructure, standards, and sustained teams—work that makes other work possible.
  • Independent institutes can be the agile fringe: small, weird, fast-moving, low-bureaucracy environments where ideas cross-pollinate before they become “fields.”
  • Industry is exceptional at iteration speed and deployment when incentives align—and at building products that stress-test ideas at scale.

Notice what this framing does: it shifts the goal from “direct outcomes” to “fertile conditions.” Instead of trying to micromanage the future, you build institutions that can host it. You make room for long projects that do not pay off on the timescale of a grant. You keep a place for the weird early ideas. You preserve domains where meaning and interpretation matter as much as optimization.

So my conclusion here is not “Europe should pick three strategic pillars.” It’s that Europe should build (and connect) a set of research habitats with different paces—and then allow priorities to emerge organically from that ecology. The future will not be polite enough to arrive on a timeline we can manage with KPIs.

Conclusion: an institutional ecology, not a single reform

If there is one policy-shaped conclusion I want to land, it’s this: Europe doesn’t need one perfect institution. It needs an ecology of institutions that run at different paces, reward different kinds of value, and can collaborate without constantly reinventing the interface. Universities are indispensable—but they cannot be the only place where knowledge work lives, and they should not be forced to carry every function the modern world demands.

So “build European national labs” is not quite the right slogan. The better slogan is: build a European platform layer—and connect it to universities, industry, and independent institutes in a way that preserves what each does best.

This is also, quietly, a values statement: output is not everything, and what we can easily count is not the same as what matters. A healthy research system must make room for work whose value shows up as capability, as craft, as interpretation, as cultural memory— and yes, sometimes as a paper.

Policy recommendation: principles that scale without becoming a bureaucracy cathedral

If Europe wants a platform layer, it probably shouldn’t copy-paste a single-country model. The scale is continental, governance is plural, and Europe’s strength is diversity. The design goal should be coordination without uniformity. Here is a sketch that feels realistic to me:

  • Networked, not monolithic: a federation of national labs and mission-oriented institutes with shared roadmaps and interoperability requirements, not one mega-agency trying to centralize everything.
  • Build shared platforms, not only projects: fund testbeds, pilot lines, metrology, long-lived datasets, open benchmarks, reference implementations, and “boring infrastructure” with explicit multi-year operating budgets (not just capex).
  • Measure what platforms do: uptime, access, throughput, validated capability, reproducibility, and the number of external teams enabled—not just publications. (A platform that no one can use is not a platform.)
  • Porous boundaries by default: joint appointments, rotating “engineer-in-residence” programs, visiting scientist tracks, and lightweight industrial residencies that are normal—not exceptional.
  • Protect the agile fringe: carve out a small but real funding stream for independent institutes and weird cross-disciplinary spaces, with governance designed to keep bureaucracy low. These places are often where “what’s next?” incubates.
  • Procurement and IP that enable collaboration: make it easy to build with industry without turning labs into vendors; treat standards, reference designs, and open tooling as legitimate outputs.
  • Training as a first-class output: create stable technical career tracks (staff scientists/engineers) and apprenticeship-style programs that produce people who can operate and improve platforms—not only write papers about them.
  • Mission clarity, but humble steering: governments should set broad missions and fund capacity, then let priorities evolve as reality changes. The goal is to create fertile conditions for breakthroughs, not to micromanage which breakthrough happens.

Europe already knows how to do parts of this—shared infrastructures, cross-border facilities, serious scientific governance. The opportunity is to extend that competence from “big science instruments” to “big technology platforms,” while keeping the system plural, agile at the edges, and humane in what it rewards.

Some (loosely kept together) Takeaways

  • Vannevar Bush’s 1945 bargain was not just “fund science,” but “build institutions that sustain capability” (Bush/NSF edition).
  • Stokes’ Pasteur’s Quadrant reminds us that the most powerful work is often use-inspired and fundamental—and it needs institutional homes beyond the standard university incentive machine (Brookings page).
  • Europe’s excellence is real, but fragmentation makes platform-building harder than it needs to be.
  • The right target is an ecosystem: networked mission-oriented labs + protected independent institutes + strong universities + productive industry interfaces.

I’ll likely follow this with something more concrete: what “platform missions” make sense for Europe in 2026, what could be funded at EU level versus national level, and how to do it without building a new cathedral of paperwork.
