From the Demographic Boom to the Demographic Decline

July 15th, 2026

From kindergartens to universities, demographic change behaves like a slow tidal wave. It starts almost invisibly, then arrives everywhere at once.

The day Mel Brooks dies we will lose a great person, one of the last representatives of a cultural world that spanned vaudeville, Hollywood, television, satire, and the twentieth century itself. But his joke also gives a useful image for thinking about aging societies. Europe feels young in its institutions. Its universities feel permanent. Its ministries, faculties, and degree programs feel as if they have always existed and therefore will always exist. Yet many of these institutions were scaled for a demographic world that no longer exists.

It was inspired by quite a few conversations I had with some friends and colleagues over the last few weeks and months, and that it was also inspired by a 2 hour documentary on the demographic decline across the world since the '70s (the documentary is worthy, it's on YouTube and it's called Birthgap). "The algorithm" suggested it to me after I watched something else (I don't recall exactly who it was), but some youtubers essentially visiting Japan, in remote areas, and visiting old kindergarten. Actually, many of these small towns were often inhabited by only one person. Now, this to me is not new. I'm from an area of Italy called Calabria. If you heard "Houses for 1 euro in Italy" once, with high probability it was in one of the towns of Calabria. The reason is that many of these towns are now inhabited by only a few people, and the young ones have moved to the cities or abroad. Sometime the areas became too difficult to reach or inaccessible, and a new town was built nearby. A famous example of one of these towns is Pentedattilo here, which is now almost ghost town and that had been inhabited since the Greeks (and gave the name to the town after "five fingers" in greek). Now you see that there are two phenomena that essentially make ghost towns. One is demographic decline, which happens everywhere, the second is emigration. Some friends of mine might have heard me saying this for a few years, but essentially the demographic decline is like a slow tsunami. The tsunamy hits first kindegarten. Then elementary schools, etc etc, till it reaches, after 18 years, universities. The problem is that the demographic decline is not uniform across the world, and it is not uniform across countries. Some countries are more affected than others, and some regions are more affected than others. I wanted to gather some data and make these thoughts a bit more quantitative.. here we go.

1. The demographic tsunami

The reason I like the image of the tsunami is that it captures both the slowness and the violence of the process. A tsunami is not a normal wave. When it is far away, in the open ocean, it can be almost invisible. It does not look dramatic. But the energy is there, moving underneath. Only when it reaches the shore does it become obvious. In a sense, demographic decline has exactly this character. It begins with births, but births are not immediately visible in the institutions we usually care about. A country can have fewer children for many years and still have full universities, because the students sitting in lecture halls were born eighteen or twenty years earlier. The university system is therefore always looking into the past. It is fed by decisions, births, migrations, and family structures that happened almost two decades before.

This is the key lag. If fewer children are born in year $t$, then the effect first appears in kindergartens a few years later, then in elementary schools, then in middle schools, then in high schools, and only after about eighteen years in universities. It is not that the system has no warning. It has plenty of warning. The problem is that the warning appears in another part of the system.

$$ \text{Births}_{t} \longrightarrow \text{Kindergarten}_{t+3} \longrightarrow \text{Primary school}_{t+6} \longrightarrow \text{High school}_{t+14} \longrightarrow \text{University}_{t+18}. $$

In this sense, the closure of a kindergarten is not only a local story. It is a prediction about the future of the university system. A kindergarten closing today is, in some sense, a university department becoming fragile eighteen years from now. The same cohort is moving through time.

This is also why I think the demographic story is easy to underestimate. We usually observe institutions in cross-section: this school is open, that university is hiring, this department has students, that city is still alive. But demography is not a cross-section. It is like a conveyor belt, in a way. The age pyramid is not only a picture of society today; it is a timetable of what will arrive later.

2. What I am actually modelling here

Before going further, I should be honest about what these plots are and what they are not. They are not an official forecast. They are not a ministry model. They are not even, yet, a properly calibrated demographic projection. They are a first attempt to make a qualitative intuition quantitative enough that one can argue with it.

The inputs I used are deliberately coarse. For Italy, the relevant empirical fact is the large fall in births from the late 2000s to the present. I used an Italy-like birth-cohort curve, with relatively high cohorts around 2008 and much smaller cohorts in the 2020s. I then propagated these cohorts forward into university-entry years. This gives the basic demographic pressure.

The plot above is not meant to be a forecast in the strict sense, but it sort of represents the mechanism. Italy is projected to be around 50 million people in 2050, down from about 60 million today. The age pyramid is projected to be very top-heavy, with a small base of young people and a large share of older people. This is what this is meant to represent. The blue curve is the age-18 cohort, which is the cleanest proxy for new university entrants. The orange curve is a weighted 18-24 pool, which better represents the students who are already inside the system. The difference between the two matters. Universities do not feel the full shock the moment one small cohort arrives. They feel it when several smaller cohorts are simultaneously moving through the system.

Then I added a second ingredient: students do not all choose the same subjects. The model therefore assigns broad field shares to social sciences/law/economics, STEM, health, and humanities/arts/education. For the first run, I used broad current-style shares, roughly:

  • Social sciences, law, and economics: about 35%;
  • STEM: about 29%;
  • Health: about 18%;
  • Humanities, arts, and education: about 18%.

These numbers should be treated as scaffolding. The right version of this exercise should use official enrollment data by year, region, discipline, gender, and ideally degree class. One should then estimate how field shares have actually changed over the last twenty or thirty years, rather than assuming a stylized drift.

I also added synthetic departments. Each department has a baseline size, a field, and a threshold below which it becomes “stressed.” This is again a simplification. In the real world, a department does not close simply because a line in a model crosses a threshold. It can be protected by politics, prestige, research income, medical-school status, national strategic interest, or simply institutional inertia. Conversely, it can be vulnerable because it is small, regional, expensive, or administratively unlucky.

So the point of the plots is not that the numbers are exact, but that the modeling is plausible. Demography pushes down numbers, and in addition we account for field preferences. Thresholds turn smooth decline into abrupt institutional change. These thresholds are strongly nonlinear. A department can lose students for years and still remain open, but once it crosses a threshold, it may close quickly.

In other words, I am not trying to say: “this department will close in this year.” I am trying to develop a language for a system in which cohort decline, student choice, institutional thresholds, and migration interact.

3. Why universities are hit late

Universities are strange institutions because they are both very old and very dependent on a very narrow age window. A university can have medieval buildings, ancient ceremonies, and departments that feel permanent, but its financial and pedagogical life depends every year on a new inflow of young people. In the 60s and 70s, the demographic boom created a large inflow of students. Universities expanded because there were many young people entering university age, and first generations of college students as well. The post war boom saw a dramatic convergence of the two phenomena. New departments were created. New campuses were built.

Back to the modelling.. A useful first approximation is to define a potential university pool $S_t$ as a weighted sum of the population between ages 18 and 24:

$$ S_t = \sum_{a=18}^{24} w_a P_{a,t}, \qquad \sum_{a=18}^{24} w_a = 1. $$

Here $P_{a,t}$ is the population of age $a$ in year $t$, and the weights $w_a$ represent how important that age is for university enrollment. In practice, one would give more weight to 18, 19, 20, and 21, and less to 23 or 24, although master programs and delayed graduation make the older ages relevant too.

This already tells us something important. Fertility alone is not enough. Fertility tells us where the system is going in the long run, but the age distribution tells us when the shock arrives. A country can have very low fertility today, but universities may still be full if the cohorts born eighteen to twenty-four years ago were large. Conversely, a university system can suddenly feel fragile even if current fertility has stabilized, because the small cohorts born years earlier have finally arrived at university age.

This is why demographic debates are often mistimed. By the time the university sector notices the problem, the relevant births are already historical facts. There is no policy that can create eighteen-year-olds next year. Immigration can help, international students can help, adult education can help, and participation rates can rise. But the missing cohort itself cannot be invented.

The possible offsets are important, though. One could imagine more international students, more adult learners, more students returning for retraining, or more students choosing university rather than directly entering work. These effects may delay the shock. In some countries, they may be large. But in regions that are not especially attractive to international students, or that are already losing young people internally, I would not assume that they are enough.

4. A toy model: cohorts, choices, departments

The model has three layers. First, there is the demographic pool $S_t$, which is the number of potential university students. Second, students choose fields. Third, departments receive enrollments and may become non-viable if they fall below a threshold.

If $p_{f,t}$ is the probability that a student chooses field $f$ in year $t$, then the field-level enrollment is:

$$ N_{f,t} = S_t p_{f,t}. $$

Relative to a baseline year, say 2026, the useful object is the field enrollment index:

$$ I_{f,t} = \frac{S_t}{S_{2026}} \frac{p_{f,t}}{p_{f,2026}}. $$

This equation is simple, but it contains the whole story. The first factor is demography. It tells us whether there are fewer students overall. The second factor is preference. It tells us whether students are moving toward or away from a given field. A field can decline faster than the population if its share falls. A field can decline more slowly, or even grow, if its share rises.

This is where the assumptions become shaky, and it is good that they are visible. I assumed that humanities lose share, social sciences lose somewhat less, and STEM and health gain share. This seems plausible to me, but it should be estimated, not assumed. The correct way to do this would be to collect the time series of Italian enrollments by discipline and fit the change in $p_{f,t}$ directly.

Even then, extrapolation would be dangerous. Student preferences are not physical constants. They respond to wages, family expectations, cultural changes, labor-market anxiety, public narratives, AI, tuition, local availability, and immigration policy. A large technological shock can change field shares. A recession can change field shares. A new professional licensing rule can change field shares. So this is a model of a mechanism, not an oracle.

The useful thing in the plot is the relative movement. In this toy model, humanities and social sciences become fragile earlier, while STEM and health are partially protected by changing preferences. I would not read the exact dates too literally. What I would read literally is the fact that a demographic shock can be very unequal across fields.

5. Why not all faculties decline in the same way

The next layer of the model is departments. Each field has many departments, and each department has a baseline size and a viability threshold.

This is where many naive discussions of demographic decline can go wrong. They implicitly imagine that if there are 30% fewer students, then every department loses 30%. But students do not redistribute like an ideal gas.

A student choosing between economics and law may be somewhat mobile across those two choices. A student choosing between medicine and literature is probably not. A student who wants to stay close to home may choose the best available local option. A student willing to migrate may choose a stronger university in another region. This is a strong factor for instance for southern regions like the one I come from. This means that demographic decline is filtered through preferences and geography. This part of the modelling will always be wrnog.

The violin plots below are therefore not “data” in the strict sense. They are simulated distributions of departments, generated to show how the spread can widen as the demographic shock propagates. Some departments remain above threshold; others fall below. This lower tail is what matters institutionally.

I like the violin plot here because it shows something that an average hides. The average field can look acceptable while the lower tail is already in trouble. This is probably how the real process will look. Not a dramatic national announcement that “the humanities have collapsed,” but a sequence of local events. One degree program is suspended. One campus merges. One small department stops hiring. One master program is no longer offered. Then another.

6. Thresholds, closures, and institutional phase transitions

Departments do not close smoothly. This is another reason why the problem is hard to perceive. A department can lose students for years and still remain open. It can compensate by increasing teaching loads, not replacing retirements, merging courses, relying on temporary contracts, or cross-listing programs with other departments.

In other words, institutions absorb stress. Until they cannot.

A simple way to model this is to assign each department a viability threshold $\theta_d$. The department becomes stressed when its enrollment falls below that threshold:

$$ \text{department } d \text{ is stressed at time } t \quad \text{if} \quad \frac{N_{d,t}}{N_{d,2026}} < \theta_d. $$

The threshold is not the same for every department. A large department can survive a large relative decline. A small department may not. A laboratory-heavy department has different costs from a lecture-based department. A politically protected or nationally strategic discipline has a different threshold again. But the important point is that thresholds create nonlinear behavior.

This is where the model becomes suggestive. If many departments have thresholds between, say, 60% and 75% of their original enrollment, then very little happens while the system remains above that band. But once the demographic curve enters that band, many departments become stressed at nearly the same time. The transition looks sudden even though the underlying demographic decline was slow.

This is the part that, as a physicist, is hard not to see as a kind of phase transition. For a while, the system changes continuously. Then a threshold is crossed and the macroscopic state of the system changes. Before the transition, the university system has many duplicated local programs. After the transition, it has fewer, more concentrated programs. The microscopic cause is just fewer students. The macroscopic outcome is a reorganization of the institutional landscape.

Of course, universities are not physical systems. They have politics, histories, unions, ministries, cities, reputations, and people. But thresholds are thresholds. If a course has too few students, if a department cannot hire, if a campus cannot justify its costs, the language may be administrative, but the underlying structure is nonlinear.

7. Geography: the migration feedback loop

The next complication is geography. And in Italy, geography is not a detail. It is probably central.

A national demographic decline does not imply a uniform regional decline. Some regions can continue to attract students even while the country as a whole shrinks. Others can lose students much faster than the national average. This is because universities are not only affected by demography; they also affect migration.

Imagine a small or medium-sized university in a region already losing young people. The local birth cohorts decline. Enrollment falls. A few programs become fragile. The university closes or merges some of them. Now a young person who would have studied locally must go elsewhere: perhaps Rome, Bologna, Milan, Turin, Padua, Florence, Pisa, or another larger center.

Once that student leaves, the probability that they return is not one. They form friendships elsewhere. They find internships elsewhere. They enter labor markets elsewhere. They may start a family elsewhere. The region has not only lost a student. It may have lost a future graduate, a future worker, and a future parent.

So the feedback loop is:

$$ \text{program closure} \longrightarrow \text{student out-migration} \longrightarrow \text{graduate non-return} \longrightarrow \text{lower local young-adult population} \longrightarrow \text{fewer future births} \longrightarrow \text{further enrollment decline}. $$

This is why I think university decline should be studied together with internal migration. In a very simple regional model one would write:

$$ Y_{r,t+1} = Y_{r,t} + B_{r,t} - D_{r,t} + M_{r,t}, $$

where $Y_{r,t}$ is the young population in region $r$, $B_{r,t}$ are births, $D_{r,t}$ are exits, and $M_{r,t}$ is net migration. The key is that $M_{r,t}$ is not external. It depends on university capacity, labor markets, and urban attractiveness:

$$ M_{r,t} = F\!\left( \text{university capacity}_{r,t}, \text{jobs}_{r,t}, \text{urban attractiveness}_{r,t} \right). $$

This makes the system recursive. A weaker university system can increase out-migration, and out-migration can weaken the future university system. This is the mechanism that can turn demographic decline into regional decline.

This map is only schematic, but it represents the mechanism I have in mind. The national number may say “30% fewer students.” But the local number may say something much more dramatic. A strong university city may lose very little, or even gain, because it attracts students from weaker regions. A peripheral region may lose far more than the national average because demography and migration point in the same direction.

This is why I suspect that spatial concentration may matter as much as fertility itself. The future of universities will not be decided only by how many young people Italy has. It will also be decided by where those young people are willing, or forced, to go.

8. The second and third waves: economics and AI

Demography is not the only force acting on universities. It is simply the most mechanical one. There are at least two other waves arriving at the same time: economic uncertainty and artificial intelligence.

Economic uncertainty matters because fertility is not only a cultural variable. It is also tied to housing, income, work stability, childcare, and expectations about the future. Economic uncertainty hits twice: it hits the students who might decide to delay going to university, and it hits the parents who might decide to delay having children. If young adults do not feel that they can afford children, they delay or avoid having them. This is true in Italy, but it is not only an Italian story. The United States, which for a long time looked demographically more resilient than Europe, has also seen low fertility. Recent provisional U.S. data show only small fluctuations around low levels rather than a return to replacement fertility. China is an even more dramatic case: births have fallen sharply, and the country is now facing the consequences of a very fast demographic transition.

This matters for universities because economic anxiety can worsen the same mechanism I have described above. Fewer children today means fewer students tomorrow. But economic uncertainty also affects field choices today. Families may push students toward degrees perceived as safer. Students may avoid long academic paths. Governments may protect professional degrees and cut fields seen as less directly connected to employment. This means that a fertility shock and an economic shock can reinforce each other.

The third wave is AI, which is happening now. I am terribly afraid of it, but I do not think anyone knows exactly what AI will do to universities. But it would be strange to assume it will do nothing. It may reduce demand for certain kinds of degrees. It may increase demand for others. It may change how students learn, how exams are designed, how universities certify knowledge, and how much families are willing to pay for traditional instruction. It may also change the labor market that students are trying to enter.

For universities this is dangerous because AI does not need to destroy the entire system to have a large effect. It only needs to change marginal demand, marginal costs, and marginal credibility. If demographic decline has already pushed some departments close to threshold, AI-driven changes in demand or teaching could push them over.

We are already seeing signs that the old idea of the permanent academic job is less permanent than many people believed. In the United Kingdom, for instance, Nottingham has faced strong criticism over proposed cuts in Physics and Astronomy, with plans reported to reduce the department from 71 to 51 staff. Exeter has also been reported to be consulting on significant job cuts, with humanities and social sciences particularly exposed. These cases have their own local causes, including the financial structure of UK universities and the post-Brexit environment, so they should not be treated as simple demographic examples. But they are useful signals. They show that even STEM and even established universities are not immune to institutional contraction.

In this sense, the university system is being squeezed from three sides. Demography reduces the number of future students. Economic uncertainty changes fertility and student choices. AI changes the value proposition of higher education itself. Any one of these would be difficult. Together, they make the next twenty years unusually uncertain.

9. A note on academic careers

There is an uncomfortable personal implication here. If one is entering the university system today at thirty years of age, and wants to remain there for more than twenty years, one is not entering a stationary system. One is entering a system that may be structurally smaller by the time one reaches fifty.

This does not mean that nobody should become an academic. That would be too strong, and also not what I believe. There will still be excellent universities, excellent departments, and excellent careers. But the risk profile is changing. The assumption that a permanent academic position is a kind of insulated life contract is becoming less convincing.

This is particularly true for fields and regions exposed to all three forces: demographic decline, weak local economies, and technological substitution. A young academic in a growing field, at a strong institution, in a region attracting students, may be fine. A young academic in a shrinking field, at a financially fragile institution, in a region losing young people, faces a very different distribution of outcomes.

One way to say this is that academic careers have become more path-dependent. The institution, field, and geography matter more than before. The average university may survive, but the variance between universities may increase. Some places will become stronger by absorbing students and faculty from weaker places. Others will quietly hollow out.

For someone planning a career, this means that the question is not only “can I get a permanent job?” but also “what will this institution, this region, and this field look like in twenty years?” That is a much harder question, and not one that the academic labor market is very honest about.

10. What this probably means

So what should one expect? We just tried to model (a very) complex system. I do not think the most likely scenario is that many universities suddenly disappear. Institutions are good at surviving. They merge, rename, reorganize, and stretch themselves. The first effect will probably be less dramatic and more pervasive: fewer programs, fewer local options, fewer small departments, fewer duplicated degrees, and more concentration.

The order of events will probably be something like this. First, small degree programs become difficult to sustain. Then departments merge. Then campuses specialize. Then some local branches become hard to justify. Then students increasingly move toward larger universities. Then the stronger universities become stronger because they attract the remaining demand, while weaker institutions lose not only students but also reputation and political leverage.

This process can be managed intelligently, or it can be allowed to happen through failure. If it is managed intelligently, one can decide which subjects should remain geographically accessible, which should be concentrated nationally, and how to preserve disciplines that are culturally important even if their student demand falls. If it is not managed, the system will still consolidate, but through local crises and emergency decisions.

There is also a possible optimistic side. A smaller student population does not automatically imply a worse university system. If resources are concentrated, if teaching improves, if universities specialize, and if the surviving programs are strengthened, the average quality could rise. A country with fewer students does not need to maintain exactly the same institutional map. But this requires planning, and planning demographic decline is politically difficult because it means admitting that some inherited structures cannot all be preserved.

The more pessimistic scenario is that institutions deny the problem for too long. They protect every existing structure until the numbers force abrupt changes. In that case, consolidation still happens, but without design. It happens through retirements not replaced, courses cancelled at the last minute, local political fights, and departments slowly becoming empty shells.

This brings me back to the Mel Brooks joke. He says he feels young: he feels ninety-eight. It is funny because the difference between ninety-eight and one hundred is tiny, and at the same time enormous. Aging is like that. So is demographic decline. For a long time, nothing seems to change. Then suddenly everything is old.

Europe is not going to wake up one morning and find that its universities have vanished. The process will be quieter. First a kindergarten closes. Then an elementary school merges. Then a high school loses sections. Then, eighteen years later, a university department wonders where the students went. But they did not disappear. They were never born, or they left, or they concentrated somewhere else.
