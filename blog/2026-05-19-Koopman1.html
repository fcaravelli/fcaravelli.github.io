Computation under the lenses of Koopman Operators

May 2026

A few years ago, Jan Korbel and David Wolpert organized a workshop in the austrian Alps, in Obergurgl. The workshop was at the intersection of computation, dynamical systems and physics. It was a fantastic workshop, and we had a few hikes up the beautiful austrian Alps. Jean-Charles Delvenne gave a talk in which the first part was about his work on reachability in dynamical systems, and the second part was about his results at the intersection of computation and stochastic thermodynamics. I was fascinated by the first part, and I had a few questions about the second part. During the talk, I asked whether he had thought about using the Koopman operator to study reachability and halting problems. He said no, but that it was an interesting idea. A few years later, here we are with a paper about exactly that.

The Koopman operator is a way of looking at a dynamical system not by following its points directly, but by following the functions we use to observe those points. Suppose a discrete-time system evolves according to \(x_{t+1}=F(x_t)\), with \(x_t\in X\). Instead of tracking the trajectory \(x_t,F(x_t),F^2(x_t),\ldots\), we take an observable \(g:X\to\mathbb{C}\) and ask how the value of \(g\) changes along the dynamics. The Koopman operator \(K\) is then defined by composition, \((Kg)(x)=g(F(x))\). Its iterates satisfy \((K^n g)(x)=g(F^n(x))\), so the full future of the system is encoded in the action of a linear operator on observables. This is the basic idea behind what is sometimes called Koopmanism: even if the original map \(F\) is nonlinear, the induced evolution of observables is linear, usually at the cost of moving to an infinite-dimensional function space. This shift makes it possible to study nonlinear dynamics using spectral objects such as eigenfunctions, eigenvalues, invariant subspaces, and one of my favourite objects: the resolvent.

A (digital) computer is on the other hand usually described symbolically: a finite state machine, a Turing machine, a circuit, a program, a sequence of instructions. But at another level, computation is just motion. A state is updated, then updated again, then updated again. The tape of a Turing machine, the configuration of a cellular automaton, the voltage pattern in a chip, or the point of an analog dynamical system all evolve according to some rule.

This suggests a natural question: can one describe computation using the language of dynamical systems? And if so, can one translate classical ideas such as halting, reachability, decidability, and universality into operator-theoretic language?

Koopman operators provide one such language. They replace nonlinear evolution of states by linear evolution of observables. The price is that the linear operator usually lives in an infinite-dimensional space. The reward is that questions about computation can be rewritten as questions about spectra, invariant subspaces, resolvents, and eigenfunctions.

em>, accepted in Journal of Physics: Complexity. The goal here is to give a less formal introduction to the main idea: computation can be studied as a dynamical process, and Koopman operators give a surprisingly clean language for doing so.

1. Computation as dynamics

The classical models of computation are already dynamical systems in disguise. A Turing machine evolves by repeatedly applying a transition rule. A cellular automaton evolves by applying a local update rule to all cells. A finite automaton reads a symbol, changes state, reads another symbol, changes state again.

In the simplest autonomous case, the entire computation can be written as

$$ x_{t+1}=F(x_t), $$

where \(x_t\) is the configuration at time \(t\), and \(F\) is the update rule. The state space \(X\) may be finite, symbolic, continuous, or a mixture of these. In a Turing machine, \(x_t\) contains the tape contents, the internal state, and the head position. In a cellular automaton, \(x_t\) is an infinite or finite symbolic configuration. In an analog computer, \(x_t\) may be a point in \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

From this perspective, a computation is a trajectory:

$$ x_0,\quad F(x_0),\quad F^2(x_0),\quad F^3(x_0),\ldots $$

The halting problem then becomes a question about this trajectory. Does it eventually enter a special region of state space? More generally, a reachability question asks whether an orbit starting in a set \(A\) eventually reaches a set \(B\).

This is already a bridge between computation theory and dynamical systems. What the Koopman framework adds is a way to lift this question from states to observables.

2. The Koopman point of view

Instead of following the state \(x_t\) directly, Koopman theory follows functions of the state. These functions are called observables. An observable is simply a function

$$ g:X\to \mathbb{C}. $$

The Koopman operator \(K\) associated with the map \(F\) acts on observables by composition:

$$ (Kg)(x)=g(F(x)). $$

This formula looks almost too simple, but it contains the whole point. The original map \(F\) may be nonlinear, complicated, discontinuous in some coordinates, or hard to analyze directly. But the map \(g\mapsto Kg\) is linear. Nonlinear dynamics has been moved into a linear operator acting on a space of functions.

Iterating the Koopman operator gives

$$ (K^n g)(x)=g(F^n(x)). $$

So if \(g\) is an observable that detects some region of state space, then \(K^n g\) tells us whether the trajectory reaches that region after \(n\) steps. This is the first hint that Koopman operators know something about computation.

In ordinary Koopman theory, one often studies eigenfunctions

$$ K\phi=\mu\phi. $$

These eigenfunctions provide coordinates in which the dynamics becomes multiplication by \(\mu\). For computation, however, the most useful object is not only the spectrum itself. A central role is played by the resolvent of the Koopman operator.

3. Reachability and the resolvent

Suppose \(A\) and \(B\) are two regions of state space. The reachability question is:

Is there an initial condition in \(A\) whose trajectory eventually reaches \(B\)?

In symbolic systems, the natural regions are often clopen sets: sets that are simultaneously closed and open. For example, in Cantor space, a clopen set may be specified by a finite prefix of an infinite symbolic sequence. Such sets are finitely describable, which is exactly what one wants for computation.

Let \(\chi_B\) be the characteristic observable of \(B\):

$$ \chi_B(x)= \begin{cases} 1, & x\in B,\\ 0, & x\notin B. \end{cases} $$

Then \(K^n\chi_B(x)=\chi_B(F^n(x))\), which is nonzero exactly when the trajectory starting at \(x\) is in \(B\) after \(n\) steps.

Now consider the resolvent

$$ (\lambda I-K)^{-1}. $$

For \(|\lambda|>1\), this can be expanded as a convergent Neumann series:

$$ (\lambda I-K)^{-1} = \lambda^{-1}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\lambda^{-n}K^n. $$

Applying this to \(\chi_B\), we get a weighted sum over all future visits to \(B\):

$$ (\lambda I-K)^{-1}\chi_B(x) = \lambda^{-1}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\lambda^{-n}\chi_B(F^n(x)). $$

Therefore,

$$ (\lambda I-K)^{-1}\chi_B(x)\neq 0 $$

exactly when the orbit of \(x\) eventually reaches \(B\). Multiplying by \(\chi_A\), one obtains the set-to-set condition

$$ \chi_A(\lambda I-K)^{-1}\chi_B\neq 0. $$

This is the Koopman version of reachability. It packages infinitely many possible future times into one operator-theoretic object.

A small but important technical point: here \(\lambda\) is a resolvent parameter chosen so that the Neumann series converges. It should not be confused with a Koopman eigenvalue of the dynamics.

This gives rise to what one can call the Koopman Halting Problem: given two finitely describable observables \(g_a\) and \(g_b\), decide whether

$$ g_a(\lambda I-K)^{-1}g_b\neq 0. $$

In the paper, we show that under natural computability assumptions on the observable space and on the Koopman operator, this problem is recursively enumerable. Informally, if the answer is yes, there is a finite procedure that eventually certifies it. This mirrors the ordinary halting problem: halting can be recognized when it happens, but non-halting cannot always be certified in finite time.

4. Halting states and eigenvalue one

Reachability asks whether a trajectory ever reaches a target set. Halting is a stronger notion. A halting state is not merely visited; once the system enters it, it remains there.

Suppose \(H\subset X\) is a halting region. This means

$$ F(H)\subseteq H. $$

In words: if the system is in \(H\), the next state is still in \(H\). The characteristic observable \(\chi_H\) then satisfies

$$ K\chi_H=\chi_H. $$

Thus \(\chi_H\) is a Koopman eigenfunction with eigenvalue \(1\). This is the spectral signature of absorption.

More generally, one can consider the basin of the halting region:

$$ B(H)=\{x\in X:\exists n\in\mathbb{N}\ \text{such that}\ F^n(x)\in H\}. $$

This is the set of initial conditions that eventually halt. The characteristic function of this basin is also invariant under the Koopman operator:

$$ K\chi_{B(H)}=\chi_{B(H)}. $$

So halting does not merely correspond to a special state. It corresponds to an invariant observable. In this sense, the long-term computational fate of a system is encoded in the eigenspace of \(K\) at eigenvalue \(1\).

Of course, the constant function \(1\) is always an eigenfunction with eigenvalue \(1\). So the mere presence of the eigenvalue \(1\) is not enough to prove that a system halts. What matters is the structure of the invariant subspace: whether there are nontrivial characteristic observables associated with absorbing computational regions.

5. Automata as coarse-grained dynamics

The same idea becomes very concrete for finite automata. A finite automaton has a finite set of states \(Q\), an input alphabet \(\Sigma\), and a transition rule

$$ \delta:Q\times \Sigma\to Q. $$

From the dynamical systems point of view, each logical state \(q\in Q\) can be regarded as a coarse-grained region \(\Omega_q\) of a larger phase space \(X\). For instance, many microscopic voltage configurations in a physical device may all correspond to the same logical state.

We then define characteristic observables

$$ \chi_q(x)= \begin{cases} 1, & x\in \Omega_q,\\ 0, & x\notin \Omega_q. \end{cases} $$

If the system is driven by an input \(u_k\), the dynamics take the form

$$ x_{k+1}=F(x_k,u_k). $$

One can make this autonomous by enlarging the state space to include the input. The extended state is

$$ z_k=(x_k,u_k), $$

and the Koopman operator acts on observables of this extended state. In the finite-state case, this action reduces to a finite-dimensional linear representation of the transition graph.

The dictionary is direct:

  • discrete states \(q\in Q\) correspond to regions \(\Omega_q\subset X\);
  • transition rules correspond to inclusions \(F(\Omega_q,U_\sigma)\subseteq \Omega_{q'}\);
  • state observables are characteristic functions \(\chi_q\);
  • halting states correspond to absorbing regions;
  • absorbing regions generate eigenfunctions at eigenvalue \(1\).

This means that the set-theoretic description of an automaton and the Koopman description are not competing pictures. They are two languages for the same coarse-grained dynamics.

6. Cycles, spectra, and transient states

The transition graph of an automaton also leaves spectral fingerprints. Suppose the coarse-grained dynamics contains a deterministic cycle

$$ q_0\to q_1\to\cdots\to q_{m-1}\to q_0. $$

On the span of the observables

$$ \chi_{q_0},\chi_{q_1},\ldots,\chi_{q_{m-1}}, $$

the Koopman operator acts as a cyclic permutation. Therefore

$$ K^m=I $$

on this invariant subspace, and the corresponding eigenvalues satisfy

$$ \mu^m=1. $$

In other words, deterministic cycles contribute roots of unity to the Koopman spectrum.

This also clarifies an important distinction. In stochastic or Markov-type systems, transient behavior is often associated with eigenvalues strictly inside the unit disk, \(|\mu|<1\), which describe asymptotic relaxation. But deterministic finite-state systems behave differently. Their transient states feed into recurrent components in finite time. Spectrally, this is better understood through nilpotent or generalized-eigenspace structure, often associated with the eigenvalue \(0\), rather than through genuine asymptotic decay.

So the Koopman picture is not a single slogan such as “computation is spectrum.” It is more precise than that. Absorbing states, cycles, transients, symbolic dynamics, and stochastic dynamics each have different spectral signatures.

7. Analog computation and hardness

The Koopman language is not limited to symbolic machines. It also applies naturally to analog dynamical systems, where computation is carried by continuous variables rather than discrete symbols.

This is especially interesting because analog computation has always lived near the boundary between physics and computation theory. A continuous dynamical system can, in principle, encode enormous information in real-valued states. But this also raises the problem of precision: what does computation mean if it requires infinitely many digits to specify an initial condition?

The reachability formulation helps here because it does not require exact point-to-point targeting. Instead of asking whether one exact state reaches another exact state, one asks whether a trajectory starting in a finitely describable region \(A\) reaches another finitely describable region \(B\). This is closer to how physical systems are actually initialized and observed.

There is also a broader motivation. Some analog formulations of hard computational problems exhibit dynamical signatures such as transient chaos, sensitive dependence on initial conditions, and fractal basin boundaries. This suggests that computational hardness may sometimes appear as a dynamical phenomenon. The Koopman operator is a natural tool for asking whether such hardness has spectral or operator-theoretic signatures.

This is speculative, but it is a promising direction: perhaps the difficulty of a computation can sometimes be read not only from an algorithmic description, but from the spectral organization of the dynamical system that performs it.

8. Outlook

The main message is simple: computation can be studied as dynamics, and Koopman operators provide a linear language for doing so.

In this language, reachability is encoded by the resolvent:

$$ \chi_A(\lambda I-K)^{-1}\chi_B\neq 0. $$

Halting regions are absorbing sets, and their basins give invariant observables at eigenvalue \(1\). Cycles in deterministic transition graphs contribute roots of unity. Transient deterministic components are associated with finite-time nilpotent structure, while stochastic or weighted systems may show true asymptotic decay through eigenvalues inside the unit disk.

This framework does not replace classical computation theory. Rather, it gives another representation of it, one that is especially well suited to comparing symbolic, analog, finite-state, and dynamical models of computation.

Several questions remain open. Can one characterize computational universality directly in terms of Koopman spectral data? Can arrival times and time-to-halting be extracted systematically from resolvent expansions? Can complexity classes be related to operator-theoretic structure? And can the same language be extended cleanly to stochastic or quantum computation?

These are not settled questions. But they point to a useful principle: the boundary between computation and dynamics is not as sharp as it may appear. A computation is a trajectory; a trajectory acts on observables; and observables are the natural home of the Koopman operator.
