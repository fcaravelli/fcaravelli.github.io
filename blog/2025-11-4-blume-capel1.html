The Blume–Capel Model in One Dimension

November 4, 2025

However, the Ising model is just one member of a broader family of lattice models that capture various physical phenomena. Going back to the Ising model in 1D, the solution of this model is a classic exercise in Statistical Mechanics, often solved using the transfer matrix method. It is the same for the Blume-Capel model, which can be seen as a generalization of the Ising model to spin-1 variables.

What is interesting about the Blume-Capel model is also a model in which microcanonical and canonical ensembles disagree in certain regions of the phase diagram, leading to intriguing thermodynamic behavior. We will come back to these at a certain point. For now, we discuss the model and its exact solution in one dimension using the transfer matrix method.

The Blume–Capel model is a generalization of the Ising model, first introduced independently by Melvin Blume and H. W. Capel in 1966. Its Hamiltonian reads:

\[ H = -J \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle} s_i s_j - d \sum_i s_i^2 - h \sum_i s_i, \]

where $s_i = -1, 0, 1$ represents a spin-1 variable that can occupy one of three states. The parameter $J$ controls the ferromagnetic coupling between neighboring spins, $d$ (often denoted $D$) is a single-ion anisotropy or crystal-field coupling, and $h$ is an external magnetic field.

The model extends the standard Ising picture by including a “neutral” state $s_i = 0$, which can be interpreted as a vacancy or non-magnetic configuration. This additional degree of freedom leads to a rich phase diagram. The system exhibits both second-order and first-order phase transitions, depending on the temperature and the value of $d$. For large positive $d$, the neutral state dominates, suppressing magnetization; for smaller or negative $d$, the $\pm1$ states become energetically favorable, and ferromagnetic order may emerge.

Originally formulated to describe magnetic materials such as UO₂ with crystal-field splitting, the Blume–Capel model was later found to apply to ³He–⁴He mixtures, where the $s = 0$ state corresponds to a ⁴He atom and $s = \pm 1$ to ³He. In that context, the quadrupole moment $1 - \langle s^2 \rangle$ corresponds to the ³He concentration. (Blume, Phys. Rev. 141, 517 (1966); Capel, Physica 32, 966 (1966); Blume, Emery, Griffiths, Phys. Rev. A 4, 1071 (1971)).

Despite its apparent simplicity, the Blume–Capel model captures the essential ingredients of first-order transitions, tricritical points, and vacancy-mediated magnetism. It also serves as a conceptual bridge between the Ising and Potts models, representing one of the earliest multi-state generalizations of the Ising paradigm.

The One-Dimensional Case

In this post, we explore the exact solution of the 1D Blume–Capel model on a ring using the transfer matrix method. Although the one-dimensional system does not exhibit a true phase transition at finite temperature, it remains a valuable pedagogical example — simple enough to solve analytically, yet rich enough to reveal the mathematical structure of the model.

\[ H = -J \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} s_i s_{i+1} - d \sum_{i=1}^{N} s_i^2 - h \sum_{i=1}^{N} s_i, \]

where $s_i = -1, 0, 1$, and $N$ is the number of sites in the chain. The goal is to express the partition function in terms of a transfer matrix, find its eigenvalues, and compute the free energy in the thermodynamic limit.

Solution Outline

The Boltzmann weight for a spin configuration is

\[ w(s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_N) = e^{-\beta H}, \]

and we define the transfer matrix $R(s, s')$ as

\[ R(s,s') = e^{-\beta H_f(s)/2} e^{-\beta H_{int}(s,s')} e^{-\beta H_f(s')/2}. \]

For the Blume–Capel model, this yields a $3\times3$ matrix representation:

\[ R = \begin{pmatrix} e^{\beta (J + d + h)} & e^{\beta (d + h)/2} & e^{\beta (-J + d)} \\ e^{\beta (d + h)/2} & 1 & e^{\beta (d - h)/2} \\ e^{\beta (-J + d)} & e^{\beta (d - h)/2} & e^{\beta (J + d - h)} \end{pmatrix}. \]

The eigenvector $\psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 0, -1)$ has eigenvalue

\[ \lambda = 2 e^{\beta d} \sinh(\beta J). \]

The remaining two eigenvalues, $\lambda_\pm$, are found analytically as

\[ \lambda_{\pm} = \frac{1}{2}\left( 2 e^{\beta d} \cosh(\beta J) \pm \sqrt{8 e^{\beta d} + \left( 2 e^{\beta d} \cosh(\beta J) - 1 \right)^2} + 1 \right). \]

The partition function is therefore

\[ Z = \lambda_+^N + \lambda^N + \lambda_-^N, \]

and in the thermodynamic limit $N \to \infty$, the free energy per site is dominated by the largest eigenvalue:

\[ f = -kT \ln \lambda_+. \]

This exact solution highlights how the transfer matrix approach provides a powerful and elegant method to treat lattice models in 1- and 2- dimensions, even when the spin variables take on more than two values. In higher dimensions, the Blume–Capel model continues to serve as a paradigmatic system for studying tricritical behavior and universality classes at the boundary between first- and second-order phase transitions.
