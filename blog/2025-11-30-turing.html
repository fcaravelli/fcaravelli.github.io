Turing Patterns via Jacobian Instability

November 25, 2025

In 1952, Alan Turing proposed that the interplay between local reactions and diffusion can destabilize a uniform state and generate spatial structure. Here I derive the classic diffusion–driven instability (Turing instability) in a concise way: start from a continuous two–species model, linearize around a homogeneous fixed point, and analyze the wave–number–dependent Jacobian to identify when modes grow.

Reaction–Diffusion Model

\[ \partial_t u = f(u,v) + D_u \nabla^2 u,\qquad \partial_t v = g(u,v) + D_v \nabla^2 v, \]

with $u(x,t), v(x,t)$ scalar fields, $f,g$ smooth reaction terms, and $D_u,D_v \ge 0$ diffusion coefficients. Let $(u_*,v_*)$ be a homogeneous fixed point: $f(u_*,v_*)=0$, $g(u_*,v_*)=0$. Define the Jacobian (reaction linearization) at the fixed point:

\[ J = \begin{pmatrix} f_u & f_v\\ g_u & g_v \end{pmatrix}_{(u_*,v_*)}, \qquad \operatorname{tr}J = f_u+g_v,\quad \det J = f_u g_v - f_v g_u. \]

Linearization and Dispersion Relation

Consider small perturbations $u=u_*+\delta u$, $v=v_*+\delta v$. After Fourier decomposing $\propto e^{\sigma t + i \mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{x}}$ with $k=\lVert \mathbf{k}\rVert$, the linearized dynamics is:

\[ \sigma\,\begin{pmatrix}\hat u\\ \hat v\end{pmatrix} = \bigl(J - k^2 D\bigr)\begin{pmatrix}\hat u\\ \hat v\end{pmatrix}, \qquad D=\begin{pmatrix} D_u & 0\\ 0 & D_v\end{pmatrix}. \]

Therefore $\sigma(k)$ are the eigenvalues of $J - k^2 D$, equivalently the roots of

\[ \sigma^2 - \tau(k)\,\sigma + \Delta(k) = 0, \] \[ \tau(k)=\operatorname{tr}J - (D_u + D_v)k^2,\qquad \Delta(k)=\det J - (D_v f_u + D_u g_v)\,k^2 + D_u D_v k^4. \]

Stability requires $\Re\,\sigma(k)<0$ for all $k$. A Turing instability means the fixed point is stable to spatially uniform perturbations ($k=0$) but unstable to some $k>0$.

Conditions for Diffusion–Driven Instability

Stability without diffusion (well–mixed):

\[ \operatorname{tr}J = f_u + g_v < 0, \qquad \det J = f_u g_v - f_v g_u > 0. \]

Instability induced by diffusion: there exists $k>0$ with $\Re\,\sigma(k)>0$. For $2\times2$ systems this occurs if and only if the following hold in addition to the above:

\[ D_v f_u + D_u g_v > 0, \qquad \bigl(D_v f_u + D_u g_v\bigr)^2 - 4 D_u D_v \det J > 0. \]

Intuitively, diffusion must skew the effective Jacobian enough to flip the sign of a mode: one species should act as a slowly diffusing activator (typically $D_u$ small) and the other as a fast diffusing inhibitor (typically $D_v \gg D_u$), so that mixed terms $D_v f_u + D_u g_v$ are positive and large. If $D_u=D_v$, diffusion merely shifts eigenvalues uniformly and cannot create a Turing pattern.

Most Unstable Wavelength

The growth rate is

\[ \sigma_\pm(k)=\frac{\tau(k)\pm\sqrt{\tau(k)^2-4\Delta(k)}}{2}. \]

When the conditions above hold, there is a band $k\in(k_-,k_+)$ with $\Re\,\sigma_+(k)>0$. The selected pattern scale is near the maximizer of $\Re\,\sigma_+(k)$, which, to leading order, is controlled by the quadratic form in $\Delta(k)$ and the linear decay in $\tau(k)$ with $k^2$.

Sign Structure (Activator–Inhibitor Heuristic)

A common minimal sign pattern near $(u_*,v_*)$ is $f_u>0$ (self–activation), $g_v<0$ (self–inhibition), $f_v>0$ (v promotes u), $g_u<0$ (u inhibits v), with $D_v\gg D_u$. This typically satisfies the stability-at-$k=0$ constraints while enabling the two diffusion inequalities to hold, thus producing stationary spots/stripes.

In a follow–up, I’ll contrast this continuous-field route to pattern formation with von Neumann’s discrete cellular automata, where local rules similarly create global order—but computation itself is the “reaction.”
